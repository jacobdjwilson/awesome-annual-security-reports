## Abstract
State of Privacy 2026 reports the results of the ISACA global State of Privacy survey conducted late in the third quarter of 2025. This report focuses on privacy staffing, operations, and budgets; privacy awareness training; privacy breaches; and privacy by design. State of Privacy 2026 also explores how privacy professionals are adopting artificial intelligence (AI) and criteria that make it easier to adopt AI for privacy tasks. Many of the survey findings are consistent with last year’s survey results, but this year’s results show that challenges related to budget cuts and staffing have worsened.

---

## Executive Summary
State of Privacy 2026 explores trends in privacy staffing, operations, budgets, and breaches; privacy awareness training; and privacy by design. This report also explores the ways in which privacy professionals are using artificial intelligence (AI) tools as well as some factors that may facilitate the adoption of AI technologies. These results are based on the sixth annual ISACA global State of Privacy survey that was conducted in September 2025.

### Key Findings
- **Privacy teams are shrinking.** The median privacy staff size of survey respondents is five, down from eight last year.
- **Many privacy professionals feel that their roles are more stressful now compared to five years ago.** The rapid evolution of technology is cited as the number one stressor, up eight percentage points from last year.
- **Although the majority of survey respondents regularly practice privacy by design** when building new applications or services, the percentage of respondents who always or frequently practice privacy by design is down slightly from last year.
- **Expert-level privacy professionals are the most challenging to hire**, followed by practitioners, then entry-level/foundational privacy professionals.
- **Organizational fit is the most important factor** in determining a candidate’s qualification for a privacy role, followed by prior hands-on experience in a privacy role and adaptability.
- **Fewer than half of survey respondents are very or completely confident** in their privacy team’s ability to achieve compliance with new privacy laws and regulations.
- **Survey respondents identify lack of training or poor training as the most common privacy failure** (51%, compared to 47% last year). The second most common failure is not practicing privacy by design, followed by data breaches/leakage.
- **Budget optimism has waned.** Less than a quarter of respondents expect their privacy budgets to increase in the next year, while half of respondents anticipate a decrease in their privacy budgets in the next 12 months.
- **Understaffing findings are consistent with last year’s findings.** Technical privacy roles are more understaffed than legal/compliance roles, similar to previous years’ results.
- **Technical privacy roles are slightly more likely to have increased demand** in the next year compared to legal/compliance roles.

## Survey Methodology
In September 2025, ISACA sent survey invitations to approximately 47,600 ISACA constituents who held the Certified Data Privacy Solutions Engineer™ (CDPSE™) designation and/or the ISACA Certified Information Security Manager® (CISM®) designation or had “privacy” or “data protection” in their job titles. More than 1,800 people completed the survey.

## Staffing
The median privacy staff size declined considerably from last year. In 2025, the median privacy staff size was eight; this year, that number fell to five. Eleven percent of total respondents said that only one full-time equivalent individual works on privacy in their enterprise.

## Open Positions and Hiring
This year, 19% of survey respondents said they had open legal/compliance privacy positions (which is a decrease from 22% last year), and 26% said they had unfilled technical privacy positions (down from 29% in 2025).

![Figure 4: Legal/Compliance Privacy Candidates’ Qualification chart]
![Figure 5: Technical Privacy Candidates’ Qualification chart]

Survey respondents identified the following factors as very important in determining if a privacy candidate was qualified:
- Organizational fit, e.g., culture (59%)
- Prior hands-on experience in privacy role (57%)
- Adaptability (56%)
- Compliance/legal experience (51%)
- Technical experience (48%)
- Credentials held (35%)
- Completion of hands-on training courses in privacy (35%)
- University degree (24%)
- Recommendation from previous employer (19%)
- Networking (19%)

## Skill Gaps
More than half (53%) of survey respondents said they were aware of skill gaps in today’s privacy professionals.

![Figure 8: Skill Gaps chart]

The top three strategies to combat these skill gaps include:
- Training to allow nonprivacy staff, who are interested, to move into privacy roles (48%)
- Increased usage of contract employees or outside consultants (36%)
- Increased reliance on AI or automation (34%)

## Conclusion
As in previous years, the 2026 State of Privacy survey results highlight that privacy teams often lack sufficient resources. The median privacy staff size dropping by one-third this year is cause for concern. Survey results underscore that AI will not compensate for resource shortages and budget concerns. Only when boards prioritize privacy and privacy objectives align with broader enterprise goals can privacy teams thrive.
