## Foreword

2025 reinforced what many GRC leaders have been feeling for the past several years: the work is expanding, the expectations are accelerating, and the margin for error is shrinking. Risk and compliance teams are being asked to demonstrate readiness more frequently, respond faster when something goes wrong, and provide clearer proof of control effectiveness to stakeholders across the business.

At the same time, third-party ecosystems continue to grow, introducing new operational dependencies and new categories of risk that cannot be managed through point-in-time reviews alone. As AI becomes embedded across workflows and vendor offerings, GRC teams are increasingly expected to govern not only traditional security and compliance risks, but the rapidly evolving risks associated with AI-enabled technology.

The findings of this year’s survey point to a market investing in maturity but still working through the operational realities of scaling. Many organizations report strong confidence in their ability to execute: most respondents said they had the budget and resources needed to achieve their goals in 2025, and a majority anticipate spending more money on GRC in 2026. At the same time, the underlying workload pressure is clear. Organizations expect to spend more time on IT risk management and compliance in the year ahead, and breach experience appears to be a meaningful accelerant: respondents who experienced a breach are significantly more likely to anticipate increased time investment in 2026 than those who did not. Taken together, this suggests that even when resourcing is available, the central challenge becomes how to scale readiness without scaling administrative overhead in parallel.

[hyperproof.io](https://hyperproof.io)

### So, what does this mean for 2026?

**The short answer? GRC operating models and tooling choices matter.** Respondents who manage risk ad-hoc are far more likely to have experienced a breach than those using an integrated, automated approach. Many organizations report high maturity and widespread adoption of third-party risk management best practices, yet their execution inputs remain mixed, with continued reliance on spreadsheets and document-based questionnaires. This gap reflects an important reality for 2026: maturity is no longer defined by intent or policy alone. It is defined by consistency, traceability, and the ability to maintain assurance over time across controls, evidence, and third parties.

As you explore the benchmarks and insights throughout this report, we invite you to consider where your program sits on the maturity curve and what it will take to move forward this year. The collective experience of your peers points to a clear direction: teams are prioritizing centralization, standardization, and automation to reduce manual effort and sustain continuous readiness. Whether you are formalizing an emerging program or evolving a mature one, the opportunity in 2026 is to turn investment into measurable outcomes that result in less time spent chasing evidence, clearer reporting for leadership, and stronger resilience when risks materialize.

## 01 How GRC Leaders Are Scaling and Operating Their Compliance Programs

Compliance teams are being asked to do more than ever: keep pace with expanding requirements, demonstrate control effectiveness faster, and deliver audit-ready evidence with fewer bottlenecks. Most organizations are organizing GRC work in ways that look mature on paper, like centralized teams, regular audits, and increasing use of controls monitoring.

However, the day-to-day reality still includes heavy manual overhead, fragmented systems, and cross-functional chasing that slows everything down. The biggest differentiator in this chapter isn’t simply whether a program is centralized; it’s whether the work is executed through integrated, repeatable workflows that keep controls and evidence current year-round.

This year’s data shows a clear picture of where this friction still persists, especially in manual work, cross-team coordination, and evidence readiness. It also underscores a consistent theme across the benchmark: the structure of your compliance operations — where it’s centralized, distributed across your organization, automated, or addressed ad-hoc — is closely tied to compliance outcomes. This points to a consistent operational reality: organizations are structurally centralizing GRC and increasingly adopting monitoring software, but they still lose significant time to manual work and fragmented processes. The strongest differentiator across our findings is not just whether a team is centralized. It’s whether risk and compliance workflows are executed in an integrated, automated way that supports continuous evidence readiness and control assurance.

### The audit prep burden is growing

Preparing for and executing audits is also time-consuming. Audit execution tasks point to a consistent operational pressure: access reviews, evidence retrieval, and auditor back-and-forth dominate where teams spend time. These are not edge-case problems. They’re foundational compliance operations that repeat every cycle and scale poorly as the business grows.

### Using AI to streamline workflows

AI adoption in GRC has moved decisively beyond experimentation and into day-to-day execution. Only **3%** of respondents say they are not using AI to streamline any workflows, demonstrating how quickly AI is becoming a baseline productivity layer for many teams rather than a niche capability used by early adopters.

What stands out is where AI is being applied. The highest-use workflows are documentation-heavy activities that historically consume substantial time and slow down delivery, like reviewing documentation and merging multiple documents. These are the exact areas where GRC teams often face bottlenecks, because they require synthesizing information across sources, mapping it to standards, and producing consistent outputs. The data suggests teams are first turning to AI where it can reduce manual reading, comparison, and rewrite work, helping them move faster without sacrificing rigor.

AI is also being used as a research and analysis accelerator, not just a writing assistant. More than half of respondents (**52%**) use AI for research, indicating that teams are increasingly relying on AI to gather context, summarize requirements, and support decision-making quickly. At the same time, a significant share are using AI for writing policies (**41%**), which points to a practical shift: policy work is still important, but teams appear to be prioritizing AI for the tasks that sit between policy and execution, such as interpreting documentation, normalizing inputs, and aligning controls.

## 02 Framework Adoption Trends and Risk Management Best Practices

Risk management programs are evolving in two directions at once. Organizations are adopting a broader set of frameworks, conducting assessments on a more regular cadence, and formalizing governance practices that support repeatability. At the same time, the data shows a persistent execution gap, and program maturity is uneven. Many organizations have the components of a mature program, but outcomes vary materially depending on operating model, tooling, and whether risk and compliance are managed as a connected system. The difference is less about whether organizations recognize what “good” looks like, and more about whether they can operationalize it consistently through standardized processes and integrated systems.

This chapter focuses on what that maturity looks like in practice: which standards organizations are using, how they handle regional variance, how frequently they reassess risk, and which operating models are most strongly associated with consistent execution. Together, these results point to a clear conclusion: as scope expands, the programs that perform best are those that can keep risk and compliance execution repeatable, measurable, and continuously up-to-date.

### Leveraging a common controls framework is a best practice now

When organizations face regional variation in privacy and security requirements, their strategy tends to fall into one of three approaches. The most common approach (**56%**) is rationalization through a common controls framework, indicating a preference for standardization and reuse. A second-highest group (**25%**) aligns with the most rigorous applicable law, essentially setting a single bar and applying it across the organization.

Regional regulatory variance continues to be a forcing function for maturity. Organizations need a repeatable method for handling differences across jurisdictions without turning compliance into a series of one-off interpretations and exceptions.

The most scalable approaches are the ones that reduce complexity at the point of execution: standardizing controls, harmonizing evidence, and ensuring reporting is consistent regardless of geography.

[hyperproof.io](https://hyperproof.io)

This is also where many teams experience the “hidden cost” of compliance: not just meeting requirements, but maintaining clarity and consistency as requirements shift. A common controls approach makes this tractable because it turns regulatory complexity into an internal standard that teams can implement and monitor consistently.

### Quarterly risk assessments have become the norm, indicating increased maturity

Quarterly cadence becoming the norm is a positive maturity signal, but cadence alone does not guarantee effectiveness. The differentiator is whether assessments are connected to remediation tracking, control evaluation, and leadership reporting, or whether they remain periodic snapshots that are difficult to operationalize and integrate.

## 03 Budgets, Capacity, and ROI: How GRC Teams Are Funding the Work in 2026

Budgets and resourcing decisions reveal how organizations translate GRC priorities into operational reality. This year’s benchmark shows that GRC is not being treated as a discretionary function. Many organizations are sustaining or increasing investment, and reported 2026 budget levels indicate that GRC programs are expected to deliver measurable value across multiple fronts: regulatory readiness, customer assurance, risk reduction, and resilience.

However, this is also where an important pattern emerges across the last several years. Survey respondents consistently report that budgets are increasing year over year, while many GRC leaders hear a different story in the market: tightened spending, delayed purchases, and scrutiny on renewals.

[hyperproof.io](https://hyperproof.io)

The most likely explanation is not that one view is “wrong,” but that they are measuring different realities. This survey captures the outlook and expectations of GRC leaders at a point in time, often reflecting planned budgets, priority-setting, or early-year allocations. Market sentiment, by contrast, often reflects later-stage adjustments such as mid-year freezes, reforecasting, shifting spend to fewer vendors, or reallocations that reduce discretionary projects while preserving mission-critical security and compliance work.

In other words, organizations may still feel pressure to invest in GRC, even as they face constraints on how that investment shows up in headcount, tooling, and external services.

This chapter examines the budget story through three lenses. First, it establishes baseline context through revenue and team size, because program complexity increases as organizations scale. Second, it looks at budget direction, the drivers behind budget changes, and what organizations are planning for 2026. Third, it evaluates how funding is being allocated across headcount, software, and external services, and what that allocation implies for ROI. CHAPTER 4
Third-Party Risk Management at Scale

Third-party risk management (TPRM) has moved from a specialized security practice to a business-wide operating
requirement. Vendors and suppliers are now embedded in nearly every critical workflow, from customer data processing and
core infrastructure to finance, HR, and AI-enabled services. As a result, third-party risk is no longer something organizations
can address only at onboarding or at annual renewal. It requires repeatable decision-making, consistent evidence
collection, and the ability to detect changes in vendor risk posture over time.

This year’s data reflects that shift in two important ways. First, third-party risk programs are increasingly expected to
deliver ongoing assurance, not just point-in-time due diligence. Respondents’ priorities emphasize cybersecurity and data
protection, and many organizations report maturity models that include continuous monitoring.

Second, the operational inputs powering TPRM remain varied. Organizations continue to rely on a mix of questionnaires,
trust centers, and risk rating solutions, along with manual tools and fragmented workflows. In practice, these mixed inputs
often create the biggest execution challenges: normalizing information, applying consistent evaluation standards, tracking
remediation, and maintaining an audit-ready record as vendors and requirements change.

  hyperproof.io
81  //   2026 IT Risk and Compliance Benchmark Report

This chapter also highlights how the scope of third-party risk is expanding in step with technology adoption. AI is
now part of the vendor ecosystem, and many organizations have begun incorporating AI-specific assessment
criteria into supplier evaluations. However, approaches differ substantially, ranging from dedicated frameworks
to lighter-weight criteria within general assessments. This variability signals an important maturity frontier:
organizations recognize AI-related third-party risk, but many are still formalizing what “good” looks like in practice,
including what evidence to request, how to validate it, and how frequently to reassess.

Across these findings, the underlying theme is consistency at scale. As vendor ecosystems grow, the primary
constraint is not intent. It is the ability to run third-party risk management as a standardized, repeatable workflow
that integrates evidence, monitoring, and remediation. Programs that can centralize vendor risk records, apply
consistent controls and evaluation standards, and reduce manual follow-up are best positioned to keep pace with
rising assurance expectations without turning TPRM into a bottleneck for the business.

Tooling is improving, but many programs still
rely on manual inputs

Even as third-party risk programs mature, the tools and inputs teams rely on remain mixed. Many organizations
are combining dedicated solutions and structured processes with manual workflows that slow down reviews and

A key implication is that third-party risk often becomes “multi-system by default.” When vendor evidence,
questionnaires, ratings, and remediation tracking are spread across different tools, teams spend more time

remediation follow-through, and make it harder to demonstrate consistency to auditors and leadership. The
organizations best positioned to scale are those that can centralize third-party risk records, standardize evidence
requirements, and automate repeatable steps, such as collection, parsing, and status tracking.

CHAPTER 5
Decision Power in GRC: Ownership,
Oversight, and Organizational Readiness

GRC decision-making has become more consequential — and more visible — than it was even a few years ago. Risk and
compliance leaders are no longer making choices solely to satisfy auditors or check regulatory boxes. They are making
decisions that directly affect how quickly the business can onboard vendors, enter new markets, meet customer assurance
demands, and respond when something goes wrong. In this environment, the structure of decision-making, who holds
oversight, and how teams are staffed are not internal details. They shape how effectively an organization can maintain
readiness and prove it under scrutiny.

  hyperproof.io
107  //   2026 IT Risk and Compliance Benchmark Report

This year’s data suggests a clear tension at the center of many programs. Decision authority is often concentrated in a
small set of leaders, which can enable speed and accountability. At the same time, GRC execution increasingly depends on
distributed participation across IT, security, legal, procurement, and business owners who must provide evidence, perform
control activities, and respond to issues on tight timelines. When governance and operating structure are clear, this distribution
becomes manageable through standardized workflows and consistent evidence practices. When governance is unclear,
distribution becomes fragmentation, leading to inconsistent control application, heavier follow-up burden, and slower
reporting.

Organizations are planning for continued growth in compliance capacity, reflecting the expectation that risk and compliance
work will require sustained effort in 2026 and beyond. However, growth introduces its own operational challenges. Larger
teams and broader programs require stronger process discipline, clearer ownership, and systems that make day-to-day work
repeatable. Without that structure, scaling can increase coordination cost rather than increasing throughput and consistency.

GRC technology decisions are rarely made by one person in isolation. Even where one role holds primary responsibility, buying
decisions typically reflect a committee dynamic with distinct champions, influencers, and financial approvers. The most
effective organizations treat this as an advantage: they align stakeholders around a shared definition of success, connect
investments to measurable outcomes, and choose operating models and tools that support standardization at scale.

Many programs are
substantial, and expectations
for impact will follow

Team size provides a baseline lens for understanding decision-making
and growth expectations. Many respondents report working in established
compliance and risk teams rather than being the only individual
responsible. That matters because as teams become more substantial,
the demand for consistency increases. Larger teams typically require
clearer workflow definitions, stronger governance, and shared systems
that prevent knowledge from being siloed in individuals or departments.

It also reinforces why technology decisions become more consequential
as programs scale. A tool that works for a small team may not support
standardized execution across a larger, distributed set of stakeholders.
As team size grows, the need for shared evidence practices, standardized
control mapping, and consistent reporting becomes more central to
program effectiveness.

For leaders, the implication is that scale should drive intentional design.
Mature teams are not simply larger; they are more consistent. They
operate with repeatable processes, clear roles, and systems that make
readiness and reporting sustainable.

Team size reflects industry
complexity and assurance
expectations

Team size distribution varies substantially by
industry, revealing very different operating
realities. Healthcare is heavily concentrated
in the 10–25 people band (57%), suggesting
structured teams with defined responsibilities but
not necessarily very large scale. Manufacturing
shows a similar concentration in 10–25 people
(55%), indicating many programs operate at a
mid-sized team level. Financial Services stands
out with a stronger tilt toward larger teams,
peaking in 25–50 people (43%), which aligns
with the complexity and breadth of third-party
and regulatory exposure typical of that sector.
“All Others” has the highest share of very large
teams (50+ people: 23%), suggesting that this
category likely includes organizations with
broader program scope or more centralized
enterprise risk/compliance functions. The key
takeaway is that “typical” team size is highly
industry-dependent, which affects what “good”
operational scale looks like.

Team scale and operating model
do not always move in lockstep

Team size also shifts with how organizations
manage IT risk. Ad-hoc approaches skew
smaller, with a higher share of respondents
in 1–5 people (11%) and a higher presence of
“sole member” teams (6%), which can make
consistent execution difficult as scope
grows. Siloed approaches concentrate in
the mid-range, with 39% in the 10–25 people
band, suggesting that larger teams do not
automatically imply integrated execution.
The largest-team signals show up most
clearly in MSSP-managed models, which
have the highest shares in 25–50 people
(31%) and 50+ people (19%). The broader
implication is that operating model maturity
and team scale move together in some
cases, but not always; organizations can
have sizable teams and still operate in
ways that create fragmentation, which
makes process standardization and shared
systems more important than headcount
alone.
