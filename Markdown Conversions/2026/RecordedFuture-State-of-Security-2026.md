## Section IV: Cybercrime in Transition

![A diagram illustrating the shift from large, centralized cybercriminal groups to a more decentralized, modular, and resilient ecosystem.]

In 2025, the cybercriminal landscape underwent a significant transformation, driven by
sustained law enforcement pressure and the increasing adoption of new technologies. The
traditional model of large, centralized criminal enterprises was replaced by a more
decentralized, modular, and resilient ecosystem, characterized by the rise of smaller, more
specialized groups and the increasing use of private infrastructure.

English-Speaking Cybercriminals: Stealing the Spotlight through High-Profile
Breaches

English-speaking cybercriminals, particularly those operating out of North America and
Europe, continued to dominate the headlines in 2025, with a series of high-profile breaches
targeting major corporations and government agencies. These groups, often characterized by
their professionalism and focus on high-value targets, demonstrated a sophisticated
understanding of enterprise networks and a willingness to invest in long-term access.

The shift toward high-profile breaches was driven by the increasing value of stolen data,
the potential for large ransom payments, and the desire for notoriety. These groups often
leveraged advanced techniques, such as supply-chain attacks and the exploitation of zero-day
vulnerabilities, to gain access to their targets.

New Hacking Collectives, Same Hacking Tactics

While the names and branding of hacking collectives changed in 2025, the underlying
tactics remained largely consistent. The use of phishing, social engineering, and the
exploitation of known vulnerabilities continued to be the primary methods for initial access.
However, the increasing use of automation and AI-powered tools allowed these groups to
scale their operations and improve their efficiency.

The rise of new hacking collectives was often driven by the fragmentation of existing
groups, as members sought to establish their own operations or join more lucrative ventures.
This trend contributed to a more competitive and dynamic cybercriminal landscape, with
groups constantly evolving their tactics to stay ahead of law enforcement and security
measures.

Telegram and Private Infrastructure

Telegram continued to be a central hub for cybercriminal activity in 2025, providing a
secure and anonymous platform for communication, the sale of stolen data, and the
coordination of attacks. The platform’s features, such as encrypted messaging and the ability
to create private channels, made it an ideal environment for criminal groups to operate
without fear of detection.

In addition to Telegram, cybercriminals increasingly relied on private infrastructure,
such as bulletproof hosting and decentralized networks, to host their command-and-control
servers and exfiltrate stolen data. This shift made it more difficult for law enforcement to
track and disrupt criminal operations, as the infrastructure was often located in jurisdictions
with weak or non-existent cybercrime laws.

Increased Competition Accelerates Innovation in Ransomware-as-a-Service

The ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) model continued to evolve in 2025, with increased
competition among RaaS providers driving innovation and the development of new, more
sophisticated ransomware variants. The RaaS model, which allows affiliates to rent
ransomware infrastructure and tools in exchange for a share of the ransom payments,
remained a key driver of cybercriminal activity.

The increased competition led to a focus on improving the usability and effectiveness of
ransomware, with providers offering better support, more advanced features, and more
effective evasion techniques. This made it easier for less skilled criminals to enter the
ransomware market, further increasing the volume and impact of ransomware attacks.

Organized Crime in Southeast Asia Industrializes Fraud

Organized crime groups in Southeast Asia continued to industrialize fraud in 2025,
leveraging the region’s weak governance and permissive environment to conduct large-scale
scam operations. These groups, often operating out of specialized compounds, used a
combination of social engineering, phishing, and the exploitation of digital platforms to
defraud victims worldwide.

The industrialization of fraud was characterized by the use of large, well-organized
teams, the adoption of professional management practices, and the use of advanced
technology to scale operations. This allowed these groups to generate significant revenue
and operate with a high degree of resilience, despite international efforts to disrupt their
activities.

Infostealers: The Hidden Threat Enabling Ransomware and Extortion

Infostealers, a type of malware designed to steal sensitive information such as
credentials, financial data, and personal information, continued to be a major threat in 2025.
These tools, often sold on the dark web, were used by a wide range of threat actors, from
individual criminals to sophisticated state-sponsored groups, to gain initial access to
networks and facilitate further attacks, such as ransomware and extortion.

The widespread use of infostealers made it easier for threat actors to conduct
large-scale credential harvesting, providing them with a constant supply of access to
enterprise networks. This, in turn, fueled the growth of the ransomware and extortion
markets, as threat actors were able to quickly and easily identify and exploit vulnerable
targets.

Criminals Experiment with AI to Support Social Engineering, Malware
Development

Cybercriminals began to experiment with AI in 2025, using it to support social
engineering, malware development, and other aspects of their operations. While the use of
AI was still in its early stages, it showed the potential to significantly improve the
efficiency and effectiveness of cybercriminal activity.

AI-powered tools were used to create more convincing phishing emails, automate the
development of malware, and analyze large datasets to identify potential targets. This
trend is expected to continue, with cybercriminals increasingly adopting AI to stay ahead
of security measures and improve their operational capabilities.

Law Enforcement Takes a Multifaceted Approach to Cybercrime

Law enforcement agencies continued to take a multifaceted approach to cybercrime in
2025, combining traditional investigative techniques with international cooperation,
public-private partnerships, and the use of advanced technology. This approach led to a
number of successful operations, including the takedown of several major cybercriminal
networks and the arrest of key individuals.

However, the decentralized and global nature of cybercrime continued to pose a
significant challenge, with threat actors constantly adapting their tactics and infrastructure
to evade detection. The success of law enforcement efforts will depend on their ability to
maintain international cooperation, leverage the expertise of the private sector, and
continuously innovate their investigative techniques.

Crossover Focus: State-Sponsored Ransomware

In 2025, the line between cybercriminal and state-sponsored activity continued to blur,
with some state-sponsored groups using ransomware as a tool for espionage, disruption,
and revenue generation. This crossover focus, often referred to as state-sponsored
ransomware, allowed states to achieve their strategic objectives while maintaining a
degree of deniability.

The use of ransomware by state-sponsored groups presented a significant challenge for
organizations, as it combined the destructive potential of state-sponsored attacks with the
financial motivation of cybercriminal activity. This trend is expected to continue, with
states increasingly using ransomware as a tool for geopolitical competition.

Looking Ahead

Looking ahead to 2026, the cybercriminal landscape is expected to remain dynamic and
challenging, with threat actors continuing to adapt their tactics and infrastructure to
evade detection and maximize their impact. The increasing use of AI, the growth of the
RaaS model, and the continued industrialization of fraud are expected to be key drivers
of cybercriminal activity.

Organizations will need to adopt a proactive approach to cybersecurity, focusing on
resilience, threat intelligence, and the ability to respond to cybercriminal activity in
real time. This will require a combination of technical defenses, employee training, and
a commitment to continuous improvement.

## Section V: AI Developments in 2025

![A diagram illustrating the AI threat landscape, including offensive uses of AI, AI-powered malware, and the US-China AI gap.]

In 2025, AI continued to be a major driver of technological change, with significant
advances in both the development and application of AI models. These advances had a
profound impact on the cyber threat landscape, with both state and criminal threat actors
leveraging AI to improve their operational capabilities.

Offensive Uses of AI

The offensive use of AI continued to evolve in 2025, with threat actors using AI to
automate and scale their operations, improve the effectiveness of their attacks, and
evade detection. AI-powered tools were used to create more convincing phishing emails,
automate the development of malware, and analyze large datasets to identify potential
targets.

The use of AI in offensive operations allowed threat actors to conduct more
sophisticated and targeted attacks, while reducing the time and resources required to
execute them. This trend is expected to continue, with threat actors increasingly
adopting AI to stay ahead of security measures and improve their operational
capabilities.

Threat Actors Are Hacking LLMs

In 2025, threat actors began to target large language models (LLMs), using techniques
such as prompt injection and data poisoning to manipulate the models’ outputs and
exploit their vulnerabilities. These attacks, often referred to as LLM hacking, allowed
threat actors to gain unauthorized access to sensitive information, generate malicious
content, and disrupt the operation of AI-powered systems.

The targeting of LLMs presented a new and significant challenge for organizations, as
it required them to develop new security measures to protect their AI models and the
data they process. This trend is expected to continue, with threat actors increasingly
focusing on the vulnerabilities of AI systems.

AI-Powered Malware Is Still Largely Experimental

While the use of AI in malware development continued to be a topic of discussion in
2025, the actual deployment of AI-powered malware remained largely experimental.
Most observed use of AI in malware was limited to the automation of existing
techniques, rather than the introduction of novel, AI-driven tradecraft.

However, the potential for AI-powered malware to significantly improve the
effectiveness of attacks remains high. As AI models become more sophisticated and
accessible, it is likely that we will see the emergence of more advanced, AI-driven
malware that is capable of adapting to its environment and evading detection.

Deepfakes and Synthetic Identity Fraud Rising

The use of deepfakes and synthetic identity fraud continued to rise in 2025, with threat
actors using these technologies to create more convincing social engineering attacks,
defraud victims, and undermine trust in digital systems. Deepfakes, which use AI to
create realistic audio and video content, were used to impersonate individuals and
organizations, while synthetic identity fraud, which uses AI to create fake identities,
was used to bypass identity verification systems.

The rise of deepfakes and synthetic identity fraud presented a significant challenge for
organizations, as it required them to develop new methods for verifying the identity of
individuals and the authenticity of digital content. This trend is expected to continue,
with threat actors increasingly using these technologies to conduct more sophisticated
and effective attacks.

The US–China AI Gap

In 2025, the competition between the US and China for AI dominance continued to
intensify, with both nations investing heavily in the development of advanced AI models
and their application in military and economic sectors. This competition, often referred
to as the AI gap, had a significant impact on the global threat landscape, with both
nations using AI to advance their strategic objectives and gain a competitive advantage.

The US-China AI gap is expected to continue to shape the global threat landscape,
with both nations likely to use AI to develop new and more sophisticated cyber
capabilities, influence operations, and other tools of geopolitical competition.

Looking Ahead

Looking ahead to 2026, AI is expected to continue to be a major driver of
technological change and a key factor in the global threat landscape. The increasing
use of AI by both state and criminal threat actors, the targeting of AI systems, and the
rise of deepfakes and synthetic identity fraud are expected to be key trends.

Organizations will need to adopt a proactive approach to AI security, focusing on
the development of robust security measures, the implementation of AI governance
frameworks, and the ability to detect and respond to AI-driven threats.

## Section VI: Lessons from 2025 and Outlook for 2026

![A diagram summarizing the key lessons from 2025 and the outlook for 2026.]

The year 2025 was a period of significant change and uncertainty, with the
fragmentation of the international order, the escalation of conflict, and the
accelerating pace of technological change shaping the global threat landscape. The
lessons learned from 2025 will be critical for organizations as they navigate the
challenges and opportunities of 2026.

Lessons from 2025

The key lessons from 2025 include:

*   **Geopolitical fragmentation is the new normal**: The weakening of international
    institutions and alliances has created a more volatile and unpredictable
    international environment, with states increasingly willing to use force and
    coercion to advance their objectives.
*   **Cyber operations are an integral part of modern conflict**: Cyber operations are
    no longer a separate domain of conflict, but are deeply integrated into kinetic
    operations, influence campaigns, and other tools of geopolitical competition.
*   **Pre-positioning is a key strategic advantage**: The ability to gain and maintain
    persistent access to critical infrastructure is a key strategic advantage, allowing
    states to project power, collect intelligence, and disrupt operations during
    periods of crisis.
*   **The cybercriminal ecosystem is more resilient than ever**: Sustained law
    enforcement pressure has forced cybercriminals to adapt, leading to a more
    decentralized, modular, and resilient ecosystem that is increasingly difficult to
    disrupt.
*   **AI is a double-edged sword**: While AI offers significant opportunities for
    innovation and efficiency, it also presents new and significant risks, with both
    state and criminal threat actors leveraging AI to improve their operational
    capabilities.

Threat Outlook for 2026

The threat outlook for 2026 is characterized by:

*   **Geopolitical Fragmentation Increases the Likelihood of Simultaneous Regional
    Crises**: The diffusion of power and the decline of effective guardrails will
    increase the likelihood of simultaneous regional crises, with cyber operations
    and economic instruments playing a key role in these conflicts.
*   **State-Sponsored Threat Actors Use Connectivity Disruptions as a Primary Tool
    of Coercion**: States will increasingly use connectivity disruptions, such as
    DDoS attacks and the sabotage of critical infrastructure, as a tool of coercion
    to pressure adversaries and shape the strategic environment.
*   **Ransomware Becomes Increasingly Fragmented, Commercialized, and Harder to
    Disrupt**: The ransomware market will continue to evolve, with increased
    competition, the adoption of new technologies, and the use of private
    infrastructure making it increasingly difficult for law enforcement to disrupt.
*   **The Synthetic Identity Crisis Deepens**: The rise of deepfakes and synthetic
    identity fraud will continue to undermine trust in digital systems, making it
    increasingly difficult for organizations to verify the identity of individuals and
    the authenticity of digital content.
*   **AI Becomes the Next Great Attack Surface**: The targeting of AI systems, the
    use of AI-powered malware, and the exploitation of AI vulnerabilities will
    become a major focus for threat actors, making AI security a critical priority for
    organizations.
*   **Quantum Readiness Moves from Planning to Spending**: As quantum computing
    advances, organizations will need to begin investing in quantum-resistant
    cryptography and other measures to protect their data and systems from future
    quantum-enabled threats.
*   **Robots and Space Systems Become Contested Cyber-Physical Terrain**: The
    increasing use of robots and space systems in military and commercial
    applications will make them a target for cyber operations, with states and
    non-state actors seeking to gain control, disrupt, or exploit these systems.

Conclusion

The year 2026 will be a period of continued uncertainty and change, with the
geopolitical, technological, and criminal trends observed in 2025 continuing to shape
the global threat landscape. Organizations that are able to anticipate these trends,
invest in operational resilience, and adopt a proactive approach to cybersecurity will
be better positioned to navigate the challenges and opportunities of the year ahead.

Effective risk management in 2026 will depend on resilience, adaptability, and the
ability to operate amid persistent uncertainty. By focusing on these core principles,
organizations can reduce surprise, prioritize effectively, and act with confidence in
an increasingly volatile global environment.

English-Speaking Cybercriminals: Stealing the Spotlight through High-Profile
Breaches

English-speaking criminal threat actors are distinctive for their loose organizational
structure, visibility-seeking behavior, and reliance on social engineering tactics to conduct
extortion campaigns. These characteristics create a constantly adapting threat, as influential
threat actors recruit opportunistically and refine social engineering tactics targeting help
desk or support employees, as well as others who could provide network access. These
groups actively court public attention for their activity to build notoriety and amplify extortion
pressure.

New Hacking Collectives, Same Hacking Tactics

Some of this year’s most publicized cyberattacks, notably the extortion campaigns against
Salesforce, Jaguar Land Rover (JLR), and Salesloft Drift, were claimed by a group calling itself
Scattered LAPSUS$ Hunters (SLSH). SLSH (also stylized Shinysp1d3r and Sp1d3rHunters) is
a self-proclaimed merger of the threat groups Scattered Spider, LAPSUS$, and ShinyHunters.
Insikt Group assesses that the effort is likely led by ShinyHunters, and we note that no known
members of LAPSUS$ or Scattered Spider have claimed or acknowledged their involvement.
The collaboration announcements may be an effort to attract public attention by playing on
the name recognition of notorious groups, as well as an attempt to troll researchers who track
these groups.

English-speaking cybercriminal threat actors rely on low-cost, high-impact social

engineering techniques, particularly help desk impersonation, to carry out extortion campaigns.
This approach bypasses multi-factor authentication (MFA) by convincing customer support
staff to reset passwords or grant access to privileged accounts. These techniques were likely
used in the compromise of British retailers Marks & Spencer (M&S) and Co-op, which were
targeted in a highly publicized ransomware campaign in spring 2025. Using social engineering
tactics, such as impersonating internal help desk personnel, threat actors were able to
request password resets and pivot into the retailers’ environments. This also illustrates how
compromising a single third party can provide access to multiple downstream enterprises,
amplifying both the scale and impact of extortion operations. The Salesloft Drift exploit
similarly exemplifies this “one-to-many” approach, as more than 200 companies were breached
via compromised authentication tokens.

Telegram and Private Infrastructure

In the aftermath of the takedowns of forums like BreachForums 2, Nulled, and Cracked,

English-language groups shifted to Telegram, a messaging platform, and set up their own
infrastructure for hosting data leak sites. For instance, SLSH launched numerous Telegram
channels to promote its involvement in extortion campaigns targeting Salesforce, Jaguar
Land Rover (JLR), and other organizations. The Telegram channels enabled the group to
establish a trusted communication network outside of the traditional centralized forums. It also
introduced its exploits to a broader audience. However, the platform’s openness introduced
new challenges, such as SLSH impersonation accounts and bans on users for terms-of-service
violations.

The forum takedowns also meant that threat actors no longer had a reliable place to host
and share stolen data. As a result, SLSH established its own data leak site in October 2025 to
host the Salesforce data. This represents a shift away from centralized forums where multiple
threat actors control the forum and post data breach claims. Attempts to recreate the forums

variants. Insikt Group identified 289 new variants this year, a 33% increase from the previous
year. The majority of these new variants originated from leaked source code and builders for
existing ransomware families, including LockBit, Chaos, Makop, and Dharma, among others.

exploitation.

Looking Ahead

We anticipa te that the trends of fragmentation, modularization, and adaptation will continue
to accelerate, driven by advances in AI technology and persistent global instability. AI is already
making certain types of cybercrime, particularly social engineering, significantly easier for a
broader range of potential threat actors. Consistent improvements in AI coding capabilities will
make developing sophisticated malware similarly more accessible.

Geopolitically, cybercrime is enabled by weak or corrupt governments that exploit criminal

playbook, leveraging criminal networks to advance their objectives. Furthermore, the erosion
of international institutions may make international law enforcement cooperation more difficult,
providing greater impunity to cybercriminals who can exploit leniency in one country as long as
they target that country’s adversaries.

AI-Powered Malware Is Still Largely Experimental

Beyond enabling new attack vectors, AI is also being used by threat actors to incrementally

enhance traditional cyber operations, though with mixed results. Despite frequent warnings
about “AI-powered malware,” observed real-world activity remains far less advanced than
the surrounding hype suggests. Most threat actor use of AI is limited to experimentation or
marginal efficiency gains — such as assistance with code generation, reconnaissance, or
evasion — rather than fully autonomous attack execution.

When mapped to Recorded Future’s AI Malware Maturity Model (AIM3), the vast majority of

known examples fall within Levels 1–3, often reflecting proofs-of-concept, research artifacts,
or AI-assisted variants of existing tools. While one widely cited case has been described as
Level 4 (Transforming), that assessment remains contested and still requires meaningful human
oversight. To date, no threat actor has credibly demonstrated a truly AI-orchestrated cyber
operation. In practice, AI is enhancing attacker efficiency at the margins, but fully autonomous
AI-driven attacks remain theoretical rather than operational.

close competition. The US has remained focused on pursuing artificial general intelligence

modified Alibaba models shared on Hugging Face now outnumber those derived from Google,

ecosystem may represent an alternative, and potentially more scalable, path to AI dominance.

2025 Outcome: Vibe Coding Is Here, But It May Not Be Safer

Ransomware Becomes Increasingly Fragmented, Commercialized, and

Ransomware is highly likely to remain fragmented and highly adaptive through 2026.

smaller, faster-moving groups that favor shorter attack cycles, lower ransom demands, and
opportunistic targeting, marking the end of the “big-game hunting” era. The ransomware
ecosystem will continue becoming more organized and commercialized, with more groups
adopting subscription models, outsourcing operations, and relying on specialized laundering
and negotiation services. The result will be a distributed criminal supply chain — resilient,
decentralized, and increasingly difficult to track or disrupt.

Ransom payments, already down 23% in Q3 2025, are likely to decline further as more
organizations refuse to pay and smaller targets yield lower financial returns. With extortion
success rates declining, threat actors are likely to prioritize disruption and visibility over direct
financial gain, conducting reputation-driven attacks to compel victims to engage. Established
families such as LockBit, Albabat, and Nitrogen are expected to remain influential, while
emerging variants merge and modularize codebases for stealth and persistence. Through 2026,
adversaries will almost certainly continue to experiment with AI-assisted phishing, payload
tuning, and automated ransom negotiations, sustaining innovation even as profits decline.

Quantum Readiness Moves from Planning to Spending

In 2026, quantum readiness will move decisively from long-term planning to sustained
investment as governments and standards bodies translate post-quantum cryptography (PQC)
guidance into actionable migration timelines. US initiatives such as CNSA 2.0 and the NIST
standardization process are both targeting full transition by 2035, alongside parallel efforts
in Europe. Although many mandates formally apply only to public-sector systems, these
frameworks are increasingly establishing de facto expectations across most sectors, making
PQC adoption a practical necessity for large organizations.

At the same time, the “harvest now, decrypt later” problem — threat actors stealing
encrypted data under the assumption that it will be decrypted in the future using quantum
computers — is narrowing the margin for delay, particularly for data with long-term
confidentiality requirements. As a result, boards are beginning to allocate dedicated quantum
security budgets in 2026, in some sectors approaching 5% of total cybersecurity spend, to
support cryptographic inventories, vendor transitions, and agility pilots.

Beyond 2026, quantum readiness will become a strategic differentiator: organizations
that act early will minimize migration risk, manage costs, and preserve customer trust as PQC
standards mature.

Signals to watch:

•  Cryptographic dependency inventories entering enterprise risk registers

•  Agility pilot programs testing hybrid and upgradeable encryption

By the end of 2025, the global thr eat environment had shifted toward a more fragmented
and unpredictable baseline. Power was exercised with fewer constraints, alliances proved more
situational, and diplomatic mechanisms were less effective at containing escalation. Rather
than isolated crises, instability increasingly emerged as a pattern of overlapping pressures,
with states more willing to test limits and absorb risk. This erosion of consensus reshaped
not only geopolitical dynamics but also the strategic calculus behind cyber operations, which
became an integral tool for shaping behavior, managing escalation, and positioning for future
conflict.

Cyber and information activity reflected a broader move toward persistence and ambiguity.

States and non-state threat actors alike prioritized durable access to digital ecosystems —
particularly identity and cloud infrastructure — while loosely affiliated threat actors amplified
disruption in ways that blurred attribution and intent. In parallel, cybercrime adapted to
sustained disruption by reorganizing into smaller, more flexible networks, often flourishing
where governance gaps and regional instability provided cover. Rapid advances in artificial
intelligence further accelerated these trends, compressing timelines, widening participation,
and increasing the scale of potential impact. Looking ahead to 2026, organizations should
expect a threat landscape defined less by discrete events than by continuous pressure, where
preparedness, operational resilience, and strategic foresight will matter more than reactive
defenses alone.

not simply react to incidents. Recorded Future enables organizations to do exactly that by
