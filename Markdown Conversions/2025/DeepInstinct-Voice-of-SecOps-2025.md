## Introduction

AI has become a routine part of doing business in 2025. It’s changing how we operate across every functional area, including SecOps. Almost three-quarters (72%) of organizations say they’ve revised their cybersecurity strategy over the past year because of AI, and 86% have increased their use of AI within security operations.

The benefits of AI for cybersecurity are evident. Organizations are saving, on average, 12 hours per week in manual processing time thanks to AI. More than half of organizations (58%) have rolled out AI-powered threat detection and response tools to help protect their data.

However, when we delve deeper into the adoption, understanding, and operational response to AI, we uncover a more complex picture. Despite the transformational impact of the technology, a quarter of organizations say they haven’t changed their cybersecurity strategy at all from last year. Survey respondents ranked zero-day attacks as the least concerning risk, despite the devastation they cause. And while AI is a boon for cybersecurity teams, 69% say the technology is also contributing to SecOps burnout.

The vast range of technological capabilities encompassed by the term ‘AI’ is contributing to this complexity. Of the 500 senior cybersecurity experts we surveyed across the U.S., fewer than a third (28%) understand the relationship between machine learning and deep learning.

There is a strong correlation between this knowledge gap in certain sectors and exposure to cyber risk: for instance, 50% of critical infrastructure organizations suffered an AI-powered attack in the last year – the same sector that was least likely to be able to accurately distinguish between machine learning and deep learning.

Different types of AI are used to address different issues: deep learning is a branch of machine learning, and one of the only ways to prevent known and unknown cyber threats in the face of cybercriminals equipped with AI-powered tools.

Protecting organizational data against increasingly complex and unpredictable threats will require organizations to truly understand and capitalize on this emerging technology in order to win the race against bad actors who are determined to do the same.

**Methodology**
Sapio Research surveyed 500 senior cybersecurity experts from companies with 1000+ employees in the U.S. The interviews were conducted online in April 2025 using an email invitation and an online survey.

Respondents worked at organizations which operated in either financial services, technology, manufacturing, retail, healthcare, public sector, or critical infrastructure (such as telecoms, energy, utilities, and transportation).

C-suite is defined as those who hold chief, global, head of department, or director roles, while reports are those who hold a manager, administrator, analyst, team lead, or officer role.

  - 86% of organizations are using more AI in their cybersecurity operations.
  - 79% of businesses have fully or mostly implemented AI into their security tools, technologies, and workflows.
  - However, fundamental AI definitions are misunderstood, with 38% unaware of or unable to define the relationship between machine learning and deep learning.

  - 43% are very or extremely concerned about the future impact of quantum computing on SecOps workloads.

## Eye on AI: Understanding AI terminology

- **Artificial Intelligence (AI)**: Artificial Intelligence, also known as AI, is an umbrella term covering all methods and disciplines that result in any form of intelligence exhibited by machines. Nearly all software that’s currently being used in just about all industries employs some form of AI, even if it’s limited to some basic, manually coded procedures.
- **Deep Learning**: Deep learning is a family of algorithms within machine learning characterized by the use of neural networks to train and analyze data. Whereas machine learning requires users to extract, compute, and vectorize a subsection of the available information to feed into the algorithm, deep learning can be trained on 100% raw data. When applied to cybersecurity, deep learning autonomously predicts threats to stop unknown malware and zero-day attacks by identifying and preventing them before they can execute.
- **Machine Learning**: Machine learning is the leading subfield of AI, where systems are trained to recognize patterns in data or make predictions without being explicitly programmed for each task. However, the process still requires a degree of human involvement, particularly in selecting the right algorithms, preparing data, and defining which features the system should focus on.
- **AI Agents**: An AI agent (also referred to as agentic AI, or self-driving AI) is an autonomous entity that perceives its environment through sensors and acts upon that environment through actuators to achieve its goals. These goals can range from simple tasks to complex problem-solving.
- **Adversarial AI**: Adversarial AI refers to cyber threats that seek to inhibit the performance of AI or machine learning (ML) systems by manipulating or misleading them. It’s one of many techniques (in addition to automated hacking, AI-powered phishing, evasion techniques, deepfakes, etc.) that bad actors use to inflict harm.
- **Dark AI**: Dark AI is the programming of AI to carry out malicious activities such as cyberattacks.
- **Generative AI (GenAI)**: GenAI refers to algorithms or deep learning models that can be used to create new content, including text, images, and video. ChatGPT, Claude, and DALL-E are examples of GenAI.

## Section 1: AI cyber adoption grows, but practical gaps remain

AI is now an integral part of most organizations’ cybersecurity operations. But while the use of AI tools has become the norm – by organizations and bad actors alike – worrying gaps in understanding fundamental technologies, such as machine learning and deep learning, expose organizations to unnecessary risk.

### More than 4 in 5 organizations have increased their use of AI in security operations
AI in cybersecurity has progressed from proof-of-concept to daily reality, with 86% of respondents having increased their use of AI in SecOps since last year, and almost a third (32%) describing the increase as significant.

![Chart showing 32% increased significantly, 54% increased slightly, 12% no change, 2% decreased slightly]

### AI-powered tools are now the norm for cybersecurity
79% of businesses have fully or mostly implemented AI into their security tools, technologies, and workflows.

![Chart showing deployment areas: Threat detection and response (58%), Identity and access management (56%), Compliance and risk management (51%), Fraud prevention (47%), Incident analysis and triage (46%), Phishing detection and prevention (45%), We have not deployed AI-powered security tools (3%)]

### Two-thirds are confused about fundamental AI definitions
Combating advanced, AI-powered malware requires a strong foundational knowledge of AI, machine learning, and deep learning. Fewer than one-third (28%) of respondents accurately described deep learning as a type of machine learning.

![Chart showing: 34% (They are both types of AI), 28% (Deep Learning is a type of Machine Learning), 16% (Machine Learning is a type of Deep Learning), 11% (They are separate and unrelated fields), 10% (They are interchangeable terms), 1% (I don’t know)]

### Deep learning is misunderstood by almost 60% of IT professionals
As zero-day attacks become more frequent and devastating, legacy cybersecurity tools and strategies struggle to keep pace. Deep learning systems sit at the core of preventative strategies, but well over half (59%) of those surveyed were unable to correctly define the term.

![Chart showing definitions of deep learning: 41% (Correct definition), 18%, 14%, 14%, 13% (Incorrect definitions)]

## Section 2: AI’s rapid expansion increases cybersecurity concerns

Cybercriminals’ use of emerging technologies is testing organizations as they work to prevent AI-powered attacks. Alongside well-recognized threats such as phishing, cloud storage is proving vulnerable to breaches, while many organizations underestimate the risk of zero-day attacks.

### Cybercriminals have targeted 2 in 5 organizations with AI-powered attacks
38% of organizations have suffered an AI-powered cyberattack in the past 12 months.

![Chart showing 38% Yes, 60% No, 2% Unsure]

### 83% say cloud storage attacks are a major cause for concern
Alongside the perennial problem of phishing attacks, 83% of organizations say their greatest risk is weaponized files being uploaded to file storage locations, including local drives, Network Attached Storage (NAS), hybrid, or public cloud environments.

![Chart showing threat rankings: Phishing (84%), Weaponized files (83%), Ransomware (83%), Insider threats (83%), Fileless attacks (82%), Adversarial AI (82%), File-based attacks (82%), Supply chain attacks (81%), Deepfakes (80%), Nation-state attacks (80%), Biometric spoofing (79%), Potentially unwanted applications (79%), Disinformation campaigns (78%), Zero-day attacks (77%)]

### Targeted phishing and deepfake impersonations are on the rise
Almost half (46%) of respondents say their organization has suffered more targeted phishing attacks over the past year. Another 43% say they’ve seen a rise in the number of deepfake impersonations aimed at their business.

![Chart showing: Targeted phishing (46%), Deepfake impersonations (43%), DDoS (43%), Vishing (42%)]

### 98% take action to prepare for AI-powered threats
Activities to prepare for AI-powered cyber attacks are encouragingly widespread: 98% of organizations are assigning resources and budget to the problem.

![Chart showing: Increasing external security services (62%), Expanding or adopting security tools (61%), Implementing AI-specific security measures (58%), Developing internal security capabilities (52%), Taking no additional actions (2%)]

## Section 3: AI alleviates workloads, but SecOps burnout continues

AI is helping to automate threat monitoring, reduce human error, and give time back to SecOps teams. However, the pace at which emerging technologies are evolving is heaping pressure on those working in cybersecurity.

### Three-quarters use generative AI (GenAI) to make SecOps easier
The majority of SecOps teams (73%) feel the benefits of new technologies with more than a quarter (26%) saying GenAI makes their role significantly easier.

![Chart showing: 26% Significantly easier, 47% Somewhat easier, 12% No impact, 12% Somewhat harder, 2% Significantly harder]

### 12 hours saved per week through AI-powered security tools
On average, organizations save 12 hours per week after introducing AI-powered security tools.

![Chart showing: 0-2 hours (2%), 3-5 hours (12%), 6-10 hours (29%), 11-20 hours (39%), More than 20 hours (16%), No savings (1%), Still evaluating (2%)]

### Data analysis is the top use case for GenAI
GenAI tools are being used at scale across organizations, particularly for data analysis and insights (60%), cybersecurity (59%), and customer service (56%).

![Chart showing: Data Analysis (60%), Cybersecurity (59%), Customer Service (56%), Software Development (55%), Product Development (46%), Risk Management (41%), Content Creation (41%)]

### Despite the benefits, 69% say AI and other emerging technologies are contributing to burnout
More than 2 in 3 respondents (69%) say AI and other emerging technologies are responsible for increasing burnout.

![Chart showing: 4% Strongly agree, 8% Somewhat agree, 19% Neither, 27% Somewhat disagree, 42% Strongly disagree]

### The C-suite calls for a shift to preemptive cybersecurity strategies
In recognition of this urgent need to switch from reacting to preventing, nearly two-thirds (64%) say their board or C-suite is pressing them to prioritize a prevention-first strategy.

![Chart showing pressure levels: 21% Significantly more, 24% Somewhat more, 29% Same, 26% Less]

### Prevention tops the list of cybersecurity priority areas
Those who are revising their cybersecurity strategies put prevention top of the priority list: 81% are focusing more on preemptive security measures this year.
