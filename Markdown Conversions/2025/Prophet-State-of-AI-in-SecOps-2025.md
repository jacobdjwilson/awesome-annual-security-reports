## Key Findings

These findings come from a survey of 282 security leaders (CISOs, Security Directors, Managers, and Analysts) from companies over 1,000 employees primarily in the United States.

- **~960 alerts generated daily**: Companies of all sizes are experiencing a barrage of alerts. Smaller enterprises and middle-market companies generate ~500 alerts per day causing a substantial management burden while larger companies are facing a tsunami of data from the ~3,000 alerts per day.
- **~40% of alerts are never investigated**: A concerning 40% of alerts (on average) are never investigated, leaving organizations vulnerable to significant, avoidable security risks. Roughly 60% of security teams have reported that an ignored alert proved to be critical, leading to a direct impact on the organization's security posture.
- **57% of companies suppress detection rules**: To cope with the alert problem, approximately 57% of companies are actively suppressing detection rules, especially for cloud and identity. Companies are consciously accepting increased risk to manage their current operational limitations.
- **AI for Security is a top 3 priority**: "AI for security" has become a top-three priority for security leaders, following data security and cloud security.
- **55% of companies use AI for alert triage & investigation**: Most companies use AI in some capacity for triage and investigation. Of the AI non-users, nearly 60% plan to evaluate an AI SOC solution within the next year.
- **~60% of SOC workloads will be completed by AI in next 3 years**: Security leaders anticipate AI solutions will handle approximately 60% of SOC workloads within the next 3 years, fundamentally reshaping security operations.

## The Alert Problem

### Organizations have 17 tools on average, while larger companies have over 20 alert generating tools

![Chart showing average number of security tools by company size, ranging from ~8 tools for 250-999 employees to ~28 tools for 20,000+ employees.]

The number of security tools generating alerts deployed by companies directly correlates with their size. This indicates a growing complexity in the security landscape, particularly for larger organizations.

### Companies generate ~960 alerts daily; Very large enterprises reach over ~3,000

![Chart showing daily average number of alerts received by companies.]

Smaller enterprises and middle-market companies tend to see fewer alerts because they have fewer alert-generating tools, fewer users, and tend to be a lesser priority for cybercriminals.

### Organizations are taking risks by not investigating ~40% of alerts generated

The sheer volume of alerts has created a critical vulnerability: the alarm is ringing, but no one is answering. An average of ~40% of all alerts are simply ignored or not investigated.

### ~60% of security teams have ignored an alert that turned out to be critical

A staggering 61% of security teams admit to having ignored an alert that subsequently proved to be critical, leading to outcomes as severe as customer data exposure, system downtime, or significant operational interference.

### On average, alert dwell time is ~56 min while MTTI is ~70 min

- **Dwell Time (~56 min)**: The period from when an alert is fired from a detection tool to when it is picked up for review.
- **Mean Time to Investigate (~70 min)**: The average time it takes for a security team to thoroughly investigate a generated alert.

## The Pain in Organizations

### Alerts are being ignored because of the constrained SOC resources

SOCs are experiencing a vicious cycle: high alert volumes, inefficient processes, and a lack of automation lead to prolonged investigation times, analyst burnout, and turnover.

### To cope with the noise, 57% of organizations deliberately suppress detection rules

Organizations are intentionally disabling or not activating certain detection rules because their human security teams lack the resources to investigate all of them effectively.

### Top two detection gaps companies have due to resource constraints are Cloud and Identity

If given more triage and investigation resources, security teams would most urgently enable detections for Cloud (65%) and Identity (61%).

## The AI SOC Shift

### Though an emerging priority, AI for security already ranks among the top 3 priorities for security leaders

AI for Security has rapidly emerged to claim a spot among the top three (~33% of respondents), signifying its critical importance in modern cybersecurity strategies.

### The top use for AI in the SOC is for Alert triage and investigation

1. **Alert Triage and Investigation (67%)**: Automating the sifting through high volumes of alerts.
2. **Detection Engineering and Tuning (65%)**: Refining detection rules and reducing false positives.
3. **Threat Hunting (64%)**: Analyzing vast datasets for subtle patterns and anomalies.

### ~55% of organizations currently use AI in some capacity

While 55% report using AI, there is a divide between "bolt-on" AI features from generalist platforms and AI-native, pure-play SOC platforms designed for investigative depth.

### 45% of respondents are non-AI SOC users but have appetite to adopt in the next year

Approximately 95% of non-users are actively engaged in exploring AI, with 60% planning to evaluate solutions and 28% currently in the evaluation phase.

### The top metrics for tracking ROI of AI SOC tools are: MTTI, MTTR, and 24/7 coverage

- **Improved MTTI (42%)**
- **Improved MTTR (41%)**
- **Improved 24/7 Coverage (40%)**

### Data privacy and regulatory concerns are the leading barrier to AI for cybersecurity adoption

The leading barrier is data privacy or regulatory concerns (24%), followed by integration difficulty (18%) and concerns of AI replacing humans (15%).

### Cybersecurity leaders believe that ~60% of the SOC workload will be performed by AI solutions in the next 3 years

83% of security leaders believe that over half of the SOC workload will be completed by AI in the next 3 years.

### 6 Key considerations when evaluating AI SOC solutions

1. **Coverage**: Ability to handle alerts across identity, cloud, endpoint, email, network, and data.
2. **Accuracy**: Reducing false positives and catching real threats.
3. **Quality**: Depth, clear reasoning, and transparency in investigations.
4. **Workflow integration**: Connecting smoothly with existing systems.
5. **Time to Value**: Reducing dwell and investigation time without long setup.
6. **Data privacy & Security**: Ensuring data is not used to train external models.

## Methodology and Demographics

This report is based on an online survey conducted from May 22, 2025, to June 27, 2025, with 282 qualified security leaders. The survey utilized a mix of Likert scales, multiple-choice, and open-ended questions. Data was analyzed using descriptive statistics and thematic analysis.

## Glossary

- **Alert fatigue**: Overwhelm and desensitization of security analysts due to excessive alerts.
- **Alert suppression**: Intentionally limiting the number of detection rules due to limited capacity.
- **Alert triage and investigation**: The process of sifting through high volumes of security alerts.
- **Cloud Security**: Protecting data, applications, and infrastructure in cloud computing.
- **Data Security**: Measures to protect digital data from unauthorized access or theft.
- **Detection Engineering and Tuning**: Refining rules to reduce false positives.
- **Detection Rules**: Configurations that trigger alerts for suspicious activity.
- **Dwell Time**: The period an attacker remains undetected.
- **False Positive**: A security alert for a non-existent threat.
- **Mean Time to Investigate (MTTI)**: Average time to investigate an alert.
- **Mean Time to Respond (MTTR)**: Total time from alert firing to containment.
- **SOC (Security Operations Center)**: A centralized unit for monitoring and response.
- **SOC Workload**: The sum of tasks performed by a SOC team.
- **Tool sprawl**: The proliferation of numerous security tools.
- **24/7 Coverage**: Continuous, round-the-clock monitoring capabilities.
