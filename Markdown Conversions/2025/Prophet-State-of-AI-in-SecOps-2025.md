## Key Findings
These findings come from a survey of 282 security leaders (CISOs, Security Directors, Managers, and Analysts) from companies over 1,000 employees primarily in the United States.

- **~960 alerts generated daily**: Companies of all sizes are experiencing a barrage of alerts. Smaller enterprises and middle-market companies generate ~500 alerts per day, while larger companies are facing a tsunami of data from the ~3,000 alerts per day.
- **~40% of alerts are never investigated**: A concerning 40% of alerts (on average) are never investigated, leaving organizations vulnerable to significant, avoidable security risks. Roughly 60% of security teams have reported that an ignored alert proved to be critical.
- **57% of companies suppress detection rules**: To cope with the alert problem, approximately 57% of companies are actively suppressing detection rules, especially for cloud and identity.
- **AI for Security is a top 3 priority**: "AI for security" has become a top-three priority for security leaders, following data security and cloud security.
- **55% of companies use AI for alert triage & investigation**: Most companies use AI in some capacity for triage and investigation. Of the AI non-users, nearly 60% plan to evaluate an AI SOC solution within the next year.
- **~60% of SOC workloads will be completed by AI in next 3 years**: Security leaders anticipate AI solutions will handle approximately 60% of SOC workloads within the next 3 years, fundamentally reshaping security operations.

## The Alert Problem
A tax on SOC resources.

![Chart showing average number of security tools by company size]

The number of security tools generating alerts deployed by companies directly correlates with their size. Companies with over 20,000 employees report an average of nearly 30 security tools, while those with 250-999 employees use fewer than 10.

> "We have way too many data sources that generate alerts and information. Any kind of incident takes a really long time to analyze and correlate." — Security Lead, Financial Services Co.

### Daily Alert Volume
Very large enterprises (20,000+ employees) generate an average of ~3,000 alerts per day, while most large enterprises (10,000-19,999 and 5,000-9,999 employees) generate around 1,000 alerts daily.

> "Security personnel cite being woken up during the middle of the night for an alert only for it to be a false positive. This doesn't just happen during the night, the false positives keep pestering analysts 24/7." — SOC Manager, Financial Services Co.

### The Risk of Ignoring Alerts
Organizations are taking risks by not investigating ~40% of alerts generated. This leads to "alert fatigue," where analysts become desensitized and overwhelmed.

> "In regulated industries, like healthcare and financial services, you aren’t allowed to turn off certain alerts due to compliance rules - even if those alerts tend to be false positives. As more tools are brought on, it becomes an even bigger haystack to search through for real threats." — CISO, Financial Services Co.

### Critical Breaches
A staggering 61% of security teams admit to having ignored an alert that subsequently proved to be critical.

> "Unfortunately, we have to tune out or ignore alerts due to the high volume, and so far we have accepted the risks that comes with that. Thankfully we haven’t been burned yet…but, this is not a game of chance worth playing." — Director of Security, Technology Co.

### Dwell Time and MTTI
- **Average Dwell Time**: ~56 minutes.
- **Average MTTI (Mean Time to Investigate)**: ~70 minutes.

These metrics are "too slow to stop attackers." Phishing threats can take as little as 48 minutes to extract sensitive information.

## The Pain in Organizations
Analyst burnout, missed detections, poor automation.

### Top 3 Challenges
1. **Triage/Investigation takes too long** (36%)
2. **Gaps in 24/7 SOC coverage** (32%)
3. **Analyst burnout and/or turnover** (31%)

### Detection Rule Suppression
57% of organizations deliberately suppress detection rules to cope with noise. This is a coping mechanism that comes at the cost of reduced visibility.

> "Detection rule suppression tends to be high because of bad detection engineering processes. Lots of detection rules tend to trigger false alerts, and because of the weak internal processes, organizations tend to turn off the rule rather than go through the hassle of tuning it." — Staff Security Engineer, Technology Co.

### Detection Gaps
If given more resources, teams would prioritize enabling detections for:
1. **Cloud** (65%)
2. **Identity** (61%)

## The AI SOC Shift
How security leaders envision the future SOC.

### Top Security Priorities
1. **Data security** (53%)
2. **Cloud security** (37%)
3. **AI for security** (33%)

### AI Use Cases
1. **Alert Triage and Investigation** (67%)
2. **Detection Engineering and Tuning** (65%)
3. **Threat Hunting** (64%)

### Adoption Status
- 55% of organizations currently use AI in some capacity.
- 45% are non-users, but 95% of those have plans to evaluate or adopt AI within the next 12 months.

### ROI Metrics
1. **Improved 24/7 Coverage** (42%)
2. **Improved MTTI** (41%)
3. **Improved MTTR** (40%)

### Barriers to Adoption
1. **Data privacy or regulatory concerns** (24%)
2. **Integration difficulty** (18%)
3. **Concerns of AI replacing humans** (15%)

## Methodology & Demographics
This report is based on an online survey of 282 security leaders conducted from May 22, 2025, to June 27, 2025. Participants were primarily from North America (85%) and represented a wide range of industries, with Technology (46%) being the largest segment.

## Appendix
The size of the SOC team scales with the organization, but even large teams struggle with the "alert problem." 52% of organizations use an in-house SOC supported by automation, while 28% use a hybrid model (in-house + outsourced).

## Glossary
- **Alert fatigue**: Overwhelm and desensitization of security analysts due to excessive alerts.
- **Alert suppression**: Intentionally limiting the number of detection rules due to limited capacity.
- **Alert triage and investigation**: The process of sifting through high volumes of security alerts.
- **Dwell Time**: The period an attacker remains undetected within a system.
- **MTTI (Mean Time to Investigate)**: The average time to thoroughly investigate an alert.
- **MTTR (Mean Time to Respond)**: The total time from initial alert to complete containment.
- **Tool sprawl**: The proliferation of numerous security tools, leading to increased complexity.

[^1]: Note: All statistics and commentary are derived from the Prophet Security 2025 survey data.
