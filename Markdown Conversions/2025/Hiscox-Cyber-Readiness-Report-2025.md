## Introduction

The Hiscox Cyber Readiness Report is based on research conducted by Wakefield Research with 5,750 businesses, where the individuals responsible for their organisation’s cyber security strategy were interviewed. This means the owner, principal or partner for those companies with fewer than 50 employees, and either the CIO, CISO, or the director/VP of security or IT for those companies with 50-249 employees.

Research was conducted between 29 July and 8 August 2025, using an email invitation and an online survey, and respondents can be broken down by geography as follows: 1,000 respondents in the USA, UK, France, Germany and Spain respectively, 500 respondents in Ireland and 250 in Portugal.

As research data, this represents a cross-section of insured and uninsured businesses that may or may not have made an insurance claim following an incident. It is not necessarily indicative of our own claims experience or that of the wider insurance industry.

### Message from Eddie Lamb, Global Head of Cyber, Hiscox
The rise of digital commerce has created tremendous opportunities for small businesses. Perhaps most powerful of these recent developments is artificial intelligence (AI). But these opportunities also present challenges. As businesses embrace new technologies, they must also navigate a landscape where evolving cyber threats can jeopardise their success.

Our report also looks at the impact of AI on small businesses, for whom it can be both friend and foe. While AI brings opportunities, it also introduces new vulnerabilities—creating blind spot entry points and exposing gaps in data security.

The vast majority of SMEs (94%) plan to increase their cyber security and data protection investments over the next 12 months. That includes hiring cyber specialists, updating training programmes, conducting regular vulnerability checks, and reassessing risks across their supply chains.

## Executive summary

Nobody ever said keeping SMEs (small-to-medium-sized enterprises) secure was an easy task. This crucial group powers 50% of the global economy. One of their most challenging roles is that of risk assessor, which requires them to understand the constant evolution of threats facing their businesses.

In this ninth edition of Hiscox’s annual Cyber Readiness Report, we look at the impact of these fast-changing digital risks and how small businesses can take action to mitigate their exposure.

- **Investment**: Almost all SMEs (94%) are expecting to increase cyber security and data protection investments in the next 12 months.
- **Resilience**: Thanks to a proactive approach, 83% of organisations report improved cyber resilience in the past 12 months.
- **Disclosure**: A significant majority (71%) of firms believe that disclosing the amounts of ransom demands paid should be mandatory.
- **AI Threats**: 60% view AI-driven social engineering and AI malware/phishing attacks as top emerging threats over the next five years.

## Key findings

- **59%** of respondents experienced a cyber attack in the last 12 months.
- **33%** were fined a significant amount after a cyber incident.
- **94%** are increasing investment in cyber security and data protection.
- **60%** are recruiting additional staff to increase their cyber resilience.
- **88%** conduct quarterly supplier and partner risk assessments.
- **91%** perform cyber vulnerability checks at least once a quarter.
- **27%** experienced a ransomware attack in the last 12 months.
- **71%** support mandatory disclosure of ransomware payments.

## State of attacks

Nearly three-out-of-five (59%) companies have experienced at least one cyber attack in the last 12 months. Among firms that reported incidents, the average number of attacks ranged from about three in sectors like chemicals, property, and media to roughly eight in nonprofits.

### Common Points of Entry
- **IoT Devices**: 33%
- **Supply Chain/Vendor Websites**: 28%
- **Cloud-based Corporate Servers**: 27%
- **AI Tools and Software**: 15%

### Ransomware Outcomes
For those who paid a ransom, 60% recovered some or all of their data. However, paying a ransom does not always solve the problem:
- 31% of those who paid had attackers demand more money.
- 27% of those who paid sustained an additional attack.

### Impact of Attacks
- **Financial**: 33% incurred significant fines.
- **Performance**: 30% reported lower business performance.
- **Human**: 39% reported high stress for employees, 32% reported burnout, and 31% reported an uptick in sick days.

## Taking action

Nearly all companies are allocating resources to prevent attacks, with 94% expecting to increase investment in cyber security and data protection in the next year.

- **Remote Work**: 79% have invested in additional cyber security training for remote workers.
- **Compliance**: 81% are actively adapting to meet increasing cyber security regulatory requirements.
- **Vulnerability Checks**: 91% conduct checks at least once a quarter.
- **Supply Chain**: 88% do risk assessments at least once a quarter for suppliers and partners.

---

## Cyber resilience

The vast majority (83%) have improved their cyber resilience in the last 12 months. 

### Methods of Improvement
- **Staffing**: 70% hiring additional staff members.
- **Training**: 60% updating the types of cyber security training.
- **Software**: 54% investing in software to identify and manage threats.

---

## AI and future threats

Nearly all who have experienced an attack (96%) believe better awareness or understanding of cyber attacks and procedures is key to better response times.

### AI Perception
Almost two-thirds (65%) of those responsible for their SME’s security consider AI more of an asset than a vulnerability. However, the top three emerging AI-driven threats in the next five years are:
1. Social engineering attacks (60%)
2. AI malware and phishing attacks (60%)
3. AI taking control of company data (60%)

### Planning for the AI Threat
- 37% ensure insurance policies include AI risks.
- 36% train employees on AI threat awareness.
- 36% perform regular AI usage audits.

## Mandatory disclosures

A first-of-its-kind law in Australia requires companies to disclose the cost of any ransom payment within 72 hours. While 71% of respondents agree with the concept of disclosing ransom payment costs, there is significant debate:

- **Arguments for**: 54% say it gives stakeholders a clearer picture of financial health; 52% believe it aids authorities.
- **Arguments against**: 53% feel private firms should not be obligated to reveal financial details; 49% warn it could incentivise bad actors.

## Country comparisons

- **Cyber vulnerability**: Germany (67%) and the UK (65%) were most likely to experience an attack.
- **Data recovery**: The USA (74%) had the highest rate of data recovery after a ransomware attack.
- **Regulation**: Portugal (86%) and Ireland (85%) are most confident in their ability to adapt to new regulations.
- **AI**: Portugal (86%) is most likely to view AI as a security asset.
- **Mandatory disclosure**: The USA (80%) has the strongest support for mandatory disclosure.
- **Insurance**: France has the lowest take-up rate for cyber insurance at 61%.

## Cyber security tips for SMEs

1. **Install a reputable software security package**: Use AI-powered tools that combine antivirus, firewall, and data back-up.
2. **Use a password manager and robust authentication**: Implement complex passwords and multi-factor authentication (MFA).
3. **Keep your systems and software up-to-date**: Enable automated updates to patch security vulnerabilities.
4. **Back-up company data securely**: Test back-up processes regularly to ensure data can be restored.
5. **Be selective about who can access data**: Restrict permissions based on specific roles and review them regularly.
