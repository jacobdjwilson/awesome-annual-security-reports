Artificial intelligence (AI) is reshaping how business gets done. That includes IT and security, where new agentic AI use cases are blossoming. AI in cybersecurity and IT operations offers tremendous benefits, but it also introduces commensurate risk to the enterprise if organizations are not able to set and enforce AI governance policies aligned with their risk appetite.

As organizations increasingly deploy AI, they must begin treating those entities with the same level of scrutiny and governance as privileged employees, contractors, or partners. This shift demands significant changes in how identity and access management (IAM) is architected.

To understand where the industry is today, with agentic AI vs generative AI deployments and risk management, Delinea recently commissioned a global survey of 1,758 IT decision-makers. This includes stakeholders from the U.S., UK, UAE, Australia, Singapore, and Germany.

![Infographic showing 94% of global companies use or pilot AI in IT, 59% use both generative and agentic AI, 56% run into shadow AI monthly, and 44% have security architecture equipped for secure AI.]

## Large firms need AI to keep up with speed of IT Ops

Most IT organizations are already betting big on AI to improve IT operations. A full 84% are already actively using some form of AI in IT operations—whether generative AI, agentic AI, or some combination of the two. A further 10% of firms are piloting or evaluating AI tools for use in their IT departments.

> "Agentic AI serves an important role in the automation and productivity programs across our IT organization. It’s also very important to our broader enterprise use cases given the volume and diversity of interactions with customers and consumers."
> – CISO, Healthcare Organization

Both agentic AI and generative AI offer significant potential to streamline operations and improve efficiency in organizations of all sizes. However, the upsides of these new technologies also bring significant emerging risks that demand a rethink of traditional policy controls.

## Organizations are grappling with AI risks

The vast majority of organizations are already using AI for IT operations and AI usage is proliferating across the business. But the hard reality is that their security architecture is not yet ready to support secure AI operations.

Less than half of organizations (44%) report that their security architecture is fully equipped to support secure AI. Meanwhile, 47% of organizations are hopeful that their security roadmap will bolster the security of AI deployments within two years.

When it comes to identity risk introduced by AI, respondents’ most common concern was malicious actors’ use of AI. Some 47% of global firms reported that AI-generated phishing/deepfakes were their top concern, and 44% reported that AI-driven credential theft is one of their biggest concerns.

## Generative AI and agentic AI bring different risks

While there are many use cases that deploy a combination of generative AI and agentic AI, decision-makers should understand that each one is a distinct type of artificial intelligence with its own strengths, limitations, and risks.

![Table showing top identity security risk by industry: Global (Phishing/deepfakes 47%), Finance (Agentic AI unchecked access 47%), Legal (Phishing/deepfakes 47%), Sales/Media/Marketing (Shadow AI 38%), IT/Telecom (Phishing/deepfakes 38%), Healthcare (Credential theft 52%).]

## Generative AI risks

Generative AI creates content in response to user input. In the context of IT use cases, this could include code generation, production of new knowledge base documents, data analysis, or generation of report summaries based on data. These technologies help speed up human-led activities, but on their own, they don’t act independently. The risks are usually more contained in scope, revolving around data and privacy-related issues.

## Agentic AI risks

Agentic AI is designed to act autonomously to achieve pre-determined goals. AI agents automatically use data to make decisions, manage workflows, and orchestrate multistep tasks. The problem with agentic AI—especially in IT use cases—is that it significantly ups the ante on risk. It brings all the same data-related risks of generative AI but adds the force multiplier of autonomy. AI agents are executing actions and decisions at scale without human intervention.

## Emerging AI risk cases

- **Overprivileged AI agents**: Agentic AI with excessive privilege and access to sensitive systems can expose organizations to the visibility and extraction of sensitive data.
- **Extreme data exposure**: Systems are often given access to tremendous troves of data, and many are experimental and may not be well-vetted.
- **Automation gone rogue**: Agentic AI "going rogue" and autonomously making undesired changes or causing outages is a very real security assurance concern.
- **Goal hijacking**: Adversarial input that targets agentic AI workflows and corrupts AI decision-making paths can help attackers subvert the goals of the agent.

## AI policies struggle to keep up with shadow AI risks

Woven throughout these AI risk scenarios is the reality that many generative AI and agentic AI deployments still lack the kind of transparency and visibility demanded of traditional automated IT systems. Shadow AI is the unsanctioned use of AI tools by employees or departments without oversight from IT or security teams.

## AI controls are lacking

Digging further into the governance and visibility measures most commonly used, our data shows that many companies still have a ways to go before they’re able to establish full visibility into their environments.

## Organizations’ confidence in machine identity management may be misplaced

The vast majority of organizations (93%) are confident that their machine identity security efforts are keeping pace with emerging threats, like AI manipulation. This confidence, however, may be misplaced, given the limitations in AI visibility at many organizations.

> "Identity is very important in the era of GenAI and Agentic AI. The AI needs to only operate where the identity can be confirmed and validated. This should be paired with audit logs that can track AI activity, which can be ingested in a security platform for detection and monitoring of AI risks."
> – CISO, Healthcare Organization

## Agentic AI demands agentic security

This growing disconnect reveals a widening governance gap. Agentic AI agents will require more granular and dynamic access controls than traditional role-based access control.

- **AI-to-AI credential brokering**: Uses AI to help automate the machine-to-machine exchange and the verification of digital credentials.
- **Visual digital identity mapping**: Finding ways to achieve a visual mapping of digital identities, including AI personas, agent IDs, and training model metadata.
- **Strengthen PAM**: Strengthen privileged access management (PAM) models to ensure monitoring and control to detect anomalies.

## 5 steps companies can take today to manage agentic AI risks

1. **Discover and classify AI identities**: Use automated tools to inventory AI agents across hybrid and multi-cloud environments.
2. **Define roles and guardrails**: Set clear operational boundaries for each AI identity classification.
3. **Enforce least privilege, just-in-time access**: Replace standing privileges with just-in-time access.
4. **Authenticate and authorize by intent**: Require strong, verifiable identities for AI-to-system and AI-to-AI interactions.
5. **Monitor, detect, and continuously improve**: Continuously monitor AI behavior to detect anomalies and misuse.

## Leading firms use AI in security operations and identity security

An overwhelming 91% of global organizations say that they’re either already using, piloting, or planning a deployment in the next 12 months to integrate AI to improve security operations.

## Next steps for stronger identity security in the AI era

Organizations need to take a programmatic approach to AI risk management that enables human-centric AI governance. We recommend a three-phase crawl, walk, run approach:

- **PHASE 1: Foundational**: Form an AI governance committee, identify and catalog all AI tools, implement baseline access controls, and provide foundational AI security training.
- **PHASE 2: Strengthen controls**: Expand access management to enforce least privilege, deploy advanced PAM and logging, and enhance supply chain security through vendor assessments.
- **PHASE 3: Full maturity**: Adopt continuous adaptive risk assessment, leverage AI-driven analytics for anomaly detection, automate compliance monitoring, and foster a security-first culture.
