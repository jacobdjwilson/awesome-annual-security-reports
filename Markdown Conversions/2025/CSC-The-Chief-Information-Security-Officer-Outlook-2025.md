## Introduction
Driven in part by the rapid rise of artificial intelligence (AI), the variety and intensity of cybersecurity threats to organizations continue to expand. Today’s bad actors have access to increasingly sophisticated methods—including deepfakes and domain generation algorithms (DGAs)—posing a growing challenge for businesses to anticipate and defend against. At the same time, chief information security officers (CISOs) and their teams must continue to guard against more established security threats such as distributed denial of service (DDoS) attacks.

In Q1 2025, CSC commissioned independent research among CISOs, chief information officers (CIOs), and other senior IT professionals to understand more about their current concerns. Our study found almost three quarters (70%) of respondents believe that security threats will increase in the year ahead; almost all (98%) predict an increase in the next three years. Almost nine in 10 (87%) believe that DGAs powered by AI pose a threat.

## What CISOs are saying: A snapshot
We surveyed 300 CISOs, CIOs, and heads of IT in Q1 2025 and found that cybersecurity threats are material risks that are becoming more challenging.

- **67%** of respondents said cybersecurity threats were either critical or significant in 2024.
- **70%** expect an increase in threats in 2025.
- **98%** believe risks will rise over the next three years.

## Domain and DNS threats will dominate the threat landscape
The top three security threats in 2024 were cited as:
1. Cybersquatting
2. Domain and domain name system (DNS) hijacking
3. DDoS attacks
4. Ransomware and malware
5. Social media cyber attacks and defamation
6. Phishing and social engineering
7. Other

The top three expected threats over the next three years are:

## Cyber threats are evolving—and only getting more complex
Almost one in 10 (9%) of our respondents said the risks presented by cybersecurity threats were “critical” in 2024. Three fifths (58%) rated them as significant, meaning that two thirds (67%) thought risks were material. A further 30% said that risks were neither critical nor insignificant.

## Domain-related threats dominate CISOs’ concerns
Only 22% said they have the “right tools” in place. 
- **76%** of respondents use a trusted DNS provider to manage digital threats.
- **99%** are either somewhat or very concerned that their domain registrars were not following Know Your Customer (KYC) policies.
- **59%** said when their firm detects domain-linked cyber threats, they have tools and processes in place to mitigate them, but it's a complex and time-consuming process.

## AI is playing a role in combating cyber threats—but its widespread use is still a cause for concern
The biggest return on investment (ROI) in AI integration is process automation, followed by internal education and data analysis. However, 87% of respondents believe that DGAs powered by AI pose a threat to their organization. Furthermore, 97% are concerned about giving AI-based third-party systems access to company data.

## Ihab Shraim’s top five measures to establish an AI governance policy
1. **Establish a clear AI policy**: Create a formal policy communicated across the business.
2. **Set explicit data sharing rules**: Define what data can and cannot be input into LLMs.
3. **Use isolated environments for testing**: Run AI models in secure, private environments.
4. **Define specific use cases**: Build models with clear objectives to prevent data contamination.
5. **Validate AI outputs**: Human oversight remains essential.

## Focus on Shadow AI
Shadow AI refers to the unsanctioned use of AI tools by employees or vendors. The risks include data breaches and compliance failures. The recommended solution is to use software agents that track all LLMs used across an organization and apply a zero-trust framework.

## IT security budgets may not be keeping up with the growing cyber threat landscape
Only 7% of respondents said their overall budget for cybersecurity had increased significantly between 2024 and 2025, while 80% said it had increased moderately. Allocations are most likely to be decided by the chief risk officer (CRO) (23%), CFO or finance team (21%), or by the CISO or IT team (18%).

## NIS2 compliance remains a work in progress for many organizations
Only 9% say their organization is fully compliant with the Network and Information Security Directive 2 (NIS2). The biggest challenges are ensuring compliance among external partners (73%) and difficulty in understanding complex regulations (64%).

## Strategic outsourcing helps CISOs manage complexity at scale
- **48%** mainly use in-house systems.
- **30%** mainly outsource to specialists.
- **18%** in-source exclusively.
- **4%** outsource everything.

## Conclusion
CISOs are encouraged to:
- Adopt a well-structured, multi-layered security strategy.
- Establish a GRC program with a focus on user monitoring, employee education, and supplier data security.
- Partner with a trusted and forward-thinking provider.
