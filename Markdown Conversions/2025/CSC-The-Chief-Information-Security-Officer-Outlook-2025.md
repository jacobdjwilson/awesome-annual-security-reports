## Introduction
Driven in part by the rapid rise of artificial intelligence (AI), the variety and intensity of cybersecurity threats to organizations continue to expand. Today’s bad actors have access to increasingly sophisticated methods—including deepfakes and domain generation algorithms (DGAs)—posing a growing challenge for businesses to anticipate and defend against. 

In Q1 2025, CSC commissioned independent research among CISOs, chief information officers (CIOs), and other senior IT professionals to understand more about their current concerns. Our study found almost three quarters (70%) of respondents believe that security threats will increase in the year ahead; almost all (98%) predict an increase in the next three years.

## What CISOs are saying: A snapshot
We surveyed 300 CISOs, CIOs, and heads of IT in Q1 2025:
- **67%** of respondents said cybersecurity threats were either critical or significant in 2024.
- **70%** expect an increase in threats in 2025.
- **98%** believe risks will rise over the next three years.

## Domain and DNS threats will dominate the threat landscape
The top three security threats in 2024 were:
1. Cybersquatting
2. Domain and domain name system (DNS) hijacking
3. DDoS

The top three expected threats over the next three years are:
1. Cybersquatting
2. Domain and DNS hijacking
3. Ransomware and malware

## Cyber threats are evolving—and only getting more complex
CISOs face a rising tide of ever-more sophisticated cyber threats. 
- **9%** rated risks as "critical" in 2024.
- **58%** rated them as "significant."
- **30%** said risks were neither critical nor insignificant.

"As organizations began moving core systems away from in-house, on-premise infrastructure to the cloud, they opened up their IT environments to new threats," says Mark Flegg.

## Domain-related threats dominate CISOs’ concerns
- **76%** are only "somewhat confident" in their ability to mitigate domain attacks.
- **99%** are concerned that domain registrars are not following Know Your Customer (KYC) policies.
- **59%** have mitigation tools, but find the process complex and time-consuming.

## AI is playing a role in combating cyber threats
While AI aids in process automation and staff training, it is a double-edged sword.
- **87%** believe DGAs powered by AI pose a threat.
- **97%** are concerned about giving AI-based third-party systems access to company data.

## Expert View: Why domain-related threats are a growing challenge
Ihab Shraim notes: "DNS and domain-related infrastructure are soft targets for cybercriminals, who use specific threat vectors like DNS hijacking or domain spoofing to exploit exposed systems. We’re already seeing high volumes of these attacks, and we expect them to grow drastically in 2025."

## Ihab Shraim’s top five measures to establish an AI governance policy
1. **Establish a clear AI policy**: Communicate across the business.
2. **Set explicit data sharing rules**: Define what data can be input into LLMs.
3. **Use isolated environments for testing**: Run models in private, secure environments.
4. **Define specific use cases**: Build models with clear objectives.
5. **Validate AI outputs**: Human oversight remains essential.

## Focus on Shadow AI
Shadow AI refers to the unsanctioned use of AI tools by employees. Mark Eggleston suggests using software agents to track LLMs across the organization and applying a zero-trust framework to validate users and enforce control.

## IT security budgets may not be keeping up
- **80%** of respondents saw only a moderate increase in budget between 2024 and 2025.
- **7%** saw a significant increase.
- Allocations are most often decided by the Chief Risk Officer (23%) or CFO (21%).

## NIS2 compliance remains a work in progress
Only 9% of organizations are fully compliant with NIS2. The biggest challenges are ensuring compliance among external partners (73%) and the complexity of the regulations (64%).

## Strategic outsourcing helps CISOs manage complexity at scale
- **48%** use in-house systems primarily.
- **30%** outsource to specialists.
- **18%** in-source exclusively.

"An experienced partner can hold your hand through the evolutions of different cybersecurity risks," says Nina Hrichak.

## Conclusion
CISOs must adopt a multi-layered security strategy, establish a GRC program focused on user monitoring and supplier security, and partner with a trusted provider to enhance resilience.
