Modern cybersecurity isn’t failing because organizations are ignoring threats—it’s failing because they’re looking in the wrong direction.

While security teams work to secure their own networks, attackers are already finding ways in through the back door—exploiting vendors, suppliers, and software providers to infiltrate organizations without ever touching their carefully monitored perimeters.

In 2024, at least 35.5% of all data breaches originated from third-party compromises, up 6.5% from the previous year. This number is likely an undercount, as many organizations may be unaware of or choose not to disclose the third-party origins of their breaches.

So while you’re updating your firewall rules, somewhere in your supply chain a vendor might be inadvertently letting in the very attackers you’ve been working to keep out. And these attackers have figured out it’s much easier to enter through a partner that already has trusted access.

At SecurityScorecard, we believe you can’t fix what you can’t see. That’s why we analyzed 1,000 data breaches across all industries and geographic areas to uncover the true scale of third-party cyber risk. Our mission is to help you illuminate these blind spots and transform how you manage your extended security perimeter.

## Don’t let a third party reality check become checkmate

More than 1 in 3 breaches (35.5%) now come through third parties—attackers aren’t breaking in; they’re logging in with your partners’ credentials while you’re busy patching your own systems.

**Industries in the crosshairs**
- **Retail & Hospitality**: A staggering 52.4% of all breaches come through third parties.
- **Critical Infrastructure**: Energy and utilities face a 46.7% third-party breach rate.
- **Technology industries**: Experience disproportionately high rates of third-party breaches relative to total breach volume.
- **Healthcare**: Has a large share of third-party breaches due to high overall breach volume.

**The cascading impact**
- 4.5% of breaches now extend to fourth parties—one breach triggers multiple organizational failures.
- Foreign subsidiaries are 2× more likely to be breach sources than domestic ones.

**Global vulnerability landscape**
More affluent countries may have stronger security but also have higher third-party breach rates:
- Singapore: 71.4%
- Netherlands: 70.4%
- Japan: 60%
- The U.S. has the largest absolute number of third-party breaches due to overall high breach volume.

**The calculated attack strategy**
- Chinese state-sponsored groups actively target supply chains and third-party access points across North America, Europe, and Asia.

**The bottom line**
- **Ransomware Connection**: 41.4% of ransomware attacks now start through third parties.
- **Software Supply Chain Under Siege**: File transfer software vulnerabilities have become the most common attack vector for third-party breaches.
- **State-sponsored cyber espionage**: Campaigns from China frequently leverage third-party access.
- **Adversaries use third-party access vectors for scalability**: Compromising multiple organizations through a single vulnerable entry point.
- **Traditional security is looking in the wrong direction**: Leaving organizations vulnerable to attacks that bypass their own defenses entirely.

## The Data
### OSINT, Threat Intelligence & Underground Research
Unlike other reports that rely solely on self-reported data, this study integrates real-world breach intelligence gathered by SecurityScorecard’s STRIKE Threat Intelligence Unit. Most breaches in our sample were not third-party related—this was intentional to provide a broader comparison sample and identify ways in which third-party breaches differ from the general risk landscape.

Our definition of a third-party breach includes:
- **Lateral movement attacks**: When attackers use compromised access at a vendor, supplier, or partner to infiltrate the networks of their business-to-business (B2B) customers or other partners.
- **Vendor data breaches**: When data from one organization is compromised while in the custody of a vendor, supplier, or service provider.

Security teams can no longer afford to treat third-party security as a compliance checkbox. Traditional vendor risk assessments—conducted annually or quarterly—are too slow to detect active threats. While you’re scheduling your next quarterly assessment, attackers are already three vendors deep into your supply chain.

## Top Takeaways

### The Rising Tide of Third-Party Breaches
35.5% of all breaches in 2024 were third-party related, a 6.5% increase from 2023. This number is likely conservative, as many third-party breaches go unreported or are mistakenly assumed to be internal incidents.

### Geopolitical & Regional Trends
- North America accounted for 59% of total breaches but only 53% of third-party breaches.
- Europe, Northeast Asia, and Oceania exhibited higher relative rates of third-party breaches.
- Chinese state-sponsored groups continue to use third-party attack vectors in supply chain compromises.

### Industries at Highest Risk
- **Healthcare, Pharmaceuticals, and Biotechnology**: Had the highest volume of third-party breaches.
- **Retail, Hospitality, and Consumer Goods**: Experienced the highest relative third-party breach rates.
- **Energy, Utilities, and Critical Infrastructure**: Had a disproportionately high rate of third-party breaches.

### Attackers & Threat Vectors
- The ransomware group C10p remains the most prolific third-party attacker.
- RansomHub is emerging as the most dominant non-C10p ransomware group.
- UNC5537’s campaign against Snowflake cloud services significantly contributed to cloud platforms becoming the second most common third-party attack vector in 2024.

### The Role of Technology in Third-Party Breaches
- 46.75% of third-party breaches involved technology products and services.
- File transfer software remained the top third-party breach enabler.

### Third-Party Breaches & Ransomware: A Growing Nexus
- 41.4% of ransomware and extortion incidents had a third-party breach component.

### The Affluence Factor
- A correlation is emerging between economic development and third-party breach frequency. Wealthier nations experience more third-party attacks due to their interconnected supply chains.

## Third-Party vs. Fourth-Party Breach Trends
### The Expanding Risk Chain
- **Third-Party Breaches Are Rising Fast**: 35.5% of breaches (355 cases) involved a third-party nexus.
- **Fourth-Party Breaches Create Cascading Failures**: 45 breaches extended beyond third parties to involve yet another organization in the supply chain (4.5% of all breaches).
- **The Hidden Iceberg**: The true scale of third-party and fourth-party breaches is likely higher than reported.

## Distribution of Third-Party Breaches by Industry
### Understanding the Three Key Metrics
1. **Total Breach Count by Industry**: Healthcare leads with 24.2% of total breaches.
2. **Third-Party Breach Count by Industry**: Healthcare leads with 22% of third-party breaches.
3. **Third-Party Breach Rate Within Each Industry**: Retail & Hospitality leads with 52.4% of its breaches coming through third parties.

## Retail & Hospitality and Technology & Telecommunications Lead in Third-Party Breach Exposure
- **Retail & Hospitality**: The only industry with a third-party breach majority (52.4%).
- **Technology & Telecommunications**: 47.3% of breaches involve third parties.
- **The Big Three**: Together with Healthcare, these three industries account for 51.8% of all third-party breaches.

## Energy and Transportation Sectors Show Alarming Third-Party Breach Rates
- **Energy, Utilities, and Critical Infrastructure**: Third-highest frequency of third-party breaches at 46.7%.
- **Travel, Transportation, and Logistics**: Fourth-highest third-party breach rate at 45.3%, largely attributed to a targeted campaign by the C10p ransomware group.

## Third-Party Breach Hotspots
- **Regional Risk Profile**: Northeast Asia stands as the only region where more than half of all breaches (54.3%) involve third parties.
- **Nation-Level Analysis**: Singapore (71.4%), the Netherlands (70.4%), and Japan (60%) show the highest third-party breach rates.

## The Wealth Effect
- **The Wealth-Risk Paradox**: Organizations in wealthier countries generally maintain stronger security, yet face disproportionately high third-party breach risks.
- **The Security Bypass Effect**: Attackers respond to strong internal security by seeking third-party vectors.
- **The Complexity Multiplier**: Advanced economies feature more extensive outsourcing and business interdependencies.

## Products and Services at the Center of Supply Chain Risk
- **Top 10 Sources**: File transfer software (14%), Cloud products & services (8.25%), and Foreign subsidiaries & acquisitions (7.75%) lead the list.

## Third-Party Breach Enablers
- While technology products and services enabled 46.75% of third-party relationships involved in breaches, over half of the breaches (53.25%) stemmed from non-technology sources.

## Healthcare and Financial Services
- Despite having below-average third-party breach rates, these industries account for a disproportionate share of industry-specific relationships involved in breaches.

## Subsidiaries & Acquisitions: The Hidden Third-Party Risk Within Your Own Organization
- Subsidiaries and acquisitions account for 11.75% of third-party breaches globally. Foreign entities appear more frequently in breach data than domestic ones.

## File Transfer Software
- File transfer software remains the most exploited third-party access point. The C10p group dominated this vector through major campaigns targeting supply chain software.

## Threat Actors
- **Ransomware groups**: 64.8% of breaches.
- **Non-ransomware criminals**: 21.9% of breaches.
- **State-sponsored actors**: 13.3% of breaches.
- **C10p** remains the most prolific group, responsible for 17% of attributable breaches.

## Ransomware and Third-Party Attacks
- 41.4% of ransomware/extortion attacks had third-party nexuses. Third-party attack vectors offer high scalability, enabling attackers to compromise multiple victims through a single entry point.

## Third-Party Software Vulnerabilities
- Third-party software vulnerabilities served as access vectors in 8.5% of breaches. The C10p Cleo campaign and Ivanti VPN vulnerabilities were primary drivers.

## Customized Third-Party Risk Management Action Items
- **Match Your TPRM Strategy to Your Risk Profile**: Account for industry, geography, and technology stack.
- **Demand “Secure by Design” Technology**: Integrate security requirements into procurement.
- **Harden High-Risk Infrastructure**: Prioritize file transfer software, cloud, and VPNs.
- **Mitigate Fourth-Party Risk**: Require vendors to maintain their own robust TPRM programs.
- **Avoid Ransomware Payments**: Payments create legal risk and fund future attacks.
