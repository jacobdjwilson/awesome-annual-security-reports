## Executive Summary

**Agentic AI adoption is accelerating faster than security maturity.**

Agentic AI has arrived not as an incremental innovation but as an architectural shift, one that changes how software behaves, how systems interact, and how threats propagate. Organizations are now moving from deterministic workflows to autonomous chains of actions, where agents can interpret, decide, and execute in ways that blur the boundaries between code, identity, and access.

**Agentic AI is anything but predictable. It hasn't just expanded the attack surface. It has atomized it.**

Securing autonomous systems requires new controls, new playbooks, and a new operational mindset. The organizations that act now will set the standard for safe autonomy in 2026 and beyond.

### The Emerging Risk Gap

This shift introduces a category of risks that current security programs were never designed to manage. Visibility remains limited, tooling is fragmented, and attackers increasingly use AI to automate reconnaissance and exploitation.

Findings show rapid deployment of agents across workflows, while foundational controls such as inventories, governance models, runtime guardrails, and monitoring, have not kept pace.

The 2025 State of Agentic AI Security Report offers a benchmark security leaders can use to evaluate posture, prioritize investment, and build the governance required for safe scale.

## Agentic AI Governance Gap Is Driving a Lack of Confidence in Securing AI

**79% of organizations do not have a security and governance policy for AI agents and MCPs.**

**Without policy, agents operate inside undefined trust boundaries.**

Inventory gaps are compounded by a second critical weakness: the absence of unified policy.

Nearly four in five enterprises have no formal governance or security policy defining how AI agents and MCP connections should be onboarded, permissioned, monitored, or managed.

### Does your organization have a security and governance policy for AI agents and MCPs?

**The new risk surface is what an agent can do, not what it can say.**

Early this year, AI security conversations revolved around prompts, how they were crafted, how they could be manipulated, and how they shaped model outputs. Agentic AI breaks this paradigm entirely. Autonomous agents are no longer producing static responses; they are executing actions across enterprise systems.

As adoption accelerates across teams and business units, the visibility and governance gaps widen in parallel, unless organizations establish a foundational layer of agent inventory + policy framework.

**Inventory alone doesn't prevent misuse. But without inventory, governance cannot begin.**

Without inventory and governance, agentic AI operates beyond the security team's line of sight. This will become the clearest dividing line between responsible early adopters and those who inherit compounding, silent risk.

Without policy frameworks, organizations have:
- No consistent standards for agent permissions
- No identity or authentication requirements
- No approval workflows for tool or connector onboarding
- No baseline expectations for monitoring or audits

**Enterprises are deploying autonomous systems into governance vacuums.**

### Confidence is low!

**42% of respondents are unconfident and 30% are neutral about securing agent-to-system interactions, underscoring major gaps in visibility and control.**

Security teams lack the visibility, tooling, and guardrails required to fully understand, let alone constrain, what agents can do across their environment.

## Top Threat Concerns for Agentic AI

### INDUSTRY WIDE

Across the survey, six threat categories consistently surfaced as the dominant risks shaping the agentic security landscape. Each represents an evolution from static models to autonomous, multi-part workflows.

- **Supply chain risks (LLMs, Agents, MCPs)**
- **Data Leakage or unauthorized Exposure**
- **Prompt Injection or Manipulation Attacks**
- **Uncontrolled Autonomous Actions**
- **Regulatory or Compliance Violations**
- **Agent Impersonation or Spoofing**

**Supply chain risks (LLM plug-ins, MCP integrations)**: Every plug-in or MCP integration becomes a new, unvetted execution surface. A compromised integration can redirect agents, steal data, or trigger unsafe actions.
**Prompt Injection Attacks**: Attackers can steer agents into unsafe actions, bypassing guardrails, triggering unauthorized tool calls, or altering logic.
**Data Leakage or Unauthorized Data Exposure**: Agents touch sensitive systems, and without strict controls, data can leak through outputs, logs, or cross-agent workflows. Data lineage becomes hard to trace.
**Uncontrolled Autonomous Actions**: Agents may escalate actions, loop endlessly, or modify state unintentionally. One of the least understood failure modes.
**Regulatory or Compliance Violations**: Agents can access restricted data or generate regulated content without traceability, creating audit and policy risks.
**Agent Impersonation or Spoofing**: Weak identity boundaries allow attackers to spoof agents, replay tokens, or assume agent permissions, often outside IAM visibility.

> "Confidence in adopting agentic AI securely starts with governance - test every agent, enforce guardrails, and monitor continuously to ensure trust and resilience"
> ― Venkata Phani Patelkhana, Technical Software Architect, Dell

## The Readiness Gap: Where Enterprises Stand Today

**60% of organizations have not conducted a formal AI or agentic risk assessment in the last 12 months.**

**Only 17% plan to conduct one in the next six months.**

In a technology wave where autonomous behavior creates new attack paths weekly, this is a critical red flag.

**Risk assessments are not optional; they are table stakes for deploying agentic systems responsibly.**

### Early Controls & Checks in Place

Most organizations today rely on controls that detect issues after they occur rather than safeguards that prevent unsafe agent actions in real time. The result is a fragmented and reactive security posture, one that mirrors the early days of cloud security: functional enough to support experimentation, but nowhere near resilient enough to support autonomous operations at scale.

#### Logging & Behavioral Audits Remain the Dominant, and Most Reactive Control

Logging and behavioral audits are the most common controls (44%), offering value for post-incident analysis but little real-time containment. In fast-moving agentic environments, reactive measures are quickly outpaced.

#### Runtime Enforcement Exists, but Coverage Is Thin

Policy-based runtime guardrails are gaining adoption (41%), yet teams report uneven coverage across tools and departments, limiting their effectiveness in multi-system, multi-agent workflows.

#### AI Firewalls Are Emerging, Still Early and Brittle

Specialized AI firewalls or guardrail systems are in place at 39% of organizations, but leaders note that today's solutions remain early, brittle, and poorly integrated, lacking the flexibility needed for complex agent ecosystems.

#### AI Traffic Monitoring Remains Limited

Only 38% of teams monitor AI traffic end-to-end prompts, tool calls, outputs, and decision chains. Most organizations still lack visibility into how instructions propagate across systems.

#### Homegrown Tools Fill the Gaps

Thirty percent of organizations rely on in-house scripts or custom tooling. It's a clear signal the market lacks mature solutions: homegrown controls don't integrate cleanly, rarely scale, and offer limited real-time enforcement. Homegrown tools rarely scale, and rarely deliver the control plane agentic systems require.

#### A Concerning Minority Has No Controls at All

Nearly one in five organizations report no controls: no monitoring, guardrails, or enforcement. These environments effectively grant agents unrestricted autonomy, a posture leaders describe as untenable.

> "The only way to stay ahead of the curve is to embed security into the entire lifecycle of Agentic AI systems. A 'secure-by-design' approach is not just a best practice; it's a prerequisite for responsible AI adoption."
> ― Jackie Mak, Cyber Threat Management, KPMG US

## Monitoring Autonomy: A Structural Blindspot in AI Monitoring

**Only 17% of organizations continuously monitor agent-to-agent interactions**

**One-third do not monitor agentic loops at all.**

Agentic loops, tool-to-tool triggers, recursive actions, multi-agent misinterpretations, are where cascading failures begin. These are not edge cases; they are the core of autonomous behavior.

**Without continuous monitoring, autonomy becomes opacity, and opacity becomes risk.**

## 2026 Will Be the Year of Agentic Security Platforms

Agentic Security Platforms **are emerging as the #1 priority for 2026.**

Organizations have moved past treating agentic AI as a prompt or LLM-output problem. Security teams now describe it as a visibility, access, guardrail and control-plane challenge, one that demands the same architectural rigor as IAM, network segmentation, and zero trust.

As agents gain the ability to invoke tools, access data, and execute multi-step workflows, organizations are prioritizing controls that define who an agent is, what it is allowed to do, and how its actions can be monitored or stopped in real time.

### CISO's Priorities for 2026

- **60%+ of organizations are prioritizing: Policy Guardrails, Runtime Enforcement, and AI Traffic Monitoring.**
- **94% respondents plan to evaluate purpose-built agentic AI security platforms in 2026.**

**Agentic AI is not waiting for security to catch up, and attackers won't either.**

### Security Leaders Want Predictable, Controllable, Auditable Agents

**The biggest ask: make autonomous systems deterministic.**

Across industries, leaders voiced the same requirement: autonomy is valuable only when paired with transparency and control. Confidence comes from seeing what agents do, enforcing constraints in real time, tracing decisions end-to-end, and intervening when judgment or compliance demands it.

**Agentic AI can only securely scale when enterprises can trust, verify, and control its actions.**

Security teams want full action logs, strict execution boundaries, sandboxing, and strong identity enforcement so agents operate under least-privilege. They expect default PII redaction, safe data flows, and the ability to block unsafe actions before impact. For sensitive operations, many require human-in-the-loop approval.

## Recommendations For CISOs Entering 2026

Based on industry findings, Akto recommends six priority areas for security leaders preparing for 2026.

- **Establish an Enterprise-Wide Agent Inventory**: Create a single source of truth for every agent, connector, tool, and permission.
- **Define a Policy Guardrail Framework**: Set clear rules for allowed actions, data boundaries, approvals, and prohibited operations.
- **Enforce Runtime Controls Everywhere**: Apply real-time validation and blocking across all agentic tools and workflows.

**Together, these actions create the control layer required to keep emerging agentic risks from becoming systemic.**

- **Monitor AI Traffic Continuously**: Track prompts, tool calls, decisions, and deviations to detect drift or unsafe behavior.
- **Add a Dedicated Agentic AI Security Layer**: Deploy agentic security capabilities that also connect cleanly into whole Agentic AI ecosystem.
- **Establish Cross-Functional Governance**: Form committees across Product, Engineering, Data, and Security to drive policy and oversee safe scale.

## Methodology

Akto conducted an independent survey on The State of Agentic AI Security 2025 from November 6 to December 3, 2025, via an online questionnaire. The study gathered responses from 100+ verified security and AI leaders including CISOs, Heads of AI Security, product security teams, and technical architects, across North America, EMEA, Asia-Pacific, and Latin America. Organizations ranged from Fortune 500s, more than 50,000 employees to fewer than 1,000, and represented major industries such as technology, financial services, healthcare, manufacturing, telecommunications, consulting, and retail. Respondents answered structured questions covering adoption maturity, governance practices, risk perception, implemented controls, monitoring, and investment priorities. Results reflect practitioner sentiment and directional trends rather than statistical absolutes; they are intended to guide security leaders as they evaluate readiness and build their agentic AI security roadmap.
