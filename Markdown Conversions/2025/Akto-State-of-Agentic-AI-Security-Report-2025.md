## Executive Summary

Agentic AI adoption is accelerating faster than security maturity.

Agentic AI has arrived not as an incremental innovation but as an architectural shift, one that changes how software behaves, how systems interact, and how threats propagate. Organizations are now moving from deterministic workflows to autonomous chains of actions, where agents can interpret, decide, and execute in ways that blur the boundaries between code, identity, and access.

> Agentic AI is anything but predictable. It hasnʼt just expanded the attack surface. It has atomized it.

Securing autonomous systems requires new controls, new playbooks, and a new operational mindset. The organizations that act now will set the standard for safe autonomy in 2026 and beyond.

### The Emerging Risk Gap
This shift introduces a category of risks that current security programs were never designed to manage. Visibility remains limited, tooling is fragmented, and attackers increasingly use AI to automate reconnaissance and exploitation.

As organizations move from experimentation to operational deployment, they now face critical questions. This report outlines how enterprises are adopting agentic AI and where security gaps are most pronounced. Findings show rapid deployment of agents across workflows, while foundational controls such as inventories, governance models, runtime guardrails, and monitoring, have not kept pace.

The 2025 State of Agentic AI Security Report offers a benchmark security leaders can use to evaluate posture, prioritize investment, and build the governance required for safe scale.

## Highlights of 2025 Agentic Survey Findings

**Every team is experimenting. Almost none are protected.**

Despite rapid deployment, only 21% of respondents report full visibility into agent actions, MCPs tool invocations, or data access.

When asked about their organizationʼs adoption of agentic AI, respondents report widespread use ranging from early POCs to enterprise-wide deployments. However, visibility into AI agents, MCP connections, tools, and external systems remains incomplete or fragmented across most organizations.

Our survey shows that Agentic AI is already in production across enterprises, but security governance has not kept pace. Most reported that their organizations though started experiments and pilots with Agentic AI in the beginning of the year but have rapidly expanded into live business workflows, external integrations, and autonomous decision-making, often without a clear inventory, ownership model, or unified security framework.

**Agentic AI adoption is accelerating but foundational security controls are lagging.**

- **79%** lack formal guardrails for how agents and MCP connections operate.
- **21%** maintain a complete, up-to-date inventory of their agents and MCP integrations.

Respondents consistently describe security as a gating factor for deploying agentic AI at scale, driven by a lack of confidence in visibility, enforcement, and control over agent behavior.

> “Visibility is the biggest gap today. You canʼt govern or enforce guardrails if you donʼt know what your agents are doing. Without observability, every control is guesswork.ˮ
> — Suhel Khan, CISO, Chargebee

## AI Agent Adoption Is Accelerating. Security Risk Is Rising

AI adoption is accelerating across the enterprise. Teams are building assistants, automating multi-step processes, and embedding autonomous logic into engineering and business systems.

But while adoption grows, most organizations have not yet established the guardrails or governance needed to manage the risks that come with autonomy. As soon as an agent can call tools, interact with APIs, authenticate with enterprise systems, or trigger downstream actions, it becomes part of the attack surface, regardless of whether it is experimental or production-grade.

Organizations have reached the inflection point where agentic AI is no longer an experiment; itʼs a security surface.

**69% of organizations are already piloting or running early production agent deployments.**

2025 is the year of Agentic AI Adoption. Nearly seven in ten organizations are now operating agentic systems in some capacity. A clear maturity curve is forming. Adoption is accelerating, but visibility and governance have not kept pace, creating material risk even at early stages.

> “The biggest concern for AppSec is the speed. Agentic AI is being adopted far faster than security teams can assess or secure the risks.ˮ
> — Bala Thripura Akasam, Application Security Manager, Tapestry

## The Lack of Inventory Is the #1 Source of Blindspots

**You cannot secure what you cannot see!**

Across industries, security leaders point to a single foundational weakness: most organizations lack a complete, continuously updated inventory of AI agents, MCP connections, and the tools they can invoke.

As autonomous systems proliferate, evolve, and chain actions across environments, lacking an inventory is no longer a visibility problem. It is a security exposure multiplier.

- **Only 21%** of organizations have a fully maintained, up-to-date inventory of AI agents and MCP connections.
- **79%** of survey respondents operate with partial, outdated, or nonexistent visibility.

Smaller organizations (<1,000 employees) show the fastest adoption, with 26% already reporting broad, enterprise-wide integration. Mid-sized companies see strong departmental deployment (36%), while large enterprises move more cautiously: 48% remain in the exploration phase due to scale and compliance demands.

The enterprises that build agent inventories, privilege policies, and runtime controls today will be the resilience leaders of tomorrow.

> "My biggest concerns are visibility and the growing gap between rapid AI development and the security tooling meant to protect it.ˮ
> — Henri Du Plessis, Managing Security Engineer, Toyota Connected North America

## Agentic AI Governance Gap Is Driving a Lack of Confidence in Securing AI

**79% of organizations do not have a security and governance policy for AI agents and MCPs.**

Without policy, agents operate inside undefined trust boundaries. Inventory gaps are compounded by a second critical weakness: the absence of unified policy. Nearly four in five enterprises have no formal governance or security policy defining how AI agents and MCP connections should be onboarded, permissioned, monitored, or managed.

The new risk surface is what an agent can do, not what it can say. Early this year, AI security conversations revolved around prompts, how they were crafted, how they could be manipulated, and how they shaped model outputs. Agentic AI breaks this paradigm entirely. Autonomous agents are no longer producing static responses; they are executing actions across enterprise systems.

**Confidence is low!**
42% of respondents are unconfident and 30% are neutral about securing agent-to-system interactions, underscoring major gaps in visibility and control.

> “Guardrails are essential for Agentic AI Security, they must be thoroughly verified, rigorously tested for their intended purpose, and strictly enforced to ensure compliance.ˮ
> — Krantikishor Bora, Director - Information Security Risk, GoDaddy

## Top Threat Concerns in Agentic AI Security

Across the survey, six threat categories consistently surfaced as the dominant risks shaping the agentic security landscape:

1. **Supply chain risks (LLMs, Agents, MCPs):** Every plug-in or MCP integration becomes a new, unvetted execution surface.
2. **Prompt Injection or Manipulation Attacks:** Attackers can steer agents into unsafe actions, bypassing guardrails.
3. **Data Leakage or unauthorized Exposure:** Agents touch sensitive systems; data can leak through outputs, logs, or cross-agent workflows.
4. **Uncontrolled Autonomous Actions:** Agents may escalate actions, loop endlessly, or modify state unintentionally.
5. **Regulatory or Compliance Violations:** Agents can access restricted data or generate regulated content without traceability.
6. **Agent Impersonation or Spoofing:** Weak identity boundaries allow attackers to spoof agents or assume agent permissions.

> “Confidence in adopting agentic AI securely starts with governance - test every agent, enforce guardrails, and monitor continuously to ensure trust and resilienceˮ
> — Venkata Phani Patelkhana, Technical Software Architect, Dell

## The Readiness Gap: Where Enterprises Stand Today

**60% of organizations have not conducted a formal AI or agentic risk assessment in the last 12 months.** Only 17% plan to conduct one in the next six months.

Most organizations today rely on controls that detect issues after they occur rather than safeguards that prevent unsafe agent actions in real time.

- **Logging & Behavioral Audits (44%):** The most common control, but reactive.
- **Policy-based runtime guardrails (41%):** Gaining adoption, but coverage is uneven.
- **AI Firewalls (39%):** Still early, brittle, and poorly integrated.
- **AI Traffic Monitoring (38%):** Limited visibility into how instructions propagate.
- **Homegrown Tools (30%):** A sign the market lacks mature solutions.

Nearly one in five organizations report no controls at all.

> “The only way to stay ahead of the curve is to embed security into the entire lifecycle of Agentic AI systems. A 'secure-by-design' approach is not just a best practice; it's a prerequisite for responsible AI adoption.ˮ
> — Jackie Mak, Cyber Threat Management, KPMG US

## Monitoring Autonomy: A Structural Blindspot in AI Monitoring

**Only 17% of organizations continuously monitor agent-to-agent interactions.** One-third do not monitor agentic loops at all.

Agentic loops, tool-to-tool triggers, recursive actions, and multi-agent misinterpretations are where cascading failures begin. These are not edge cases; they are the core of autonomous behavior. Without continuous monitoring, autonomy becomes opacity, and opacity becomes risk.

## Why Security Teams Struggle: The Human & Organizational Data

The agentic security gap is not purely technical; it is a challenge of people, process, and expertise.

- **Immature Tooling (32%):** Current security tools donʼt support agentic execution models.
- **Lack of internal expertise (24%):** Teams lack experience with agentic threats.
- **Integration challenges (20%):** Existing SIEM/IAM/DLP don't map to agent behavior.
- **Governance gaps (13%):** No clear ownership or policies.
- **Budget & prioritization (11%):** AI security viewed as critical but underfunded.

## 2026 Will Be the Year of Agentic Security Platforms

**Agentic Security Platforms are emerging as the #1 priority for 2026.**

Organizations have moved past treating agentic AI as a prompt or LLM-output problem. Security teams now describe it as a visibility, access, guardrail, and control-plane challenge.

- **60%+** of organizations are prioritizing: Policy Guardrails, Runtime Enforcement, and AI Traffic Monitoring.
- **94%** of respondents plan to evaluate purpose-built agentic AI security platforms in 2026.

Security teams want full action logs, strict execution boundaries, sandboxing, and strong identity enforcement so agents operate under least-privilege.

## Recommendations For CISOs Entering 2026

Based on industry findings, Akto recommends six priority areas:

1. **Establish an Enterprise-Wide Agent Inventory:** Create a single source of truth for every agent, connector, tool, and permission.
2. **Define a Policy Guardrail Framework:** Set clear rules for allowed actions, data boundaries, and approvals.
3. **Enforce Runtime Controls Everywhere:** Apply real-time validation and blocking across all agentic tools.
4. **Monitor AI Traffic Continuously:** Track prompts, tool calls, and decisions to detect drift.
5. **Add a Dedicated Agentic AI Security Layer:** Deploy capabilities that connect into the whole Agentic AI ecosystem.
6. **Establish Cross-Functional Governance:** Form committees across Product, Engineering, Data, and Security.
