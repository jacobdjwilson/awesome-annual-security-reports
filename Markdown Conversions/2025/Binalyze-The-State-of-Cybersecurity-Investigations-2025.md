## Executive Summary

They are holding our cities to ransom; disrupting our courts and judiciary; and stealing the data of innocent patients. Their impact is felt from critical national infrastructure to food supply to manufacturing. As AI democratizes cyberattacks, the attackers are getting bolder: even boasting to global media about their successes.

Help isn’t coming. Governments can only react to new threats, and move slowly when they aren’t paralyzed by political maneuvering. Even then, legislation to improve security cannot and will not prevent breaches, and many governments are reduced to sending advice letters instead of taking concrete action.

Against this backdrop, the odds of a successful cyberattack are no longer up for debate. The question is not if. It’s when. With an enemy no longer at the gates but scaling the walls, cybersecurity is at a crisis point. As IT environments and attack surfaces rapidly expand with new technologies, security teams have a critical role. Not only defending against attacks, but preventing them from causing crippling damage and turning the tide against attackers.

Cyber Scene Investigation (CSI) plays a vital role. Understanding attacks and attackers is the key to victory. Rapid, forensic investigation is critical to stopping attacks before they do serious damage; informing stakeholders to protect the organization’s reputation; and sharing intelligence so other organizations avoid falling victim.

We found gaping breaches in enterprises’ crisis management frameworks, with most organizations failing to learn the right lessons from cyberattacks. This in turn means increased financial and reputational damage. Delayed investigations cost hundreds of thousands of dollars each time, while a lack of visibility creates inconclusive investigations that again cost millions. And all the while, CSI teams are over-worked and at risk of burnout.

### Key Findings
- **84%** of CISOs say a successful cyberattack is “inevitable”.
- **65%** admit their organizations “haven’t always” learned the right lessons.
- **75%** of CISOs say once a cyberattack has happened there’s “no guarantee” that the exact same attack won’t succeed again.
- **$114,000**: The cost of each hour of delay in responding to a cyberattack.
- **$1.1 million**: The average cost to enterprises due to a lack of clarity in investigations.
- **90%** of CISOs say a lack of skills has hampered investigations.
- **68%** of CISOs have “inaccurately reported” a breach to regulators because of a lack of forensic clarity.

## Part One: The Inevitability of Attack

To cyber attackers, this can feel like a golden age. AI has opened new opportunities, allowing them to iterate and distribute attacks at previously unimagined scales. At the same time, attack surfaces are growing while cybersecurity itself becomes increasingly complex.

79% of organizations’ IT budgets are balanced in favor of prevention; 12% in favor of response (and 9% 50/50). On average, budgets have a 2:1 ratio towards prevention ($3.02 million on prevention, and $1.54m on response).

### Timeline: How soon after an attack should forensics come into play?
- **49%**: Within the first week
- **26%**: Within the first 72 hours
- **19%**: Within the first 24 hours
- **5%**: Within the first 12 hours
- **1%**: Immediately

Mean time to bring forensics into action: **8.6 hours**.

## Part Two: Crisis Management Frameworks in Crisis

When the worst happens, a crisis management framework makes the difference between a focused, organized reaction and blind chaos. However, only 40% of CISOs have “complete confidence” in their organization’s crisis management framework.

### Can CISOs answer these questions with complete confidence?
- **50%**: Does the attacker still have access?
- **55%**: How did they get in?
- **52%**: Was data stolen? And if so, which data?

### Crucial cybersecurity elements of a crisis management framework:
- **44%**: The ability to investigate every potential cyberattack in detail.
- **44%**: The ability to present stakeholders with relevant information in a timely manner.
- **45%**: Having detailed action plans for every form of cyber incident.
- **46%**: Having a crisis management team in place, with defined roles.
- **37%**: Having rigorous review processes in place to prevent future crises.

## Part Three: The Need for Speed and Depth

The average cyberattack investigation produces results 8.5 days after the attack is discovered. 43% of investigations take longer than three days.

### The Map of IT Environment Black Holes
On average, CISOs claim to have visibility over 57% of their organization’s IT environment at any one time.
- **On-premise IT**: 25% black holes.
- **Connected devices**: 28% black holes.
- **Public cloud storage**: 27% black holes.
- **Corporate devices and software**: 28% black holes.
- **Personal devices and software**: 31% black holes.
- **Supply chain partners**: 31% black holes.

Enterprises use on average **11 tools** to investigate cyberattacks, including Threat intelligence platforms (52%), Network monitoring (50%), and EDR systems (33%).

## Part Four: The Skills Gap and Burnout

The average enterprise has 18 skilled cyber investigators on staff. However, 90% of CISOs say a lack of skills has hampered their cyber investigations in the past five years.

- **71%** of enterprises have “delegated” investigations to employees without the necessary skills.
- **71%** of CISOs worry that their skilled investigators are over-worked and at risk of burnout.
- **71%** of CISOs have had investigators resign or take time away because of burnout.

---

## Conclusion: From Crisis to Capability

Cyberattacks are no longer the exception, they’re the expectation. What defines resilience today is how quickly and intelligently you investigate once defenses are breached. CISOs must move beyond reactive defense and embed investigation at the heart of their security strategy. The goal is not more tools, but the right ones: platforms that unify visibility, accelerate response, and allow teams to illuminate every corner of their IT environment.

---

## Methodology

Findings are based on a survey of 200 US CISOs and others with sole responsibility for IT cybersecurity decision-making at enterprises with 500 or more employees. Research was performed in September 2025.
