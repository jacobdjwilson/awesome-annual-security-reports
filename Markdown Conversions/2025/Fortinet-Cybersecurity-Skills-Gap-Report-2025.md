## Methodology
The findings in this report are based on responses obtained from online interviews and an email survey of 1,850 IT and cybersecurity decision makers, conducted by Sapio Research in February 2025.

**Demographics and Data:**
- **Company Size:** 100-499 employees (25%), 500-999 employees (24%), 1,000-2,499 employees (22%), 2,500-4,999 employees (14%), 5,000+ employees (15%).
- **Role Type:** Owner (12%), C-level executive (34%), Vice president (9%), Head (11%), Director (33%).
- **Gender:** 69% male, 31% female.
- **Business Sectors:** Technology (22%), Manufacturing (16%), Financial Services (12%).
- **Regional Distribution:** Asia-Pacific (30%), Europe, Middle East, and Africa (27%), North America (22%), Latin America (22%).

Overall results are accurate to ± 2.3% at a 95% confidence limit. Due to rounding, percentages may not total 100%.

## Executive Summary
A sharpened focus on risk management formed cybersecurity strategy in 2024, driven by a continuously evolving threat landscape, the need to safeguard business continuity, and the growing influence of AI on corporate decision making. 

- **AI:** 49% of respondents worry that AI use by bad actors will increase cyberattacks. 97% are using or plan to use a cybersecurity solution that leverages AI.
- **Boards:** Only 49% of leaders think their board members are fully aware of the potential risks posed by using AI. 52% say directors or executives have faced fines, jail time, or loss of employment following a cyberattack.
- **Breaches:** The top three causes of breaches are: Lack of security awareness (56%), Lack of IT security skills and training (54%), and Lack of cybersecurity products (50%).
- **Certifications:** 89% prefer to hire candidates with certifications. 67% say it validates cybersecurity awareness and knowledge.
- **Talent:** 70% of IT decision makers have structured recruiting initiatives targeting women, and 57% have them for minorities. Only 45% have them for veterans; just 38% have them for veterans’ spouses.

## Introduction: A Growing Need to Manage Risk
Organizations are operating in a new cybersecurity "normal" where breaches are inevitable, AI is a real and present danger, and knowledge gaps are a critical liability. The vast majority (86%) of respondents reported one or more breaches in 2024, with 28% reporting five or more. More than half (52%) of organizations say breaches cost them more than $1 million. Closing the gap requires a coordinated strategy built on three pillars: increased awareness and education, targeted training and certification, and the implementation of advanced security technologies.

## AI Is Seen as a Threat, an Opportunity, and a Challenge
The primary concern surrounding AI is its potential to drive an increase in cyberattacks (49%). Despite this, 97% of respondents are either already using or planning to implement AI-enabled cybersecurity solutions. 

**Challenges to AI Integration:**
- 48% of IT decision makers point to a lack of staff with sufficient AI expertise.
- 47% cite difficulty ensuring data privacy and information security.

## Boards Lack Cyber Knowledge—Even Though It’s a Priority
While 76% of respondents say their board put more focus on cybersecurity in 2024, only 49% feel their board members are fully aware of AI risks. 

**Board-Driven Actions:**
- Mandatory training or certifications for IT and security staff (62%).
- Security awareness training for all staff (55%).
- Purchasing security solutions that use AI (55%).

## Organizations Want Cybersecurity Personnel with Certifications
In 2024, 89% of IT decision makers expressed a preference for certified candidates. However, the percentage of organizations willing to pay for employees to obtain certifications fell to 73% from 89% in 2023.

**Benefits of Certifications:**
- Validates cybersecurity skills and knowledge (67%).
- Sign of ability to keep up with the evolving security landscape (61%).
- Familiarity with security vendor products (56%).

---

## Potential Talent Pools Are Being Overlooked
Organizations are struggling to recruit from specific talent pools, with veterans (43%) and veterans’ spouses (41%) being the most challenging to find. 

**Recruiting Initiatives:**
- Women: 70%
- Minorities: 57%
- Veterans: 45%
- Veterans’ Spouses: 38%

---

## Conclusion
Cybersecurity has become an increasingly strategic concern. As boards take on more responsibility, they require better education on AI risks. Organizations must move beyond traditional hiring requirements, such as four-year degrees, and embrace multiple pathways to expertise, including certifications and on-the-job training, to close the skills gap.
