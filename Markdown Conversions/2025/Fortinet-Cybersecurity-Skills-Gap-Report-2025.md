## Methodology

The findings in this report are based on responses obtained from online interviews and an email survey of 1,850 IT and cybersecurity decision makers, conducted by Sapio Research in February 2025.

Responses were obtained from 29 locations: Argentina, Australia, Brazil, Canada, Colombia, France, Germany, Hong Kong, India, Indonesia, Israel, Italy, Japan, Mainland China, Malaysia, Mexico, Netherlands, New Zealand, Philippines, Singapore, South Africa, South Korea, Spain, Sweden, Taiwan, Thailand, United Arab Emirates, United Kingdom, and the United States of America.

- **Overall results**: Accurate to ± 2.3% at a 95% confidence limit.
- **Rounding**: Due to rounding, percentages may not total 100%.
- **Size of Company**: 100-499 employees (25%), 500-999 employees (24%), 1,000-2,499 employees (22%), 2,500-4,999 employees (14%), 5,000+ employees (15%).
- **Role Type**: Owner (12%), C-level executive (34%), Vice president (9%), Head (11%), Director (33%).
- **Gender**: Male (69%), Female (31%).
- **Top Three Business Sectors**: Technology (22%), Manufacturing (16%), Financial Services (12%).
- **Total respondents**: 1,850 (Asia-Pacific 30%, Europe, Middle East, and Africa 27%, North America 22%, Latin America 22%).

## Executive Summary

A sharpened focus on risk management formed cybersecurity strategy in 2024, driven by a continuously evolving threat landscape, the need to safeguard business continuity, and the growing influence of AI on corporate decision making. Organizations are maintaining a multi-pronged approach to cybersecurity—combining security awareness, certifications, and the deployment of the right technologies.

### AI Is Seen as a Threat, an Opportunity, and a Challenge
- 49% of respondents worry that AI use by bad actors will increase cybersecurity attacks.
- 97% are using or plan to use a cybersecurity solution that leverages AI.
- Lack of staff with sufficient AI expertise (48%) is the biggest challenge foreseen by IT decision makers.

### Boards Lack Cyber Knowledge—Even Though It’s a Priority
- Only 49% of leaders think their board members are fully aware of the potential risks posed by using AI.
- 52% say directors or executives have faced fines, jail time, loss of position, or loss of employment following a cyberattack.
- 76% say their board has put more focus on cybersecurity in 2024, up from 72% in 2023.

### Lack of Cybersecurity Awareness and Training Remains the Top Cause of Breaches
- IT leaders continue to say the top three causes of breaches are: Lack of security awareness (56%), Lack of IT security skills and training (54%), and Lack of cybersecurity products (50%).
- After an attack, most decision makers look to expand their IT/security teams (63%) or mandate certifications (62%).

### Organizations Want Cybersecurity Personnel with Certifications
- 89% prefer to hire candidates with certifications.
- 67% of respondents say it validates cybersecurity awareness and knowledge.
- Only 73% would pay for an employee to obtain a cybersecurity certification—down from 89% in 2023.

### Potential Talent Pools Are Being Overlooked
- 70% of IT decision makers have structured recruiting initiatives targeting women, and 57% have them for minorities. Only 45% have them for veterans; just 38% have them for veterans’ spouses.
- 65% of respondents consider professional certifications when hiring.
- Just over half (52%) consider whether a candidate has a four-year degree.

## Introduction: A Growing Need to Manage Risk

Organizations are operating in a new cybersecurity ”normal” where breaches are inevitable, AI is a real and present danger, and knowledge gaps are a critical liability. 

The vast majority (86%) of this year’s Cybersecurity Skills Gap survey respondents say they had one or more breaches in 2024, with nearly one-third (28%) reporting five or more. More than half (52%) of organizations surveyed say breaches cost them more than $1 million.

Closing the gap requires a coordinated strategy built on three pillars: increased awareness and education, targeted training and certification, and the implementation of advanced security technologies.

---

## AI Is Seen as a Threat, an Opportunity, and a Challenge

While organizations have concerns about AI-related risks, they also see AI as a potentially powerful tool for shoring up defenses. 97% of respondents are either already using or planning to implement AI-enabled cybersecurity solutions.

- **Primary concerns**: Increase in cyberattacks (49%), spread of misinformation (39%), surveillance and privacy violations (38%), and loss of control over superintelligent AI (37%).
- **Biggest challenge to integration**: Lack of staff with sufficient AI expertise (48%).

## Boards Lack Cyber Knowledge—Even Though It’s a Priority

Fewer than half (49%) of all respondents feel their board members are fully aware of AI risks. 

- **Board focus**: 76% of respondents say their board put more focus on cybersecurity in 2024.
- **Business priority**: 96% of respondents say cybersecurity is a business priority, and 95% say it is a financial priority.

---

## Lack of Cybersecurity Awareness and Training Remains the Top Cause of Breaches

IT leaders attribute breaches to:
1. Lack of employee security awareness (56%)
2. Lack of cybersecurity skills and trained IT or security staff (54%)
3. Lack of necessary cybersecurity products (50%)

### Top 20 Attack Types
- Malware attacks (40%)
- Phishing attacks (32%)
- Web attacks (30%)
- Password attacks (27%)
- Trojan horses (24%)

---

## Organizations Want Cybersecurity Personnel with Certifications

In 2024, 89% of IT decision makers expressed a preference for certified candidates. 

- **Why certifications matter**: Validates cybersecurity awareness and knowledge (67%), indicates ability to keep up with the evolving landscape (61%), and indicates familiarity with vendor products (56%).
- **Willingness to pay**: Dropped to 73% from 89% in 2023.

---

## Potential Talent Pools Are Being Overlooked

Organizations are struggling to recruit from specific pools:
- **Women**: 20% report difficulty finding qualified candidates.
- **Minorities**: 29% report difficulty.
- **Veterans**: 43% report difficulty.
- **Veterans’ Spouses**: 41% report difficulty.

---

## Conclusion

Cybersecurity has become an increasingly strategic concern. As boards take on more responsibility, they must prioritize cyber-risk education. Organizations must ensure their teams are "AI aware" and continue to invest in training and certifications to close the skills gap.
