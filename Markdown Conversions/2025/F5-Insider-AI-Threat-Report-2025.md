AI is creating a trust transition

AI isn’t just another workplace tool — it’s becoming an entity employees lean on emotionally

and operationally. For many, AI already feels more reliable, more impartial, and, at times,

more capable than their colleagues or managers.

This shift has created a trust transition where employees willingly transferring trust —

and sometimes authority — from humans to AI systems. This transference of trust to

AI can expedite adoption but it also blinds employees to the risks.

When nearly half of employees say they trust AI more than their co-workers, or that they

would quit if they couldn’t use AI at work, it signals a new power dynamic inside the

enterprise. AI is influencing retention, morale, productivity, and decision-making —

but without the governance structures that normally accompany that level of influence.

The stakes are even higher in regulated industries, where employees use on AI without

fully knowing the regulatory compliance requirements and legal ramifications.

The 2025 insider AI threat reportInternal misuse of AI

The most significant AI threat to enterprises today isn’t external attackers.

It’s employees who use AI without understanding the security implications.

Workers increasingly view AI as a shortcut to getting things done—a way to “outsource”

tedious tasks, move faster, or appear more productive. But with that convenience comes

casual risk-taking in the form of using proprietary information in GenAI prompts, accessing

sensitive data with AI assistance, or ignoring usage policies altogether.

More than half of employees admit they would use AI against policy if it made their job easier.

A third have already used it to access sensitive documents. And nearly everyone believes

leadership or IT would detect an AI-led breach — a level of misplaced confidence that could

accelerate the next major internal incident.

This isn’t malicious behavior. It’s misaligned incentives and misplaced trust—and it’s becoming

one of the biggest open doors in enterprise security.

The 2025 insider AI threat reportThe C-Suite is leading AI, but doesn’t
understand it

Executives are leading AI adoption from the top—but many lack the fluency needed to govern

it. C-level leaders use AI frequently, often without understanding what it’s doing under the

hood or whether they’re using it responsibly.

Their confidence is disproportionately high with most believing IT would detect an AI-driven

breach instantly, and many trusting AI over human colleagues. Yet a notable portion cannot

explain the difference between an AI agent and a virtual employee—despite overseeing

multimillion-dollar systems that may depend on both.

This creates a critical governance gap. When strategic decision-makers misunderstand the

tools they champion, enterprises risk building policy, investment, and operations based on

ill-informed practices.

The 2025 insider AI threat reportEntry-level employee usage risks

Entry-level workers often the least equipped to evaluate the risks of AI usage. Many feel

disconnected from usage policies, confused about what’s allowed, or indifferent altogether.

For them, AI becomes:

•  A productivity crutch

•  A learning substitute

•  A fast way to complete tasks they don’t fully understand

The combination of inexperience, high reliance and trust in AI, and low policy awareness

creates a uniquely vulnerable risk profile. And with younger workers expecting AI access as

a workplace norm, companies face an emerging generation of employees who see secure AI

use as optional.

The 2025 insider AI threat reportFinancial services and banking

One of the most compliance-heavy industry is quietly bending—and sometimes

breaking—its own rules in the age of AI. Employees trust AI enough to use it even when

prohibited, and many find usage policies  too restrictive or outdated to reflect the realities

of modern workflows.

AI is becoming a shadow decision-maker in a space that has little room for error. Submitting

proprietary financial data for analysis, drafting regulated communications with AI, or using AI

tools without approval can have direct compliance and audit implications.

When over half of employees are knowingly bypassing policies, unmonitored AI usage

becomes compliance risk embedded in everyday work.

The 2025 insider AI threat reportHealthcare

In healthcare, the people closest to sensitive patient data are increasingly treating AI as an

ally often ignoring the risks. Many trust AI more than colleagues and use it to accelerate

administrative tasks, documentation, and research—often without confirming whether the tool

is approved or secure.

The issue isn’t intent; it’s awareness. Employees may assume an AI tool is harmless if it

helps them work faster. But when patient data intersects with generative models, even small

missteps can escalate into privacy breaches, regulatory violations, and patient mistrust.

Healthcare’s challenge isn’t resistance to AI — it’s unintentional overconfidence in it.

The 2025 insider AI threat reportSecurity

AI isn’t only a risk for the untrained. Even the people responsible for defending against AI

threats aren’t immune to risky behavior. AI trust among security professionals is high,  and

many admit they would break policy if AI made their job easier.

This reveals a critical organizational tension as employees tasked with modeling risk

mitigation are simultaneously participating in risky AI usage.

Security teams also report unclear policies and uncertainty about IT’s ability to catch

AI-related leaks. When defenders are this uncertain or unwilling to subscribe to AI usage

policies, the organization’s overall security posture becomes unbalanced.

The 2025 insider AI threat reportIT and telecoms

IT professionals are some of the most enthusiastic about using AI at work, and the most

confident that they’re on top of its threats.

No industry embraces AI more enthusiastically than IT and telecom—and no industry is more

confident that it can manage the risks. But this confidence can mask important blind spots.

IT workers use AI frequently, trust it deeply, and believe they can detect misuse or distinguish

AI agents from virtual employees. Yet high usage combined with high trust can normalize

risky behaviors such as using AI to handle sensitive data, pulling proprietary information into

prompts, or automating tasks without verifying security guardrails.

Here, the challenge isn’t adoption — it’s ensuring that confidence doesn’t drift into complacency.

Conclusion

AI is redefining workplace behaviors, expectations, and risks faster than organizations

can adapt. Employees trust AI, rely on it, and increasingly treat it as a core partner in their

daily workflows.

To mitigate internal and external AI risks, enterprises need more than technical controls.

They need:

•  Clear policy grounded in real employee behavior

•  Industry-specific risk modeling

•  Guardrails that balance innovation with security

•  Tools that secure AI wherever it runs — SaaS, cloud, edge, or self-hosted

AI is here. The question is whether enterprises will secure it as quickly as employees adopt it.
