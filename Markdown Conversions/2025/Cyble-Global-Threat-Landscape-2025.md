## Executive Summary

The first half of 2025 saw an unprecedented 54% increase in ransomware attacks compared to the same period in 2024, totaling 3,201 reported incidents. February was the most active month, with 848 attacks, largely driven by the aggressive campaigns of the CL0P ransomware group.

### Key Highlights

- **North America** remained the most targeted region, accounting for 65% of all attacks, with the United States alone experiencing 1,814 incidents (57% of the global total).
- **APAC**: Taiwan, Singapore, and India were the top targets. Groups like CrazyHunter, Qilin, RansomHub, and NightSpire exploited regional geopolitical tensions.
- **ANZ**: Ransomware attacks doubled year-over-year in Australia and New Zealand. Healthcare, SMEs, and professional services faced the brunt.
- **Middle East & Africa**: UAE and South Africa were top targets. Groups like Everest, RansomHub, and Lynx focused on IT, construction, and energy sectors.

### Zero-Day Exploitation Trends

- 63 zero-days were reported in H1 2025, with Microsoft (19) and Apple (6) being the most affected vendors.
- Exploitation of Microsoft Windows, Edge, and Ivanti Connect Secure was rampant, enabling deeper and faster intrusions.

### Supply Chain Threats on the Rise

- A 30% rise in software supply chain attacks was observed, with April alone recording 31 incidents.
- The Construction and Professional Services sectors were the most attacked globally.

### Top Threat Actors

- Three ransomware operators—CL0P, Akira, and Qilin—were behind 34% of all attacks, emphasizing the growing dominance of fewer, more capable threat groups.
- **CL0P** led global activity, accounting for 37% of February’s attacks alone. The group’s hallmark is its exploitation of zero-day vulnerabilities in enterprise file transfer software, such as MOVEit and GoAnywhere MFT, with a focus on North America.
- **Akira** focused on North America and Europe, particularly targeting Germany’s manufacturing and professional services sectors.
- **Qilin**, operating as a Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS), was notably active in the U.S., Europe, and Asia, with a significant April attack on the UK’s NHS, demanding $50 million in ransom.

### Emerging Ransomware Groups

- Groups like Dire Wolf, DATACARRY, Silent Team, and Gunra have surfaced with new leak sites, targeting victims across Asia, Europe, and the Americas.
- These groups are experimenting with data extortion, some without even deploying lockers, signaling a tactical evolution.

### Hacktivism Evolves into Industrial Threat

- Hacktivist campaigns in 2025 showed a dramatic increase in sophistication and scale.
- Russia-linked groups Z-Pentest, Sector 16, and Dark Engine launched coordinated attacks on industrial control systems (ICS) in energy, transportation, and utilities sectors.
- Cyber campaigns tied to geopolitical conflicts (e.g., Iran-Israel, Ukraine-Russia) now regularly feature ransomware and ICS exploits.

## CL0P, Akira, Qilin Ransomware Operators Top Contributors

### Cl0P
The Cl0P ransomware group, active since at least 2019, is a financially motivated threat actor known for high-impact, large-scale cyber extortion campaigns. Cl0P’s expertise lies in exploiting zero-day vulnerabilities in widely used enterprise software. Their hallmark is the use of double extortion: not only encrypting victims’ data but also leaking or threatening to leak stolen information on their dedicated leak site, “CL0P^_- LEAKS.”

Most notably, the group exploited CVE-2023-34362—a critical SQL injection flaw in Progress Software’s MOVEit Transfer. Cl0P previously targeted similar platforms, exploiting Accellion FTA and GoAnywhere MFT.

### Akira
Akira has majorly targeted the North American region. However, the ransomware group also focused on Europe where the core sectors of the economy—professional services, construction, and manufacturing—were the prime targets. In Europe, Germany was the most attacked country.

### Qilin
Qilin ransomware operators complete the top three most active ransomware actors list. Qilin operates in the ransomware-as-a-service provider bracket and enables affiliates to launch highly customizable attacks across various sectors, including healthcare, manufacturing, and government services. The gang grabbed most attention for its attack on the Synnovis systems that crippled emergency services at UK’s NHS centers.

## Region Wise Ransomware Threat Landscape

### United States
The U.S. once again led all countries in ransomware with 1,814 attacks, or 57% of the global total. Construction and Professional Services were the most targeted sectors.

### APAC
In Asia and Pacific, Taiwan was the most targeted country with 37 recorded incidents followed by Singapore (32), India (28), and Japan (24). A total of 219 attacks were recorded here.

### Europe and United Kingdom
A total of 663 ransomware attacks were recorded in this region in H1 2025. Germany and the U.K. were the most targeted countries. Professional Services, Construction, and IT & ITES sectors were the most targeted.

### Australia and New Zealand (ANZ)
The ransomware threats “Down Under” doubled in the first six months of the year. Australia saw 57 ransomware attacks whereas New Zealand registered seven.

### Middle East and Africa
Middle East saw the UAE take the top slot while South Africa remained the focus of ransomware actors in Africa. Healthcare, professional services, and SMEs were the most targeted sectors.

## Software Supply Chain Attacks: Targeted Industries and Countries

An analysis of Cyble data reveals that software supply chain attacks have increased from an average of just under 13 a month (Feb-Sept 2024) to just over 17 a month (Oct 2024-June 2025), an increase of more than 30%.

- **Targeted Industries**: 62% of attacks targeted IT, technology, and telecommunications companies.
- **Targeted Countries**: The U.S. was a target in 42 of the 105 incidents. European countries were targeted in 32 incidents, and APAC countries in 36 incidents.

## Notable Supply Chain Incidents

1. **Swiss Banking Tech**: Everest Ransomware claimed an attack on a Swiss banking technology solutions company.
2. **IT Services Subsidiary**: Akira ransomware impacted an IT services subsidiary of a large international conglomerate.
3. **Satellite Data**: A threat actor on DarkForums offered for sale a 92 GB dataset pertaining to a high-throughput telecommunications satellite for Indonesia.
4. **Display Tech**: Hellcat ransomware compromised a China-based company specializing in display technologies.
5. **Biometric Recognition**: DragonForce extortion group claimed an attack on a U.S. company specializing in biometric recognition.
6. **IAM Solutions**: VanHelsing ransomware compromised a U.S.-based company specializing in enterprise security and IAM solutions.
7. **Fintech SaaS**: Unauthorized access offered for sale to the cloud infrastructure of an India-based fintech company.
8. **Singapore Tech**: Crypto24 claimed a cyberattack on a Singapore-based technology company, alleging the theft of 3TB of data.
9. **IT/Telecom**: Killsec hacking group compromised an Australia-based company that offers IT and telecom solutions.
10. **Public Sector IT**: Medusa ransomware compromised a U.S.-based technology solutions provider specializing in IT infrastructure for public sector clients.

## Hacktivism Gets More Sophisticated

Hacktivism in 1H 2025 tended to follow conflicts, with Ukraine-Russia, Israel-Iran, India-Pakistan, Thailand-Cambodia, and Morocco-Algeria among the flashpoints.

### Critical Infrastructure Hacktivism
- **Z-Pentest**: Russia-linked group with 38 ICS attacks in Q2.
- **Dark Engine**: Accounted for 26 ICS-targeted incidents in Q2.
- **Sector 16**: Linked to 14 attacks in the most recent quarter.

### Hacktivists Turn to Ransomware, Extortion
- **Handala**: Active in the Iran-Israel conflict, with at least 15 claims of ransomware/extortion incidents against Israeli targets.
- **BO Team**: Conducted a ransomware attack on a Russian industrial manufacturer, encrypting over 1,000 hosts and 300TB of data.
- **C.A.S.**: Carried out a coordinated cyber operation against a Russian technology firm, exfiltrating 3 terabytes of data.

## Conclusion

The first half of 2025 was marked by a significant 54% increase in ransomware attacks. Beyond ransomware, the landscape saw a 30% rise in software supply chain attacks and the evolution of hacktivism into a sophisticated threat against critical infrastructure and industrial control systems. Organizations must adopt a proactive approach, strengthening threat intelligence capabilities, ensuring robust patching hygiene, and implementing cross-sector cyber resilience strategies.
