## Security Habits and Knowledge Gaps

### Terms and concepts: the same gaps remain
Nearly a third of survey participants were unable to correctly define terms like “phishing” and “malware.” For more advanced attacks like “ransomware,” “smishing” (SMS phishing) and “vishing” (voice phishing), around two-thirds answered incorrectly.

### Security habits: blurred lines
Over three-quarters of people use their work devices for personal activities, with almost the same proportion using personal devices for work. 
- 78% use work devices for personal activities.
- 72% use personal devices for work.
- 48% let family and friends use their work devices.

### Security habits: password hygiene
The most common method is using a single unique password, entered manually per account. However, 28% of users reuse passwords for multiple work-related accounts.

### Security habits: Wi-Fi woes
While 69% of people password-protect their home network, less than a third change default wireless and administration passwords. 7% of respondents took no home Wi-Fi security measures at all.

### Security habits: risky business
More than a third of respondents (34%) took at least one risky action during the year, with clicking on malicious links being the most frequent.

## Recognize Risk

### TOADs and multi-factor phishing
Telephone-oriented attack delivery (TOAD) involves messages containing fake invoices or alerts with a customer service number. At peak, Proofpoint observed over 600,000 TOAD messages per day. Additionally, phishing-as-a-service providers are increasingly including MFA bypass in their kits.

### Brand abuse
In 2022, there were nearly 1,600 campaigns involving brand abuse. Microsoft was the most abused brand, appearing in over 30 million messages.

### BEC goes global
Business Email Compromise (BEC) remains a top concern for board members. While English is the primary language for these attacks, there has been significant growth in non-English speaking regions, including Spain, France, and Germany.

### Ransomware remains
- 76% of organizations experienced an attempted ransomware attack.
- 64% experienced a successful infection.
- 90% of organizations that were infected had cyber insurance.
- 64% of infected organizations paid at least one ransom.

### Insider threat
Nearly 65% of security professionals reported that their organization had experienced data loss because of an insider. Of those who changed jobs in the past two years, 44% admitted to taking data with them.

### Counting the cost
30% of organizations that endured a successful attack experienced a direct monetary loss, an increase of 76% year over year.

## Benchmarks: Failure Rates, Reporting and Resilience

### Template failure rates
Attachments still have a higher failure rate than links. 
- Link-based failure rate: 66%
- Data entry failure rate: 27%
- Attachment failure rate: 8%

### Failure rates by industry
Legal has the lowest overall failure rate, while electronics has the highest.

### Failure rates by department
Development, R&D, supply chain, legal, and managerial groups shared the highest failure rates.

### Reporting and resilience
The average reporting rate for simulated phishing was 17%, with an average failure rate of 10%, resulting in a "resilience factor" of 1.7.

## Security Awareness: Insights and Opportunities

### Building a security culture
While 98% of organizations have a training program, only 56% train everyone in the organization, and only 35% run phishing simulations. There is a significant discrepancy between how security teams perceive the priority of security and how employees perceive it.

---

## Conclusions

Proofpoint suggests three approaches to improve security:
1. **Reduce complexity**: Ask the right questions regarding who is being attacked and where the gaps are.
2. **Pair threat intelligence with education**: Tailor training to the specific threats targeting your users.
3. **Build a security culture**: Go beyond training by measuring behavioral metrics and implementing fair remediation.
