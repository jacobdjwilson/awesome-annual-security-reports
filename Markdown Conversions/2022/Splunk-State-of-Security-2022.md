## The Heat Is (Still) On

The state of security in 2022 is highly adrenalized — more so than usual. Two years into the deadly and disruptive global pandemic, not only are we continuing to see more attacks, we’re also seeing more actual breaches.

New global research conducted by Splunk and the Enterprise Strategy Group of more than 1,200 security leaders found that 49% of organizations say they’d suffered a data breach over the past two years, up from 39% in our survey a year earlier. And while certain attack vectors dominated headlines in the last year or two, criminals are still finding success with traditional playbooks:

- 51% report business email compromise, up from 42% a year ago.
- 39% of organizations report insider attacks, up from 27% a year ago.
- 79% say they’ve encountered ransomware attacks, and 35% admit one or more of those attacks led them to lose access to data and systems.

Additionally, 40% of respondents report a regulatory violation (up from 28% a year ago). More and more sophisticated attacks, acute talent shortages and pandemic-specific challenges have SOCs reeling.

### Methodology
Researchers surveyed 1,227 security and IT leaders who spend more than half their time on security issues.

**11 Countries:** Australia, Canada, France, Germany, India, Japan, Netherlands, New Zealand, Singapore, United Kingdom, United States.

**15 Industries:** Aerospace and defense, consumer packaged goods, education, financial services (banking, securities, insurance), government (federal/national, state and local), healthcare, technology, life sciences, manufacturing, media, energy, retail/wholesale, telecom, transportation/logistics, utilities.

### Security teams are falling behind
Fully 64% of respondents say keeping up with security requirements has gotten harder in recent years, up from 49% a year ago. Top reasons include:
- The dangerous threat landscape (38%).
- An overwhelmingly complex security stack (30%).
- Skilled labor recruitment and retention issues (29%).

### Remote workers remain a challenge
Organizations are still supporting, on average, double the number of remote workers compared to pre-pandemic norms. While today’s average, 46% of workforces, is 10 points lower than in 2021, there’s no return to pre-pandemic levels in sight. Organizations expect that 12 months out, 41% of their workers will remain remote. As a result, 90% see a need to adjust security controls and policies.

### Supply chain worries are front and center
Ninety percent of organizations reported that they have increased their focus on third-party risk assessment as a result of high-profile attacks like SolarWinds and Kaseya. In fact, 61% of CISOs are now regularly briefing their boards and/or line of business executives about activity in this space.

### Ransomware: Playbooks and payoffs
The top response to ransomware: Pay up. Among respondents who fell victim to a successful ransomware attack, only 33% avoided the ransom by restoring from backup. About 66% reported that the criminals were paid, either by the organization (39%) or their insurance company (27%). The average largest ransom paid was about US$347,000.

### Security teams reel from talent crisis
Fully 87% of respondents report skills or staffing challenges; 53% say they can’t hire enough staff and 58% cite an inability to find talent with the right skills.

## The State of Cloud: Business-Critical Baseline

Across the board, the still-growing role of cloud computing has become essential to organizations. About 73% use multiple public clouds today, and 34% use three or more public cloud service providers.

### Cloud hampers security visibility
More than three-quarters of respondents (79%) say their CISO is under pressure to demonstrate increased cloud fluency. Two cloud-native security challenges continue to stand out:
- Maintaining consistency across data centers and cloud (45%).
- Use of multiple security controls leads to cost and complexity (37%).

## Responding to Rising Challenges

### Talent solutions
Asked to pick strategies that will overcome the talent crisis, respondents picked a combination of tools and training:
- 58% selected “increase funding for training.”
- 52% picked “increase use of cybersecurity tools with AI/ML.”

### Analytics and automation are increasingly essential
Eighty-two percent of respondents say their CISO is under pressure to increase their data analysis capabilities. Fully 85% say that security analytics plays a bigger role in their overall cybersecurity strategy and decision-making today than it did two years ago.

### The shift toward DevSecOps
Fully 80% of respondents say their CISOs are under pressure to develop their DevSecOps skill sets. 75% of organizations say they use DevSecOps today.

## The Path Forward

Nearly every organization we surveyed — 93% — expect to increase spending on security over the next 12-24 months. Increasing training for cybersecurity and IT operations staff is the leading area of focus.

---

## Key Recommendations

1. **Seek talent, teach skills**: Consider alternative candidate pools, including people with journalism, HR, and creative backgrounds who possess innate curiosity.
2. **Know your cloud**: Dedicate time and training to understand the complex interplay of public, private, and SaaS solutions.
3. **Build an SBOM**: Every software organization should maintain a software bill of materials that lists all software components running in production.
4. **Use automation to enhance human analysts**: Automate with the intention of liberating human analysts to do their job better, rather than just giving them more tools to monitor.
5. **Take DevSecOps forward**: Apply DevSecOps specifically to defending against ransomware and advanced persistent threats.
6. **Consolidate sprawling tool sets**: Focus on a platform approach that gathers multiple inputs on one dashboard to empower analysts.

## Appendix

### Industry highlights
- **Communications and media**: Suffer from more frequent downtime than other industries.
- **Financial services**: Tend to have larger SOCs and are ahead in DevSecOps adoption.
- **Healthcare and life sciences**: More cloud-reliant and report higher rates of data breaches.
- **Manufacturing**: Tend to have smaller SOCs and take a more reactive approach to supply chain attacks.
- **Public sector**: Analytics adoption is slower, and there is more cloud reticence.
- **Retail**: Embrace cloud-first policies and are more likely to pay ransomware attackers.
- **Technology**: Lead on multicloud adoption and have seen more malicious activity.

### Country highlights
- **Australia and New Zealand**: Report fewer attacks but longer downtime.
- **Canada**: Increasing investment at a slower rate than global peers.
- **France**: More resilient to downtime and slower multicloud adoption.
- **Germany**: High cloud-first policy adoption but also high on-prem-first policies.
- **India**: Aggressively integrating non-security analytics with security analytics.
- **Japan**: Less reliant on cloud and more conservative in DevSecOps adoption.
- **Singapore**: Tentative approach to cloud and significant skills shortages.
- **United Kingdom**: Less likely to report project failures due to labor shortages.
- **United States**: High multicloud usage and more sophisticated security controls.
