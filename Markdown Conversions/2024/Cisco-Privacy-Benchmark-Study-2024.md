## Introduction
Over the past few years, privacy has evolved from relative obscurity to being a business imperative, a regulatory mandate, and a customer requirement. During this time, Cisco’s Privacy Research has explored the value of privacy investment and its economic benefits for organizations who have learned to better inventory, manage, and protect data. Customers who were once resigned to accept the lack of transparency from organizations now demand that their data be used only in transparent, legal, and appropriate ways, including in Artificial Intelligence (AI) applications. As such, privacy has become a critical element and enabler of customer trust.

## Methodology
This report, Cisco’s seventh annual review of privacy perspectives for organizations, explores current privacy trends, challenges, and opportunities. It draws upon data gathered in summer 2023 from an anonymous survey in which the respondents did not know who was conducting the study and respondents were similarly unknown to the researchers. The survey respondents included 2,600 security and privacy professionals in 12 countries (5 Europe, 4 Asia, and 3 Americas)[^1]. They were asked about their organizations’ privacy practices and spending, reactions to privacy legislation, AI, and data localization requirements. The findings from this research demonstrate the continuing importance of privacy to businesses and how they serve their customers.

[^1]: Australia, Brazil, China, France, Germany, India, Italy, Japan, Mexico, Spain, United Kingdom, and United States.

## Key findings
1. **Privacy has become a critical element and enabler of customer trust**, with 94% of organizations saying their customers would not buy from them if they did not protect data properly.
2. **Organizations strongly support privacy laws around the world**, with 80% indicating legislation has had a positive impact on them.
3. **The economics of privacy remain attractive**, with 95% saying benefits exceed costs and the average organization realizing a 1.6x return on their privacy investment.
4. **There has been relatively slow progress on building customer confidence with respect to AI**; 91% of organizations still recognize they need to do more to reassure their customers.
5. **Organizations are already getting significant value from generative AI applications**, but they’re also concerned about the risks to their intellectual property or that the data entered could be shared with competitors or the public.
6. **Organizations believe that global providers**, operating at scale, can better protect their data compared to local providers.

## Results

### 1. Privacy as a critical enabler of customer trust
Customers increasingly want to buy from organizations they can trust with their data. Ninety-four percent of organizations in our survey said their customers would not buy from them if they did not adequately protect customer data. What’s more, customers are looking for hard evidence that organizations can be trusted when it comes to privacy. Ninety-eight percent of respondents said that external privacy certifications, such as ISO 27701, Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC) Cross-Border Privacy Rules, and European Union (EU) Binding Corporate Rules, are important in their buying process. Organizations know these issues are important to customer trust, and nearly all organizations (97%) feel they have a responsibility to use data ethically.

![Figure 1. Privacy’s importance to customer trust: Bar chart showing growth in trust metrics from 2021 to 2023.]

Organizations are also increasingly recognizing the connection between privacy investment and business benefits, especially in building brand trust and loyalty. This year, over 70% indicated they were getting “significant” or “very significant” benefits from each of these six areas: reducing sales delays, mitigating losses from data breaches, enabling innovation, achieving operational efficiency, building customer trust, and making their company generally more attractive.

![Figure 2. Privacy investment’s impact on loyalty and trust: Bar charts showing percentage of respondents getting significant benefits.]

For privacy-mature organizations, the percentage getting benefits from “Loyalty and Trust” is even higher. Ninety-two percent of the most privacy-mature organizations indicated they are getting significant benefits in this area, compared with 71% of those that are the least mature.

![Figure 3. Privacy maturity’s correlation with loyalty and trust: Line graph showing positive correlation between maturity and benefits.]

> “Privacy has become inextricably tied to customer trust and loyalty.”
> — Harvey Jang, Vice President, Deputy General Counsel and Chief Privacy Officer, Cisco

Translating privacy objectives to organizational activities can be difficult. Among this year’s respondents, nearly all (98%) are reporting one or more privacy metrics to the Board, and over half are reporting three or more.

![Figure 4. Number and type of privacy metrics reported to the Board: Bar charts showing distribution of metrics and types of metrics reported.]

### 2. Strong support for privacy laws globally
Eighty percent of all corporate respondents said privacy laws have had a positive impact on their organizations, with 14% neutral, and only 6% indicating the laws have had a negative impact. This sentiment was quite consistent across regions, with 78% in Asia, 80% in Europe, and 83% in the Americas indicating the privacy laws have been positive.

![Figure 5. Impact of privacy laws on organizations: Map/chart breakdown by country.]

Most (91%) believe that their data would be inherently safer if it is only stored within their country or region. But almost the same number (86%) also said that a global provider, operating at scale, can better protect their data compared to a local provider.

![Figure 6. Data localization: Bar charts showing preference for local storage vs. global provider security.]
![Figure 7. Percentage agreeing that a global provider can protect data better than a local provider, by country: Bar chart breakdown.]

### 3. Privacy economics remain attractive
Ninety-six percent of respondents agreed that privacy is a business imperative, not just a compliance burden, and 95% indicated that privacy’s benefits are greater than its costs.

![Figure 8. Business benefits of privacy investment: Percentage agreement.]

Average spending overall was similar to 2021 and 2022 at $2.7 million. Average spending rose at the largest organizations (10,000+ employees) to $3.9 million.

![Figure 9. Privacy spending, 2019-2023: Bar chart showing spending trends by company size.]
![Figure 10. Estimated financial benefits, 2019-2023: Bar chart showing benefit trends by company size.]

The average organization reports getting privacy benefits of 1.6 times their investment. In addition, 30% of organizations estimate returns at least two times with some (12%) realizing returns upwards of three times their investment.

![Figure 11. Estimated ROI ranges for respondents, 2023: Pie chart showing ROI distribution.]

### 4. Slow progress on transparency and AI readiness
When we asked if organizations needed to do more to reassure customers about their data use with AI, 91% agreed, indicating not much progress has been made during the past year.

![Figure 12. Organizations who say they need to do more to reassure customers about their data use with AI: Pie chart.]

Organizations identified their top priorities as complying with privacy laws (25%) and avoiding data breaches (23%).

![Figure 13. What organizations can do to build and maintain trust when it comes to customer data: Comparison of consumer vs. organizational views.]
![Figure 14. Steps taken by organizations regarding their use of AI: Bar chart of mitigation strategies.]

> “The risks of AI are real, but they are manageable when thoughtful governance practices are in place as enablers, not obstacles, to responsible innovation.”
> — Dev Stahlkopf, Executive Vice President and Chief Legal Officer, Cisco

The Cisco AI Readiness Index showed that only 14% of organizations worldwide believed they were fully ready to integrate AI into their businesses.

![Figure 15. Cisco AI Readiness Index: Bar chart showing readiness across six domains.]

### 5. Data concerns with GenAI
Most organizations have become very familiar (55%) or somewhat familiar (41%) with GenAI. Most are already deploying it, as 79% of respondents said they are getting significant or very significant value from GenAI today.

![Figure 16. Familiarity and value from GenAI: Pie charts.]

Ninety-two percent of organizations said they see it as a fundamentally different technology with novel challenges. Top concerns include legal/IP rights (69%) and data leakage (68%).

![Figure 17. Concerns with GenAI: Bar chart of risk factors.]
![Figure 18. Types of information entered into GenAI applications: Bar chart of data types.]

Sixty-three percent have established limitations on what data can be entered, 61% have limits on which GenAI tools can be used, and 27% have banned GenAI applications altogether.

![Figure 19. GenAI controls: Bar chart of implemented restrictions.]

## Conclusion and recommendations for organizations
1. Provide greater transparency in how your organization applies, manages, and uses personal data.
2. Establish protections, such as AI ethics management programs, involving humans in the process, and working to remove any biases.
3. Apply appropriate control mechanisms and educate employees regarding the risks associated with GenAI applications.
4. Consider the costs and consequences of data localization.
5. Continue to invest in privacy to realize the significant business and economic benefits.
