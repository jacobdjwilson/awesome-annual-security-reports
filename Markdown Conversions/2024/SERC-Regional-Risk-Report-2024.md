# 1.0 Executive Summary

## Introduction

The electric grid is essential to modern life, supporting economic activity, societal functions, and technological advancements. The reliability and security of the electric grid is crucial for national security, public health and safety, and community well-being. As society’s dependence on electricity grows due to technological advancements in communication, transportation, and overall moving toward more electrification, ensuring the grid’s resilience is more critical than ever. This overall growing demand for electricity increases society’s dependence on new generation technologies in the pursuit of reduced emissions.

![Figure 1-A: SERC Region]

SERC's mission is to assure the effective and efficient reduction of risks to the reliability and security of the bulk power system (BPS). SERC is one of only six companies across North America that is responsible for this important work pursuant to Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) approved delegation agreements with the North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC). SERC is responsible for the reliability and security of the electric grid across the southeastern and central regions of the United States. This area covers approximately 630,000 square miles, serves a population of more than 97 million, and includes all or portions of Florida, Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas, Oklahoma, Arkansas, Missouri, Iowa, Illinois, Kentucky, Tennessee, Virginia, North Carolina, and South Carolina.

The SERC Regional Risk Report is SERC’s independent assessment of the risks to the BES across SERC’s footprint. The SERC Risk Report builds upon and serves as a regional supplement to the NERC 2023 ERO Reliability Risk Priorities Report, which provides a continent-wide view.[^1] In developing the report, SERC staff also collaborated with SERC’s technical committees, including the Reliability Risk Working Group (RRWG), to gather invaluable industry expertise, insights, and feedback.

This report highlights the essential themes and emerging risk trends in the SERC region.

[^1]: 2023 ERO Reliability Risk Priorities Report (nerc.com)

## Identified Risk Themes and Takeaways

1. **Supply Chain Constraints**: The SERC footprint faces risks due to supply chain dependencies on suppliers; and the associated impact that geopolitical tensions, natural disasters, and cybersecurity threats pose to those suppliers, their products, and the corresponding supply chain. Diversifying suppliers, enhancing cybersecurity, and improving inventory management are crucial mitigating actions necessary to manage grid reliability and security. Additionally, as supply chains grow more complex, collaboration with key stakeholders and regulators will be crucial for managing these risks proactively.

2. **Extreme Weather Impacts**: The increasing occurrence of severe and extreme weather events such as hurricanes, extreme cold weather, and heatwaves have the potential to disrupt the BPS and the supply of energy across SERC footprint. The growing frequency of these events, driven by climate change, underscores the need for resilience in operational planning and infrastructure investments. Preparedness measures, including extreme weather readiness and collaborative restoration efforts, are essential for maintaining grid stability and operational readiness.

3. **Resource Uncertainty**: The shift toward renewable energy introduces challenges in managing resource adequacy and system stability. A diverse energy portfolio, advanced modeling techniques, implementing different resource adequacy metrics to accurately gauge risks, and flexible planning are necessary to successfully integrate these new generation resources. The transition from coal to natural gas, renewables, and battery energy storage increases operational risks, requiring updated tools and strategies to ensure reliability in an evolving energy landscape.

4. **Shortage of Required Skillsets**: Stakeholders across SERC’s footprint are facing a shortage of skilled professionals in operations, planning, cybersecurity, and advanced technologies. The shift in workforce dynamics, alongside the growing need for expertise in emerging technologies, cybersecurity, and renewable integration, creates a potential talent gap. Expanded training programs, educational partnerships, and enhanced recruitment are vital to address this gap.

5. **Exploitation of Vulnerabilities**: Increased digitalization has heightened cyber security risks across SERC’s footprint. Implementing robust protocols, network segmentation, encryption, leveraging advanced technologies for better security, and continuous risk management practices are key to protecting critical infrastructure from potential cyber-attacks. As the reliance on digital systems grows, entities must adopt advanced cybersecurity solutions and collaborative risk management practices to safeguard grid security from increasingly sophisticated threats.

6. **Rapid Nontraditional Load Growth**: The ongoing rise in demand from sources like data centers, crypto mining, artificial intelligence, electric vehicles, and semiconductor manufacturing puts stress on the transmission and distribution systems across SERC’s footprint. This rapid load growth calls for infrastructure modernization, strategic investments, and the integration of advanced technologies like artificial intelligence (AI) and grid monitoring tools to maintain grid reliability. Upgrading infrastructure and balancing generation capacity with demand are crucial to maintaining grid reliability.

7. **Lack of Fuel Diversity and Availability**: Heavy reliance on natural gas poses risks related to fuel supply disruptions and volatility in availability. Mitigation includes monitoring fuel availability, enhancing operational tools, enhancing infrastructure, and diversifying the fuel mix to ensure reliable power generation. The transition to alternative fuels and increased integration of storage technologies will be essential for long-term fuel security and grid reliability.

8. **Legacy Architecture Compatibility**: Legacy systems often struggle with compatibility issues when integrated with modern technologies, thus affecting performance, reliability, and security. The rapid evolution of emerging technologies, including grid monitoring tools, AI, and advanced cybersecurity solutions, demands ongoing investment in infrastructure upgrades to ensure these systems can handle future challenges. Collaboration with industry experts and strategic prioritization of upgrades can help address these challenges.

9. **Extreme Physical Events: Sabotage and Attacks**: The SERC footprint faces the risk of physical attacks from malicious actors, including sabotage and extreme vandalism, which can lead to major disruptions and equipment losses. Comprehensive security measures, including access controls, barriers, surveillance, and incident response plans are essential. Collaborative efforts with law enforcement and continuous vulnerability assessments will enhance regional security.

10. **Integrating Variable Energy Resources**: The growth of renewable energy sources introduces challenges in grid management across SERC’s footprint. As renewable integration increases, advancements in inverter technologies and updated modeling techniques are necessary to manage the variability of resources like wind and solar. Adhering to industry guidelines and investing in infrastructure are necessary to manage the variability of renewable energy resources and maintain grid reliability.

## Risk Profiles

The SERC Regional Risk Report builds upon and serves as regional supplement to the 2023 ERO Reliability Risk Priorities Report.[^2] SERC’s top regional risks fall within the following risk profiles identified in the 2023 ERO Reliability Risk Priorities report:

- **Energy Policy Changes**: Governmental decisions can profoundly influence BPS reliability. Key regional risks within this category include supply chain constraints, resource uncertainty, and challenges related to integrating variable energy resources.

- **Grid Transformation**: The shift toward non-synchronous and digital technologies introduces a new set of nontraditional risks. These include skill shortages, rapid nontraditional load growth, challenges with fuel diversity, legacy infrastructure compatibility, and the potential for sabotage.

- **Resilience to Extreme Events**: This category emphasizes the BPS's ability to recover from severe conditions such as hurricanes and heatwaves. Extreme weather events are identified as a significant concern affecting system reliability.

- **Security Risks**: As the BPS infrastructure rapidly expands, its vulnerability to cyber-physical threats increases. This includes risks from supply chain issues, skill shortages, and attacks on legacy systems.

- **Critical Infrastructure Interdependency**: The increasing interconnectedness between the energy sector and other essential sectors—such as transportation and communications—introduces cascading risks. Notable risks include lack of fuel diversity and sabotage.

## Risk Management and Status

To further address these challenges, the 2024–2026 SERC Regional Risk Report categorizes risks into two groups: “Manage” and “Monitor.”

The **Manage** status group includes emerging risks where mitigation plans need to be developed and implemented—through SERC, NERC, or other Industry engagements—or additional mitigation plans need to be considered.

The **Monitor** status group includes risks where mitigation plans and guidance are already underway but additional time is required to fully implement these actions and assess their effectiveness in reducing risk.

In this report, the risks related to Supply Chain Constraints and Shortage of Required Skillsets have shifted from "manage" status to "monitor" status. This change reflects the proactive efforts of SERC stakeholders in developing and implementing mitigation strategies to deal with the supply chain for key parts and equipment (see section 4.1, Supply Chain Constraints). In addition, stakeholders have formed partnerships and strategies to improve workforce training and recruitment (see section 4.4, Shortage of Required Skillsets). As a result, these risks have been reclassified to "monitor" status.

# 2.0 Background

The mission of SERC Reliability Corporation (SERC) is to assure effective and efficient reduction of risks to the reliability and security of the Bulk Power System (BPS). To achieve this mission, SERC maintains a diverse team of experts across numerous disciplines to address the complex, evolving, and dynamic challenges facing the grid. Our team also partners with the best and brightest individuals from the power industry and the federal and state governments to understand and address the challenges facing the grid. These key partnerships make our work more informed, pragmatic, responsive, and impactful.

The 2024–2026 SERC Regional Risk Report is an independent assessment of the risks affecting the SERC Region, developed by SERC. The SERC Regional Risk Report serves as a supplement to the NERC ERO Reliability Risk Priorities Report by providing a more granular focus on the SERC Region to complement the ERO Reliability Risk Priorities Report’s continent-wide view.

## ABOUT SERC
SERC is one of six Regional Entities across North America responsible for this important work under the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) approved delegation agreements with the North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC). SERC is specifically responsible for the reliability and security of the electric grid across the southeastern and central regions of the United States. The SERC Region covers approximately 630,000 square miles and serves a population of more than 97 million. It includes all or portions of 16 states: Florida, Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas, Oklahoma, Arkansas, Missouri, Iowa, Illinois, Kentucky, Tennessee, Virginia, North Carolina, and South Carolina.

Geographically, the SERC Region is divided into seven subregions. These subregions are SERC Central, SERC East, SERC FL-Peninsula, SERC MISO-Central, SERC MISO-South, SERC PJM, and SERC Southeast. SERC’s seven subregions encompass these six Reliability Coordinator (RC) Areas:

- **Florida Reliability Coordinating Council, Inc (FRCC)** is the Reliability Coordinator for the Florida Peninsula subregion (FL-Peninsula).
- **Midcontinent Independent System Operator (MISO)** is the Reliability Coordinator for two of the SERC sub regions, MISO-Central, and MISO-South.
- **Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA)** is the Reliability Coordinator for the Central subregion.
- **Southern Company Services (SCS)** is the Reliability Coordinator for the Southeast subregion (SeRC).
- **VACAR South (VACS)** is the Reliability Coordinator for the East subregion.
- **PJM Interconnection, LLC (PJM)** is the Reliability Coordinator for the PJM subregion.

SERC currently has 86 members,[^3] which include Investor-Owned Utilities (IOU), Municipals, Cooperatives, Federal/State, Regional Transmission Organizations (RTO)/Independent System Operators (ISO)/Reliability Coordinators (RC), Merchant Electricity Generators, and Marketers. In the SERC Region, there are approximately 309 NERC registered entities.[^4]

## 3.2 Tie to the ERO Reliability Risk Priorities Report

The 2024 report builds on the 2023 ERO Reliability Risk Priorities Report,[^5] recognizing progress but also underscoring that new risks continue to emerge, and that the potential severity of risks can change from year to year as mitigating plans are put in place and implemented and as conditions change.

For the SERC Regional Risk Report, the process begins with the 2023 ERO Reliability Risk Priorities Report, which outlines five high-level risk profiles in Figure 9: Energy Policy, Grid Transformation, Security Risks, Resilience/Extreme Events, and Critical Infrastructure Interdependencies. These risk categories are then applied to the SERC region and further prioritized based on a more detailed analysis, considering both the likelihood of occurrence and the potential severity of impact on reliability and security.

![Figure 9: 2023 ERO Reliability Risk Priorities Report - Five High Level Risks]

**Energy Policy** – Energy Policy can drive changes in the planning and operation of the BPS. Accordingly, policy can affect BPS reliability and resilience and could present risks to its reliable operation. Ensuring reliability during and after policy-driven transitions should be a key consideration in setting Energy Policy. The implementation of policy decisions can significantly affect the reliability and resilience of the BPS.[^5] Whether it drives changes in generating resources, accelerates retirements, or incentivizes the addition/location of certain kinds of new loads, energy policy at the state and federal level can have direct and subtle impacts on reliability and security. The following risks have been identified as SERC Regional Risks that may be affected by Energy Policy: Supply Chain Constraints, Resource Uncertainty, Lack of Fuel Diversity, and Integrating Variable Energy Resources.

**Grid Transformation** – Transformation of the grid continues to accelerate toward generation and load resources that are increasingly non-synchronous, diverse, digital, and dynamic, while dependence on uninterrupted electric energy increases. At the same time, electricity demand for homes, transportation, commerce, and industry shows signs of growth at rates much more rapid than previously anticipated. This rapid transformation results in an array of nontraditional operating conditions and risks.[^5] The following risks have been identified as SERC Regional Risks related to Grid Transformation: Supply Chain Constraints, Resource Uncertainty, Shortage of Required Skillsets, Rapid Nontraditional Load Growth, Lack of Fuel Diversity and Availability, Legacy Architecture Compatibility, Extreme Physical Events: Sabotage & Attacks, and Integrating Variable Energy Resources.

**Resilience to Extreme Events** – BPS resilience to extreme events is an area of increasing focus. Reliability and resilience are related but distinct concepts:[^6] BPS reliability involves performance consistency under various reasonably expected known or historical operating conditions. Resilience, on the other hand, involves the ability of the BPS to withstand and recover quickly from significant abnormal conditions or extreme events.[^7] This is especially important for the SERC Region given its large geographic exposure to hurricanes, tornadoes, winter storms, and extreme heat, among other extreme events. The following risks have been identified as SERC Regional Risks in the category of resilience to extreme events: Extreme Weather Impacts, Lack of Fuel Diversity and Availability, and Extreme Physical Events: Sabotage & Attacks.

**Security Risks** – The rapid growth of BPS infrastructure with new facilities, services, and technology integration poses significant challenges to operational security, crucial for a reliable and resilient BPS. This expansion increases the cyber-attack surface and the risk of coordinated attacks. Cyber and physical security are interconnected, as vulnerabilities in one can compromise the other, potentially leading to asset damage, functionality loss, or impaired situational awareness essential for BPS operations and restoration. These vulnerabilities are further aggravated by insider threats, poor cyber hygiene, equipment limitations, and supply chain issues.[^7] The following risks have been identified as SERC Regional Risks in the category of security risks: Supply Chain Constraints, Shortage of Required Skillsets, Exploitation of Vulnerabilities, Legacy Architecture Compatibility, and Extreme Physical Events: Sabotage & Attacks.

**Critical Infrastructure Interdependency** – The interdependencies between critical infrastructure sectors (e.g., communications, water, wastewater, financial, manufacturing, transportation) and energy subsectors (e.g., natural gas, oil) must be fully understood, communicated, coordinated, and planned. Otherwise, disruptions in other critical infrastructures can affect BPS reliability. The interdependence between energy subsectors is increasing, particularly with natural gas. The growing reliance on natural gas as a fuel source for electric generation is potentially heightening the risk of common-mode failures that could lead to reliability issues. Additionally, the dependency of BPS reliability on natural-gas-fired generation does not always align with the service priorities of the natural gas delivery system, nor with the weatherization requirements for natural gas gathering and delivery infrastructure. Furthermore, the natural gas delivery system depends on reliable electric service to deliver natural gas at acceptable pressures; however, the foreseeable growth and dependence of BPS reliability on natural-gas-fired generation does not align with the expected pace of pipeline development.

As the grid evolves and electrification of critical sectors like transportation increases, these interdependencies will grow, causing potential ripple effects across multiple infrastructures.[^7] The following risks have been identified as SERC Regional Risks that are notably interdependent with BPS reliability and security: Lack of Fuel Diversity and Availability, and Extreme Physical Events: Sabotage & Attacks.

## 3.3 Risk Categories by the Manage and Monitor Definitions

**Risk Categories**

SERC also categorizes Regional Risk according to the maturity of mitigation plans that are in place to reduce the impacts or probability of occurrence. The status assigned to each risk is based on the existence of mitigation plans or guidance.

The **Manage** status group includes emerging risks where mitigation plans need to be developed and implemented—through SERC, NERC, or other Industry engagements—or additional mitigation plans need to be considered: Resource Uncertainty, Exploitation of Vulnerabilities, Rapid Nontraditional Load Growth, Lack of Fuel Diversity and Availability, Legacy Architecture Compatibility, and Extreme Physical Events: Sabotage & Attacks.

The **Monitor** status group includes risks where mitigation plans and guidance are already underway, but additional time is required to fully implement these actions and assess their effectiveness in reducing risk: Supply Chain Constraints, Extreme Weather Impacts, Shortage of Required Skillsets, and Integrating Variable Energy Resources.

In the review of SERC Reliability Risks the Engineering Committee (EC), Operations Committee (OC), and Critical Infrastructure Protection Committee (CIPC) separately identified the top Reliability Risks to the SERC footprint from their perspectives. Then the groups consider those risks for which two or more of the technical committees identified a risk as significant and determine the overall ranking for the most significant risks for the SERC footprint.

The Regional Risk Profile section of this report provides the following for each risk: the committee that identified and evaluated the risk, a definition and justification as to why this risk made the top ten list, and an update on the mitigation activities for the risk identified.

## 3.4 Improvements for the 2024–2026 SERC Regional Risk Report

**Scoring Changes for 2024**

In 2023, SERC conducted a statistical review to assess the effects of not scoring or assigning a zero to certain impact areas (Appendix C). The objective was to determine whether reducing the number of impact areas would significantly affect the total risk score and ranking of major risks in the SERC footprint. By scoring fewer impact areas, the three disciplines gained more flexibility in evaluating and scoring each impact area for each risk. The review analyzed four different scenarios (scoring 6, 8, 10, or 12 impact areas). It found that scoring 10 or 12 impact areas did not significantly alter risk rankings. Consequently, SERC adopted the approach of scoring 10 impact areas for the 2024 Risk Register, resulting in a slight increase in the total risk score for each risk.

**Naming Convention Changes in 2024**

To enhance understanding and clarity of the risks identified in the risk registry and report, SERC introduced additional descriptors in the naming convention for each risk in 2024. This effort aims to provide more detailed and precise descriptions, thereby improving comprehension of each risk.

**Additional Resources**

To bolster the justification and mitigation strategies of risks, SERC incorporated a variety of industry reports, articles, and white papers into the SERC Regional Risk Report. This comprehensive approach provided robust support for the ranking of each risk, ensuring a thorough explanation of the rationale behind each risk's position.

**New Sections of the Report**

To address the rapidly evolving BPS and the influx of risk nominations, SERC emphasized new and emerging risks in this report (Section 5). SERC received five new risk nominations: Rapid Nontraditional Load Growth, Lack of Generative Artificial Intelligence Governance, Coordination of Critical Generation or Load Through a Single Substation, Disruption of Cloud Services, and Lack of Insider Threat Governance Programs. This new section aims to provide an overview, introduction, additional resources, and details of ongoing industry activities related to these risks.

Additionally, to enhance understanding of the risk registry, SERC included the entire 2024 Reliability Risk Working Group Consolidated Risk Registry (Appendix A). This comprehensive registry details and scores all thirty risks reviewed annually, offering a complete picture of the current risk landscape.

# 4.0 SERC Top 10 2024 Regional Risk Profiles

## Risk 1: Supply Chain Constraints (Monitor)

Identified and evaluated by the Critical Infrastructure Protection and Engineering Committees

**Definition/Justification of risk**: As the demand for electricity increases in tandem with technological advancements and the shift toward renewable energy, it is essential to address primary risks within supply chains. These risks include both vulnerabilities (the risks vendors could potentially introduce within industry environments) and product delays (i.e., delays in projects created by shortages of needed materials to produce final products). The electric sector encompasses the generation, transmission, and distribution of electricity. Efficient supply chain management is crucial for ensuring reliable service delivery while minimizing costs and environmental impacts. However, several risks threaten the stability and efficiency of supply chains in this sector. See Figure 10.[^8]

![Figure 10: Supply Chain Attacks on the Risk]

**Risks**:

- **Supplier Dependency**. This is typified by over-reliance on a limited number of suppliers for critical components (e.g., transformers, circuit breakers, renewable technology, etc.). Potential disruptions can occur due to supplier bankruptcy, political instability, or natural disasters affecting production.
- **Geopolitical Risks**. Trade restrictions, tariffs, and geopolitical tensions can limit access to essential materials and technologies. This could result in increased costs and delays in project timelines, and potential shifts in market dynamics.
- **Natural Disasters and Climate Change**. The electric sector is particularly vulnerable to weather events such as hurricanes, floods, wildfires, and earthquakes, which can disrupt supply chains. Damage to infrastructure and facilities may lead to significant supply delays, increased repair costs, and regulatory challenges.
- **Cyber Security Threats**. The integration of smart technology and the Internet of Things (IoT) into supply chains can introduce vulnerabilities to cyber-attacks. A cyber breach can disrupt operations, lead to the theft of sensitive data, and compromise safety protocols.
- **Regulatory Changes**. Changes in regulations related to environmental standards, labor laws, or import/export restrictions can impact supply-chain dynamics. Companies may face increased compliance costs, operational disruptions, and potential legal liabilities.
- **Operational Disruptions**. Supply-chain disruptions can lead to delayed project timelines, impacting electricity generation, transmission, and distribution.
- **Financial Loss**. Increased costs from sourcing alternative suppliers, expediting shipments, or dealing with inventory shortages can significantly impact business or entities.
- **Reputation Damage**. Failure to meet service expectations due to supply-chain disruptions can harm customer trust and brand reputation. Delays in complying with regulatory requirements can result in fines, sanctions, and further erosion of public trust. Mitigating supply chain risks might lead to unintended consequences affecting sustainability goals.
- **Vendor Risk Management**. Assessing and managing the security posture of third-party vendors is crucial. This includes evaluating their security policies, practices, and potential vulnerabilities. Vendors with weak security can become entry points for attacks, so ongoing risk assessments and monitoring are essential.
- **Incident Response and Management**. Developing and testing an incident response plan tailored specifically for supply chain disruptions is essential. This plan should outline how to quickly address and mitigate the impact of a cyber security incident that affects the supply chain, including communication strategies and coordination with affected parties.
- **Data Protection and Privacy**. Ensuring the protection of data shared across the supply chain is vital. This involves implementing strong encryption, access controls, and data loss prevention measures to safeguard sensitive information from unauthorized access or breaches.

![Figure 11: Supply Chain Graphic]

The Engineering Committee has identified significant supply chain risks that threaten the stability of the energy sector. Prolonged lead times for critical components like transformers and 230 kV breakers, disruptions in coal deliveries, and shortages of essential chemicals for thermal power plants could lead to operational shutdowns. Figure 11.[^9] is a pictorial example of dependencies in the supply chain.

These challenges are delaying new generation projects, particularly in solar and storage, amidst the rapid retirement of fossil fuel plants and rising energy demand.

As of late 2023, about 25 GW of fully approved generation projects in MISO’s Generator Interconnection Queue had missed their in-service deadlines by an average of 650 days, with developers citing supply chain and permitting issues as the two biggest reasons for the delays. See Figure 12.[^10]

![Figure 12: MISO Generation Queue Delays]

Additionally, disruptions in oil refineries are raising concerns about the availability of liquid fuels during natural gas shortages. Political tensions in resource-rich regions are also threatening the supply of essential materials like titanium, magnesium, and precious metals needed for the BPS. While industry has limited control over these geopolitical factors, proactive planning and information sharing are vital to understanding and mitigating these risks. The COVID-19 pandemic and ongoing international tensions have underscored the vulnerability of the utility industry to supply chain disruptions, even during stable conditions.

**Mitigation Status and Strategies**:

- **Diversification of Suppliers**. Developing relationships with multiple suppliers across different regions will increase the diversification of suppliers and reduce dependency on single sources; therefore, improving the resilience to supplier disruptions.
- **Inventory Management Practices**. Implement safety stock policies to balance inventory levels, enhance flexibility, and reduce the risk of stockouts. By maintaining safety stock, organizations can adapt to market changes while minimizing excess inventory. This approach provides a buffer to prevent stockouts, resulting in an efficient inventory management system that supports operational continuity.
- **Enhanced Cyber Security Measures**. Develop robust cyber security protocols, including implementing regular security audits, employee training, and updated software systems. These protocols reduce vulnerability to cyber threats.

## Risk 2: Extreme Weather (Monitor)

Identified and evaluated by the Operations Committee

**Definition/Justification of risk**: The SERC Region frequently faces extreme weather events, such as cold weather with ice and wind, and extreme heat, and typically endures tornados and tropical storms annually. These extreme weather conditions pose significant risks to BPS reliability by causing disruptions in fuel supply, loss of telecommunications, extended growing seasons that can lead to vegetation encroachment on transmission lines, reduced situational awareness, and forced outages of generation and transmission facilities.

Severe cold weather can disrupt natural gas pipeline supplies, cause freezing of critical piping or instrumentation at generation plants, and lead to frozen coal piles, all of which jeopardize the continuity of power generation. Conversely, extremely hot weather can impair generation capacity by limiting plant cooling options, reducing the availability of cooling water, and affecting hydroelectric generation due to drought or flooding. Additionally, in recent years, the United States and the SERC Region have experienced increasingly frequent and intense heat waves and heat domes. These extreme temperatures can reduce facility ratings and put additional strain on the system during peak demand periods. Weather-related challenges not only threaten grid reliability but also lead to substantial disruptions and resource strains. Figure 13 illustrates the impact of weather and climate disasters from 2023, as reported by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA).[^11]

![Figure 13: NOAA 2023 Weather and Climate Disasters]

As the energy mix shifts with an increasing reliance on natural gas and inverter-based resources, reliability risks increase during winter, especially in the early morning or evening when temperatures are at their lowest, demand is high, and solar output is minimal. This situation becomes more critical if there is insufficient wind, a condition referred to as a wind drought.

![Figure 14: Wind Output during Winter Storm Uri]

**Wind drought**: MISO experienced a wind output of less than 1percent of the wind generation nameplate aggregate for 39 consecutive hours on January 28-30, 2020. During this period, 20.2 GWs of wind decreased to almost 0. Another example of wind drought occurred during Winter Storm Uri (in February 2021), when wind output was low across multiple regions for 12 days (see Figure 14).

**Solar in winter months**: During the winter, a combination of shorter days with widespread cloud cover and fog greatly decreases solar output. Any calm night or calm cloudy day can create a potential capacity shortfall. This may become increasingly critical as we move away from conventional generation resources. In addition, uniform high-load factors leave little room for charging Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) or electric vehicle batteries.

“Imagine a night during a future wind drought where there is no solar, little wind and depleted batteries.”[^12]

The potential of limited wind and/or solar output during winter and increased winter load during extremely cold weather limits the ability to restore reservoirs or charge batteries. Electrification exacerbates this issue.[^12]

**Mitigation Status and Strategies**: The SERC Operating Committee (OC) actively mitigates the risks associated with extreme weather by utilizing the SERC OC Conservative Operations Guideline, which was updated and approved in 2024. This guideline assists SERC members in developing conservative operations procedures tailored to the extreme weather challenges faced by the SERC footprint.

In addition, NERC’s Reliability Guideline, Generating Unit Winter Weather Readiness provides industry practices for preparing and operating through extreme cold weather. SERC has circulated this guideline among OC members to support their winter preparedness activities.

The OC updated a severe cold weather guideline document in 2024, drawing on member-submitted checklists. To maintain focus on preparedness, the OC established a standing agenda item at their fall meeting dedicated to cold weather operations.

**Guideline Documents**:

- SERC OC Conservative Operations Guideline
- NERC’s Reliability Guideline, Generating Unit Winter Weather Readiness
- SERC OC Extreme Cold Weather Guidelines

Sharing best practices is a cornerstone of SERC's approach. Members collaborate extensively during extreme weather events, utilizing mutual assistance for restoration efforts. SERC enhances regional resilience by covering Cold Weather Preparedness and Extreme Weather topics during annual outreach events, where entities exchange lessons learned and strategies for improving extreme weather resilience.

The OC has conducted a post-winter survey over the past four years, revealing ongoing improvements in generator preparedness, training, and knowledge regarding cold weather. The 2024 survey saw the highest participation yet, with around 80 percent of generator owners and operators contributing. Insights from this survey will guide the development of further mitigation efforts, including seminars, training sessions, and new guidelines.

The newly formed Generator Working Group is also addressing the implementation of new cold weather standards, such as EOP-011-3, and EOP-012-2. Additionally, the SERC Engineering Committee (EC) continues to evaluate the impacts of extreme weather in both seasonal and long-term planning horizons. The EC’s working groups assess system constraints under higher-than-projected loads and widespread generation outages to identify potential vulnerabilities and improve regional support during extreme events.

The EC is also incorporating the evaluation of extreme events into planning studies, including FERC Order 896, which initiated the forthcoming Transmission System Planning Performance Requirements for Extreme Temperature Events TPL-008 Reliability Standard. TPL-008 establishes transmission system planning performance requirements to ensure the BES operates reliably during extreme temperature events. While specific guidelines are still in development, these studies represent ongoing efforts to address the increasing frequency and severity of extreme weather events within the SERC footprint.

## Risk 3: Resource Uncertainty (Manage)

Identified and evaluated by the Operations and Engineering Committees

**Definition/Justification of risk**: The traditional methods of long-term planning, short-term planning, and real-time operations must adapt to the challenges presented by changes in generating resources. that are challenging uncertainties surrounding resource availability. While the energy mix is shifting toward renewable energy sources, the increasing reliance on natural gas complicates the narrative. Natural gas, while technically a fossil fuel, plays a crucial role in the current energy transition.

Natural gas remains a cornerstone of power generation, providing essential services that current renewables (like solar and wind) cannot fully supply, such as system inertia and ramping, and voltage regulation. Accelerating changes in these resources (see Figure 15 and Figure 16)—as well as a growing dependence on natural gas presents operational challenges, particularly as resource uncertainties—such as fuel deliverability and load variability—become more pronounced and complicates operations and planning.

The rapid integration of renewables, coupled with the ongoing retirements of coal plants, amplifies the need for reliable natural gas supply and infrastructure, especially since renewable energy sources currently lack the ability to provide essential ancillary services at the same level as conventional generation.

![Figure 15: SERC Capacity Resources for 2014, 2024, and 2034]

These transitions, driven by public policy shifts and market dynamics, are reshaping the SERC regional (and national) energy landscape. The reliance on natural gas, spurred by its lower emissions and replacement of coal, is set to increase as coal plants continue to retire. However, this creates a less diverse generation portfolio. Meanwhile, renewables such as photovoltaic solar and, to a lesser extent, wind are rapidly increasing in prominence, thanks to state-based incentives and falling installation costs.[^13] Coal plant retirement drivers (see Figure 17),[^14] include state and corporate carbon reduction goals, federal emission reduction rules, and competition from other sources of generation.[^15] In Figure 18 shows a summary of renewable policies by state.[^16]

![Figure 16: U.S. Electricity Mix]
![Figure 17: Future Coal Capacity Retirements (through 2025)]
![Figure 18: DSIRE Renewable & Clean Energy Standard December 2023]

Although the risk is described as resource uncertainty, there is a need for continued improvements in planning approaches to support the reliability and security for the BPS through this transition and in the future. Historically, power generation was more predictable, with established resources that had well-understood dispatch capabilities and dynamic behaviors. These resources were connected to the grid in ways that allowed operators and planners to leverage years of documented knowledge and experience.

However, the new mix of generation of resources introduces greater complexity. Their availability fluctuates daily, and their dynamic behavior can be altered by simple firmware updates or software changes, making their operation less predictable. These newer resources are often smaller, quicker to deploy, and tend to cluster in areas with cheaper land, further complicating both real-time grid operations and short- and long-term planning efforts.

Inverter-based generators, in particular, require advanced Electromagnetic Transient (EMT) modeling to accurately predict and manage their behavior during transient events, which is critical for maintaining system stability. This complexity underscores the need for continuous improvements in planning approaches, ensuring that the BPS remains reliable and secure through this period of transition and beyond.

![Figure 19: PJM’s Increased Coal Retirements and Fuel Insecurity]

The 2018 PJM Grid Reliability and Resilience Study used the PJM system as a case study to illustrate the potential reliability consequences of two major risks: increased coal retirements and fuel insecurity. (See Figure 19.) The study showed that premature retirement of fossil fuel generation could adversely impact PJM’s ability to meet reliability criteria.

PJM and other grid operators have issued warnings recently stating that the reliability of the grid in many regions of the country is at risk for electricity shortages due to:

- Premature retirement of dispatchable electricity resources driven by federal and state policies,
- Delays in new or replacement capacity coming on-line, and
- Increasing electricity demand (e.g., data centers and electrification).

Retirements have continued to increase since the 2018 study:

- More than one-third of the nation’s coal fleet has retired.
- Electricity generators have announced plans to retire roughly one-third (more than 60,000 MW) of the remaining fleet within the next 5 years.
- In total, almost two-thirds of the nation’s coal fleet will have retired by the end of 2028.[^17]

![Figure 20: MISO System Attributes]

**Mitigation Status and Strategies**: In 2023, MISO completed a foundational analysis of attributes, with a focus on three priority attributes where risk for the MISO system is most acute (see Figure 20):

- **System adequacy** is the ability to meet electric load requirements during periods of high risk. MISO focused on the near-term risk factors of availability, energy assurance, and fuel assurance.
- **Flexibility** is the extent to which a power system can adjust electric production or consumption in response to changing system conditions. MISO focused on the near-term risk factors of rapid start-up and ramp-up capability.
- **System stability** is the ability to remain in a state of operating equilibrium under normal operating conditions and to recover from disturbances. MISO focused on the nearest-term risk factor of voltage stability.[^18]

**Diverse Resource Needs**:

- No single resource provides all necessary system attributes, necessitating a diverse resource fleet.[^19]

“Variable solar and wind resources contribute a smaller percentage of their nameplate capacity to resource adequacy and peak load compared to dispatchable generation like natural gas, the researchers noted.”

“Decarbonizing the electric sector requires higher levels of installed solar and wind capacity to achieve the same resource adequacy contributions.” (See Figure 22.) To mitigate these risks, it is recommended that the SERC OC utilize insights from the Engineering Committee’s Variable Energy Resources Working Group (VERWG). The VERWG should present updates on their activities and best practices at future SERC OC technical committee meetings, allowing the OC to identify and address any pertinent issues.

A gap exists in the modeling and monitoring of Distributed Energy Resources (DERs)[^21] within Energy Management Systems (EMS). To address this issue, it is recommended that SERC OC members with relevant expertise share best practices through presentations. Key topics presented so far include the registration and integration of DERs, as well as methods for modeling DERs within the EMS.

At its semi-annual meetings, the OC typically presents best practices, such as for solar and battery facilities. Additionally, the OC’s newly established Generator Working Group discusses upcoming standard changes that address resource uncertainty, as well as share lessons learned.

The Planning Coordination Subcommittee (PLCS) will begin evaluating additional studies, including the impact of gas pipeline unavailability, the concentration of variable energy resources (like solar sites) in specific areas, and the need for resource diversity to enhance reliability. The Resource Adequacy Working Group (RAWG) will continue to monitor and report on resource adequacy trends.

## Risk 4: Shortage of Required Skillsets (Monitor)

Identified and evaluated by the Operations and Engineering Committees

**Definition/Justification of risk**: The electric utility industry is undergoing a significant transformation, driven by the integration of new technologies, the retirement of aging infrastructure, and the need for a more sustainable energy future. This shift requires a workforce with a diverse set of skills, including expertise in renewable energy integration, cybersecurity, advanced grid operations, and data analytics. However, the industry is facing a critical shortage of skilled professionals in these areas.

The aging workforce, combined with the difficulty of attracting and retaining new talent, is creating a significant talent gap. Many experienced professionals are nearing retirement, taking with them decades of institutional knowledge and expertise. At the same time, the industry is struggling to compete with other sectors for top talent, particularly in fields like cybersecurity and data science.

This shortage of skilled professionals poses a significant risk to the reliability and security of the BPS. A lack of expertise in critical areas can lead to operational errors, delayed project timelines, and increased vulnerability to cyber-attacks. Furthermore, the inability to effectively manage and operate the evolving grid can hinder the transition to a more sustainable energy future.

**Mitigation Status and Strategies**: To address the shortage of skilled professionals, the industry is implementing a variety of strategies, including:

- **Expanded Training Programs**: Utilities are investing in comprehensive training programs to upskill their existing workforce and prepare them for the challenges of the evolving grid.
- **Educational Partnerships**: Utilities are partnering with universities and technical colleges to develop curriculum and training programs that align with the needs of the industry.
- **Enhanced Recruitment Efforts**: Utilities are implementing more aggressive recruitment strategies to attract top talent, including offering competitive salaries, benefits, and opportunities for professional development.
- **Knowledge Transfer Programs**: Utilities are implementing programs to facilitate the transfer of knowledge from retiring professionals to the next generation of workers.
- **Industry Collaboration**: Utilities are collaborating with industry organizations and associations to share best practices and develop strategies to address the talent gap.

SERC is actively supporting these efforts by providing resources and guidance to its members. The SERC OC and EC are working together to identify the skills and expertise needed for the future grid and to develop strategies to address the talent gap.

## Risk 5: Exploitation of Vulnerabilities (Manage)

Identified and evaluated by the Critical Infrastructure Protection Committee

**Definition/Justification of risk**: The increasing digitalization of the BPS has significantly expanded the attack surface for cyber-attacks. The threat landscape is constantly evolving, with attackers becoming increasingly sophisticated and persistent. - **Advanced Cybersecurity Solutions**: Leveraging advanced technologies, such as AI-powered threat detection and response systems, can help to identify and mitigate cyber threats in real-time.
- **Employee Training and Awareness**: Providing regular cybersecurity training to employees is essential to ensure that they are aware of the risks and know how to protect themselves and the organization.
- **Collaborative Risk Management**: Sharing threat intelligence and best practices with other utilities and industry organizations is crucial to stay ahead of the evolving threat landscape.

# 5.0 New and Emerging Risks

## 5.1 Lack of Generative Artificial Intelligence Governance

Generative AI is rapidly evolving and being integrated into various aspects of the electric utility industry, from customer service to grid operations. ## 5.4 Lack of Insider Threat Governance Programs

Insider threats, whether malicious or accidental, pose a significant risk to the security and reliability of the BPS. The lack of robust insider threat governance programs, including clear policies, procedures, and monitoring, is a significant concern.

5.1 Lack of Generative Artificial Intelligence Governance

Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) is becoming integral to the electric industry for tasks
like grid management and forecasting, but its adoption introduces risks, including data quality
issues, cyber security threats, and ethical challenges. The National Institute of Standards and
Technology (NIST) and the U.S. Department of Energy highlight the need for adaptive
governance to mitigate these risks, especially as GenAI capabilities expand rapidly. Potential
harm from GenAI, including impacts on reliability, security, and ethical considerations, make it
essential for the SERC footprint to address this risk and develop effective risk mitigation
strategies.

Generative AI vs Traditional AI: Generative AI refers to a type of AI that can create new content,
such as text, images, music, or other data, by learning patterns from existing data. Unlike
traditional AI, which typically performs tasks based on predefined rules or direct input, GenAI
models can generate original outputs that mimic human-like creativity and problem-solving.
Generative AI is trained on large datasets and learns to generate new content by identifying
patterns. This enables generative AI to create original, creative outputs, such as text, images,
and designs, based on the patterns it has learned.

GenAI is defined as a machine-based system that can, for a given set of human-defined
objectives, make predictions, recommendations, or decisions influencing real or virtual
environments.

Reports of successful implementation of machine learning and artificial intelligence 52 from
across the North American BPS is prevalent as AI has been present in the electric utility
industry for many years. Past and present applications of AI include forecasting, system
planning, system protection, predictive maintenance, grid management, enhancing the output of
wind and solar resources, storm recovery, wildfire risk assessment, customer analytics, etc.

A report released in April of 2024 from the U.S. Department of Energy highlights the rapid
expansion of AI capabilities across the energy sector while cautioning that we need to, “navigate
this emerging technology with a keen, technically grounded and risk-informed awareness of its
potential and pitfalls.” 53

The coordination of critical generation or load concentration through a single substation poses a
significant reliability risk to the BPS. If compromised, these substations could lead to substantial

These high-impact, low-probability events require a focused approach to ensure BPS reliability
and security.

mandates Transmission Owners
(TO) to identify and safeguard
critical transmission stations,
substations, and associated control
centers that, if attacked, could
cause instability or cascading
failures. TOs must perform periodic
risk assessments, using technical

impacts of substation loss on the
Interconnection.

established following the 2013
Metcalf Substation attack in
California and has undergone
minor changes, resulting in the current CIP-014-3 standard. Due to an increase in physical

infrastructure identification. A standard drafting team is currently updating CIP-014-3 under
project 2023-06 to clarify risk assessment methodologies and criteria for identifying critical
stations. Including the risk of coordinating critical generation or load concentration through a
single substation in the 2024–2026 SERC Regional Risk Report aims to raise awareness and
emphasize the need for physical protection and resilience measures.

By identifying and addressing these risks through compliance with CIP-014-3 and ongoing
updates to the standard, SERC is committed to enhancing the protection and resilience of
critical substations. The inclusion of this risk in the 2024–2026 SERC Regional Risk Report
underscores the importance of proactive measures to safeguard the BPS against potential
disruptions.

From a security perspective, the electric industry landscape is facing an increase in the number
and sophistication of cyberattacks, and security teams are seeking tools and capabilities to
elevate their security programs. Security solutions with greater visibility, detection, correlation,
analytics, and responsiveness are available using cloud services to help security teams to
reduce potential impacts of security events and speed recovery. Cloud services play a critical
role in providing greater capability across the security domains (see Figure 48) 55

Concurrently, from an operational and reliability perspective, the modern power grid landscape
is changing, driven by rapid grid modernization, digital transformation, decentralization of
electric resources and decarbonization targets. These factors are increasing the data volumes
required to continue operating a reliable and resilient grid and thus increasing the need for data
analytics and resources such as computing, network, and storage.

Renewable capacity expansion is accelerating. The International Energy Agency updated its
growth projections in 2022, to an estimate of 359.5 GW in renewable capacity growth in the US,
2022-2027. As renewable installations grow, site classifications may change from low to
medium impact levels, putting operators at risk of having to revert to on-premises resources to
meet compliance language rather than benefitting from the cloud services available to lower
impact sites. Limiting the resources and capabilities available for operators to manage these
use cases can result in impacts to reliability. The disruption of cloud services poses significant
risks to the electric sector, as it increasingly relies on cloud-based technologies for various

cloud services experience outages or breaches, it can lead to significant operational disruptions,

One major concern is the potential for downtime in energy management systems, which can
affect grid reliability. According to the U.S. Department of Energy, the integration of cloud

instance, if a cloud service goes down, electric utilities may struggle to gather and analyze real-

failure in one area can have cascading effects, impacting other systems and services linked to
the cloud.

efficiency of smart grid operations, it simultaneously exposes utilities to potential outages,
which can severely affect grid reliability.

2.  Cyber Security Vulnerabilities: The rise in cyberattacks targeting cloud services further
exacerbates risks. NERC highlights the electric sector's susceptibility to cyber threats,
suggesting that unmitigated vulnerabilities in cloud infrastructures can lead to data breaches
or operational disruptions that impact service delivery.

3.  Cascading Failures: The interconnectedness of cloud services means that a disruption in
one cloud provider can trigger a series of failures across various electric sector operations,
creating a domino effect that could compromise system integrity and public safety.

As the electric sector increasingly integrates cloud technologies for enhanced efficiency and
data management, it simultaneously faces heightened risks associated with service disruptions
and cyber security threats. Ensuring the resilience of cloud infrastructures through
comprehensive risk mitigation strategies is essential for maintaining the integrity and reliability of
the electric grid. 58

2024 ERO Enterprise Risk Elements

Purpose

The strategic Compliance Monitoring and Enforcement Program (CMEP) Implementation Plan
(IP) 65 highlights the focus of ERO Enterprise monitoring and enforcement efforts in 2024 on the
risk elements identified within. The CMEP IP gives guidance to the employees of the ERO
Enterprise 66 involved with monitoring and enforcement, and through public posting, informs the
ongoing conversations with industry about the risks to mitigate. The risk elements described
herein are all developed with the four risks designated “manage” in the 2023 RISC ERO
Reliability Risk Priorities Report 67 as well as the four risk profiles unchanged from 2021. The
risks designated “manage” are: 1) Changing Resource Mix, 2) Cyber Security Vulnerabilities, 3)
Resource Adequacy and Performance, and 4) Critical Infrastructure Interdependencies. In
addition, the report focuses on five risk profiles: 1) Grid Transformation, 2) Security Risks, 3)
Extreme Events, and 4) Critical Infrastructure Interdependencies, and a new one added in 2023,
5) Policy, which will not be addressed in the 2024 CMEP IP. While compliance with Reliability
Standards is evaluated as part of continuous monitoring, the focus of a mature CMEP is on how
the ERO Enterprise and industry proactively identify and mitigate risks to the BPS.

The CMEP IP represents the ERO Enterprise’s high-level priorities for its CMEP activities. While
the ERO Enterprise will decide how to monitor each registered entity based on its unique
characteristics, registered entities should consider the risk elements and their associated areas
of focus as they evaluate opportunities and priorities to enhance their internal controls and
compliance operations to mitigate risks to reliability and security. There is no expectation that
every risk element or every requirement mapped to a risk element should be contained within
every possible engagement. Risk elements serve as an input in determining the appropriate
monitoring of risks and related Reliability Standards and requirements in the Compliance

The Reliability Risk Working Group (RRWG) started 3 key initiatives in 2021 to enhance the
Regional Risk Report with a focus on improving scoring, developing a consistent group of impact
areas, and further defining the Major Risk Nomination Process.

Key Initiatives:

•  Review the current scoring and develop a methodology to improve consistency in

scoring by enhancing the Probability of Occurrence and Severity of Impact ratings and
developing the overall total risk score.

•  Review the current Impact Areas to improve the number of Impact Areas and provide
consistency across all the Technical Committees in the development of their risk
registry.

•  Review the current Nomination Process for Potential Major Risk and incorporate
changes to ease the use of the Nominations for Potential Major Risks form. In
addition, develop a process and timeline for submitting and vetting nominations for
consideration in the current year.

Outcome:

•  Scoring for the Probability of Occurrence: The task force provided further clarification

and definition to each score in the range (1 to 5) for consistent application.

•  Scoring for the Severity of Impact: The task force implemented a weighted scoring

consisting of four different areas.

1.  Materiality (M) – impact of given event finances, load, and/or industry

reputation) weight 40%.

2.  Controllability (C) – the amount of influence we have over mitigating the

event/risk) weight 40%.

3.  Span (S) – the length of time the event/risk could potential last once

occurrence happens) weight 10%.

4.  Velocity (V) – how quickly the event could materialize) weight 10%.

•  Scoring Formulas:

o  Probability of Occurrence (PO) for each Impact Area – Score of 1 to 5 = PO
o  Severity of Impact for each Impact Area (SOI) – Score of 1 to 5 for each
  Multiplied by weighting: (M*.4) +(C*.4) +(S*.1) +(V*.1) = SOI

Impact Area Risk Score = PO*SOI

o
o  Total Risk Score is the average of all the Impact Area Risk Scores

for Impact Areas that may not apply to each Major Risk being evaluated and
scored on the same set of 15 Impact Areas.

will help ensure the scores are standardized and comparable across each committee (CIPC, EC,
and OC). However, it is important to note that not all impact areas will apply to every risk. To
maintain consistent and equitable scoring for every risk, the RRWG found it necessary to
establish parameters for this situation.

Before beginning the 2021 risk scoring and prioritization process, the RRWG discussed the
appropriateness of utilizing a score of zero (0) for the Probability of Occurrence. In the past, the
use of a zero (0) for a Probability of Occurrence was not contemplated; each of the Impact Areas
used in developing the individual risk score were uniquely identified during the scoring process,
suggesting that Impact Areas with no impact were simply not considered (or included) in the
process. However, when using a consistent set of 15 Impact Areas, it is appropriate to consider
that one or more Impact Areas may have negligible or no impact on a proposed SERC major risk.
In this case, a score of zero (0) would be appropriate for the Probability of Occurrence.

As a result, the RRWG agreed to allow the use of a zero (0) for a Probability of Occurrence for an
individual Impact Area.

PUBLICAppendix D  Regional Risk Framework

SERC’s Mission is to assure effective and efficient reduction of risks to the reliability and security
of the bulk power system. To achieve this objective, the SERC RRWG develops the annual
regional risk registry. It is important to have a clear understanding of risk, mitigation process,
stakeholder participation, outreach, and measures of effectiveness to manage the risk to reliability
and security of the BPS.

A defined risk framework provides the structure required for efficient resource allocation and
prioritization. The framework also helps SERC to evaluate the effectiveness of the deployed
mitigation, which in turn informs the future risk prioritization. The structure helps to establish
responsibility and accountability.

SERC, in collaboration with its stakeholders, developed a four-step regional risk framework. Each
phase has its own unique set of processes, tools, and engagement with internal and external
stakeholders.

1.  Risk Identification: SERC and its stakeholder subject matter experts are actively engaged

in identifying the current and emerging risks through RRWG engagement. The risk
identification also takes into consideration the NERC Reliability Issues Steering
Committee (RISC) report, and SERC internal identification through compliance
monitoring, studies and assessments, events, and outreach.

2.  Risk Assessment and Analysis: The SERC data analytics group assesses the risk based
on the available data on the subject and analysis of the data. The stakeholders also help
provide valuable input in determining the prioritization of the quantified risk by defining the
impact areas and probability of occurrence. The framework provides provision to set the
risk tolerance for a particular risk.

3.  Mitigation Planning: The planning starts with the determination of whether the risk needs
to be managed or monitored. If managed, the risk mitigation planning/development is the
responsibility of SERC and its stakeholders through the SERC technical committees,
subcommittees, and the working groups. The technical committees also take advantage of
all the work carried out by ERO Enterprise, trade associations, vendors, and other
industry initiatives.

4.  Mitigation Implementation, Monitoring, and Control: The SERC technical committees

oversee the mitigation implementation, monitoring, and control through their annual work
plan development. SERC and the stakeholders also deploy measures to monitor the
effectiveness of the mitigation implementation.
