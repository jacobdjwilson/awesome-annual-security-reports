## Security at the epicenter of innovation

While excitement and budgets are rising for cutting-edge security programmes, progress on actually improving security is sluggish, even stagnant. PwC’s 2024 Global Digital Trust Insights survey of 3,876 business and tech executives at the largest global companies—30% of respondents have revenues of $10 billion or more—shows considerable room for improvement in cybersecurity.

We note 179 respondents who seem to be doing just that. These top 5%—our stewards of digital trust—are reaping benefits that others are missing. They’re experiencing fewer breaches, and the attacks that do hit them aren’t as costly.

## 9 degrees of separation: top performers vs the rest

Top performers are:
- 6x more likely to have already implemented transformative cybersecurity initiatives from which they are realising benefits.
- 5x more likely to be very satisfied with their current cyber technology capabilities.
- 4x more likely to be continually updating their risk management plan to mitigate cloud risks.
- 9x more likely to be mature in their cyber resilience practices.

## Cyber risk management: Ripe for reinvention

Mitigating cyber risk is a top priority for 2024. After dropping to fourth place in last year’s PwC CEO Survey, it’s now second, behind only digital and technology risks.

## Simplification of cyber tools: The bane of bad actors

Nearly half (49%) of business leaders selected technology modernisation as a priority, and 45% chose optimisation of existing technologies. An overabundance of point solutions may be one reason why only 5% of IT and tech respondents say they’re ‘very satisfied’ with the technology capabilities of their cyber solutions in all eight key areas.

## Cloud security: Overdue for concerted attention

Cloud security is the No. 1 cyber risk concern for nearly half (47%) of our respondents. Nearly every organisation—97%—has gaps in its cloud risk management plan. Only 3% maintain up-to-date plans that address all nine cloud security areas.

## Generative AI for cyber defence on the rise

Nearly seven in 10 (69%) say their organisation will use generative AI (GenAI) for cyber defence. 
- 47% are already using it for cyber risk detection and mitigation.
- 21% are already seeing benefits to their cyber programmes.

## Regulations: Providing a safe place to play and grow

About a third of respondents agree that four types of regulation will be most important to securing future growth: regulation of AI (37%), harmonisation of cyber and data protection laws (36%), mandatory reporting of cyber risk management (35%), and operational resilience requirements (32%).

## Dare to break cyber-as-usual: The 2024 C-suite playbook

In 2024, we challenge C-suite leaders to break out of the stasis. Key recommendations include:
- **Speak a new language**: Talk about cyber in business-speak, not just tech-speak.
- **Try bold, new ways of managing cyber risk**: Use sophisticated risk modeling and modern bug bounty programmes.
- **Shape guardrails**: Involve yourself early in regulatory processes.
- **Free your teams for creative thinking**: Use automation and GenAI to handle mundane chores.
- **Welcome cyber into the boardroom**: Make it a staple topic for strategic growth.
- **Think like the business owner**: Embrace cyber as a whole-of-business endeavor.

## About the survey

The 2024 Global Digital Trust Insights is a survey of 3,876 business, technology and security executives conducted in the May through July 2023 period. Respondents are based in 71 countries across various industries.
